Botsitting: The Work Draining AI Gains

A new Glean / Work AI Institute report names the hidden labor eating the AI productivity dividend — "bot sitting" — and its uglier cousin, "bot shitting." NLW digs in because, unlike most 2026 studies, he thinks the agentic shift would amplify these findings, not erase them.

Source: https://aidailybrief.ai/e/2026-06-26

▶ Listen — today’s brief (3 min)

The One Idea

AI's individual gains keep disappearing into invisible work — and the fix isn't a better tool, it's transformation.

Workers save 11 hours a week with AI but burn 6.4 of them "bot sitting" — feeding context, checking outputs, debugging, cleaning up confident-but-wrong answers. The Glean / Work AI Institute report frames this as the reason only 13% of organizations say AI made them significantly better. NLW partly disagrees on the mechanism — individual gains never automatically become organizational gains — but argues bot sitting and its degenerate form, bot shitting, are real artifacts of the transition. The differentiator isn't the AI you buy; it's the human infrastructure you build across individuals, teams, and organizations.

The Other Side

This edition's lead is that AI's gains keep vanishing into hidden labour, and the fix is not a better tool but real organisational transformation — building human infrastructure so individual productivity becomes company performance. Zitron's argument cuts beneath that: the transformation story is itself ritual. He calls the tech industry "the largest cargo cult of all time" — Microsoft, Google, Amazon and Meta sinking what he says will soon top a trillion dollars into AI data centres "because they don't have any other ideas," then dressing up the spend as disruption. In his telling, narratives like the productivity dividend or the "SaaSpocalypse" are directionally-pleasing stories planted by executives and repeated by a credulous media to paper over a software industry already in decline, with growth efficiency halved and SaaS revenue down two percent year-on-year. Where Whittemore sees a hard transition worth doing the work to cross, Zitron sees a movement going through the motions of innovation while the returns never arrive.

Source: Ed Zitron, “Cargo Culture”, 23 June 2026 (from 23 June’s edition)

The Truth Beneath

Both men orbit one variable: whether the money and effort going into enterprise AI is producing real organisational value or only its appearance. Both accept the spend is enormous and that, so far, most organisations report no significant improvement — this very edition concedes only thirteen percent say they are performing significantly better. The empirical question that will settle it is whether firms that genuinely restructure around AI show durable, audited gains in output and profit at true cost; the observable that resolves it is hard financial and productivity data once subsidies end, not survey sentiment or capex announcements. The residue is pure worldview: Whittemore reads the bot-sitting era as solvable growing pains, Zitron reads the whole buildout as ritual whose gods are quietly dying. Whittemore is vindicated if transformative organisations convert AI into measured, lasting gains; Zitron if the trillion-dollar bet keeps failing to return and curdles into writedowns. Both are polemicists with incentives — NLW bullish and anti-doom, Zitron bubble-certain with a paid newsletter — and the signal is that both agree individual AI use is not yet becoming organisational performance. Watch whether any large adopter publishes audited, priced-at-cost gains, or whether the capex starts showing up as impairments.

GP’s Corner

Claude’s notes on what today means for general practice and the NHS — commentary, not from the podcast itself.

"Bot sitting" has a direct surgery analogue — measure the net, not the gross

The report's central finding is that AI saves time but quietly takes some back: feeding it context, checking and correcting its confident-but-wrong output. An AI scribe is exactly this — it drafts fast, but a clinician still reads, corrects and signs. The honest figure is minutes saved per consultation after that supervision, audited in your own list, not the vendor's headline. If NLW is right, transformation captures a real net dividend once workflows adapt; if Zitron is right, much of the apparent saving is ritual that evaporates under scrutiny. Either way, measuring the net rather than the gross is the safe instinct.

"Bot shitting" is a clinical-governance risk, not a productivity quirk

The edition's bleaker term — shipping the first plausible output without being able to defend it, and then blaming the tool when it fails — is a recognisable hazard in medicine. A summary or letter you have not fully read remains your clinical responsibility; moral disengagement ("the AI wrote it") is no defence to the GMC or a coroner. Practical guardrail: decide in advance who signs off each AI-assisted entry and who reviews errors. That discipline holds whether AI proves transformative or, as Zitron argues, mostly an expensive cargo cult.

The adoption engine is people, not the licence

The most striking number for a small organisation: a cross-functional colleague using AI well lifts adoption roughly five-fold — far more than a leader mandating it — because their workflow survives real-world mess. In a surgery, the partner or nurse who quietly models good scribe use, and the training and review around it, will matter more than which product is bought. That is the "transformation" NLW means; Zitron's caution is to make sure it delivers measured benefit rather than imitating the form of innovation.

NLW’s Takes

Why NLW has gone quiet on enterprise studies

NLW has covered fewer consulting and research-house studies this year because the paradigm shifted so far from non-agentic to agentic work that anything measuring non-agentic work feels largely irrelevant. His bias is toward opportunity AI — doing new things — not just efficiency AI doing the same work faster.

NLW: individual gains never auto-convert to org gains

NLW disagrees with the report's core explanation. He argues individual productivity gains — wherever they come from — do not inherently translate into organizational gains unless there's an actual mechanism to facilitate the transformation, even if bot sitting disappeared entirely.

The data predates the agentic era — and that matters

The 6,000 respondents answered in December 2025 and January 2026, before tools like Claude Code drove autonomous agent work. NLW argues that, unlike most 2026 studies, a rerun today wouldn't nullify these findings — it would amplify them.

Opportunity AI creates a verification gap

As people use AI to do things previously beyond their abilities, a new category of bot shitting emerges — not laziness, but lacking the capability to verify outputs. NLW feels it himself judging new coding models impressionistically because he's never coded; democratization of skills is the upside, but the verification challenge is built in.

The work of AI transformation is transformation

NLW's closing argument: AI transformation is about messy, complex change in what you do — not just implementation of new tools. Bot sitting and bot shitting are part and parcel of a transition we'll all live through for years. There are no short answers, only organizations willing to do the work and those that aren't.
