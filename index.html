The AI Chart Everyone Is Getting Wrong

SpaceX prices the largest IPO ever, Bezos unveils an "artificial general engineer," and a scary downward chart sends Wall Street from token-maxing to token-panic overnight. NLW spends the back half dismantling that chart — and explains why the signal everyone's screenshotting isn't the signal they think it is.

The One Idea

The scary token chart isn't measuring demand and it's not measuring total expenditure.

Wall Street has decided the Silicon Data "token expenditure index" proves the agentic boom is over. It doesn't. The chart measures the weighted-average price of a million tokens, drawn only from third-party token routers — companies whose entire job is to find cheaper tokens. It says nothing about total demand, volume, or expenditure. The real story is the shift from token subsidy to token scarcity, and when the median firm still spends $11.38 per employee per month on AI, the growth in total tokens consumed will dwarf any drift toward cheaper baskets. That's not a bubble popping — it's a market rationalizing.

The Other Side

Writing four days before this episode, Zitron reads the very spending caps NLW waves away as the first hard evidence of demand stalling — arriving precisely when the industry's own commitments require it to accelerate. The data-centre buildout and compute contracts only make sense if AI revenue reaches roughly two trillion dollars a year by 2030, yet OpenAI and Anthropic still account for the vast majority of compute demand and 89% of AI-startup revenue, with most enterprises unable to see their own costs until the bill lands. His long-standing prediction — "businesses will squeal when they are made to pay the true cost of AI" — is, on his telling, now playing out. What NLW calls a market rationalising, Zitron calls the bill arriving.

Source: Ed Zitron, “AI Is Slowing Down”, 8 June 2026 (from 8 June’s edition)

The Truth Beneath

Strip the rhetoric and both men are staring at the same dial: enterprise token spend under transparent, usage-based billing. They accept the same facts — median firm spend is tiny, the caps at Uber and T-Mobile are real, ROI is currently unmeasurable, and frontier usage is concentrating in fewer hands. The empirical question is whether total spend keeps climbing once the subsidies end; the observable that settles it is the labs' disclosed revenue trajectory, which IPO filings will shortly make impossible to obscure. For NLW to be vindicated, the eleven-dollar median must climb toward the caps as agents spread. For Zitron, post-transparency spend must stall while the buildout's debts come due. The residue no evidence will touch is whether the output is worth having at all. Both are polemicists — NLW sells optimism, Zitron sells the bubble — so the signal is where they agree despite opposite priors: spend is concentrating, and measurement has arrived. Watch OpenAI's IPO prospectus.

GP’s Corner

Claude’s notes on what today means for general practice and the NHS — commentary, not from the podcast itself.

Your scribe contract — hedge both ways

If NLW is right, token prices fall and your scribe licence should get cheaper at renewal — ask. If Zitron is right, the subsidy era is ending and vendors' true costs surface, so the risk isn't price but vendor failure. The practical move is identical either way: shorter terms, break clauses, and confirmation that you can export your templates and data.

The $11.38 question, read twice

NLW reads the median firm's tiny AI spend as headroom; Zitron reads it as the verdict on demand. For an ICB the working conclusion is the same: the NHS has lost little by moving carefully, and the labs' IPO disclosures over the coming year will show which reading was right before any large commitment needs signing.

Measurement has arrived

The one thing both polemicists agree on: nobody can yet measure AI's return per task. Before a PCN buys anything agentic, make the supplier define the observable that proves value — consultations saved, coding accuracy, complaint rates. The same discipline that exposed the token chart this week applies to procurement.

NLW’s Takes

Don't read SpaceX as a referendum on AI models

NLW pushes back on treating the SpaceX listing as the market's first price on a frontier AI lab. You can't apply anything about Musk to anyone else — he operates in his own vortex — and the 11th-hour pivot to SpaceX as a neo-cloud reframes the whole thing as an infrastructure build-out story with a heavy dose of Elon halo, not a models story.

Wall Street went from token-maxing to token-panic head-spinningly fast

NLW's annual ritual: a new chart inflames investors into an AI counter-narrative frenzy, convinced this time the bubble is bursting. The Silicon Data LLM Token Expenditure Index — shared by Citadel Securities — went viral as proof the agentic boom collapsed. It shows nothing of the kind.

Every AI company is now in the token-efficiency business

The shift from assisted to agentic use cases radically increases AI consumption, and with finite tokens, prices rise as demand outpaces supply. Companies that never had to think about token efficiency — or mixed-basket models that route different use cases to different token tiers — suddenly do. The chart simply reflects that transition in progress.

Total token growth will dwarf any shift to cheaper baskets

If every firm followed Uber and capped at $1,500/month, the market expansion from a median of $11.38 to $1,500 per employee would massively outweigh any revenue lost to companies getting more efficient. NLW says it's very hard to imagine a short- or medium-term scenario where total AI consumed doesn't dwarf the rebalancing toward cheaper tokens.

If you see someone end a tweet with an ellipsis, run

NLW's tell for empty doom-narrative discourse: phrases like "the whole setup depends on this..." or loud claims that contradict everyone's lived experience. When a viral post insists the agentic frenzy has snapped back to a pre-agentic state, ask what the chart actually says — and who's incentivized to misread it.
