David's AI brief for Tuesday, the thirtieth of June.

The one idea today is a dispassionate answer to a simple question: how big is the AI economy, really? With the loudest bubble talk on a low ebb, the research group Exponential View audited more than a thousand AI companies and concluded that the demand is real, big and fast — and, crucially, backed by money actually banked rather than money merely promised. Their figures are striking. Around a hundred and ten billion dollars of revenue over the past twelve months, an annualised run rate of a hundred and seventy-five billion, and growth running three times faster than any previous wave of information technology. In two thousand and twenty-three, the industry took a hundred and eighty days to add a billion dollars of revenue. It now adds a fresh billion every two days.

The spending is vast — around eight hundred and forty-eight billion dollars this year, two trillion since twenty-twenty. But the report argues the payback is holding up: since the end of last year, quarterly revenues have exceeded the depreciation on all that kit, and older graphics chips are earning well into their seventh, eighth, even ninth year, long past the point sceptics assumed they would be obsolete. The tidy summary from Whittemore is that the indicators of return are far more positive than the average conversation would have you believe.

Now, the other side. The writer Ed Zitron, in a piece published the very same day, argues that this framing is precisely the trick. The revenue, he says, is largely circular — the big cloud companies pour money into their own loss-making labs, which spend it straight back on compute, and only a fraction returns as so-called revenue that is really venture subsidy recycled. He notes the giants won't disclose their real AI revenues, hiding behind what he calls vague run rates, and warns, citing the Bank for International Settlements, that the whole build-out serves demand that does not exist outside a couple of companies. The honest reading is that both men are circling one question — whether all this spending turns into durable, self-funding demand, or just the promise of it — and the thing to watch is whether, from the autumn, the cloud giants' spending finally overtakes the cash they generate.

For general practice, a few thoughts. Whether it's a bubble or not, the sensible posture is the same: budget for tools that outlast the hype, avoid deep dependence on a single cut-price vendor, and keep a manual fallback for note-taking. Watch the billing model too — the industry is shifting from flat subscriptions to charging by usage, and an ambient scribe that transcribes a whole consultation is far heavier than a one-line query, so ask the cost per consultation, not just the monthly headline. And a quieter one: the memory shortage the brief calls Ramageddon is pushing up the price of ordinary computers and storage, so factor a real-terms rise into any hardware replacement plan.
