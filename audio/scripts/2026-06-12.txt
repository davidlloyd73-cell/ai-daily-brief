David's AI brief for Friday, the twelfth of June.

One idea matters today. A chart has been racing around Wall Street that appears to show spending on artificial intelligence falling off a cliff, and it has turned investor mood from euphoria to panic in under a week. The trouble is that the chart doesn't show what people think it shows. It comes from a firm called Silicon Data, and although it was labelled a token expenditure index, it actually tracks the average price paid for a million tokens — tokens being the units of work that AI models consume. And it draws its data only from third-party brokers whose entire business is hunting down cheaper tokens. So a falling line does not mean demand is collapsing; it means the cheapest tokens are getting cheaper, among precisely the customers most determined to find them. The podcast's host argues the real story is a shift from subsidised tokens to scarce ones, anchored by one small number: the median firm pays just eleven dollars thirty-eight per employee per month for AI, while the dramatic spending caps in the headlines come from the top one per cent, who spend thousands. That gap, he says, is why total growth will dwarf any drift towards cheaper models. Not a bubble popping, but a market rationalising.

Now, the other side. Ed Zitron, writing four days earlier, reads those same spending caps as the first hard evidence of demand stalling, arriving just as the industry's commitments require it to accelerate. Both men are watching the same dial — what enterprises actually spend once billing turns transparent. The labs' flotation documents will settle that question; whether the output is worth having at all, no number ever will.

So what does this mean for general practice? Three thoughts.

First, your AI scribe. If the optimist is right, token prices fall and your licence should get cheaper at renewal — so ask. If the pessimist is right, subsidies end, true costs surface, and the risk is vendor failure rather than price. The move is identical either way: shorter terms, break clauses, and make sure you can export your templates and your data.

Second, the eleven-dollar question, read twice. One side calls the median firm's tiny AI spend headroom; the other calls it the verdict. For an ICB the working conclusion is the same: the NHS has lost little by moving carefully, and the flotation disclosures over the coming year will show which reading was right before any large commitment needs signing.

And third, measurement has arrived. The one thing both polemicists agree on is that nobody can yet measure AI's return per task. Before a practice buys anything agentic, make the supplier define the observable that proves value — consultations saved, coding accuracy, complaint rates. The discipline that exposed the token chart this week applies equally to procurement.
