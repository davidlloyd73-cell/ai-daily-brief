David's AI brief for Friday, the twelfth of June.

One idea matters today. A chart has been racing around Wall Street that appears to show spending on artificial intelligence falling off a cliff, and it has turned investor mood from euphoria to panic in under a week. The trouble is that the chart doesn't show what people think it shows. It comes from a firm called Silicon Data, and although it was labelled a token expenditure index, it actually tracks the average price paid for a million tokens — tokens being the units of work that AI models consume. More to the point, it draws its data only from third-party brokers, companies whose entire business is hunting down cheaper tokens. So a falling line does not mean demand is collapsing. It means the cheapest tokens are getting cheaper, among precisely the customers most determined to find them. Silicon Data itself has conceded the index should really have been called a price index.

The podcast's host argues the real story underneath is a shift from an era of subsidised tokens to an era of scarcity, with overall consumption still climbing steeply as AI moves from chat assistants to agents that work on their own. And one small number anchors the whole debate. The median firm on one large spending platform pays just eleven dollars thirty-eight per employee per month for AI. The dramatic spending caps in the headlines come from the top one per cent, who spend around seven and a half thousand dollars. That enormous gap between the median and the frontier is why, in his view, total growth will dwarf any drift towards cheaper models. Not a bubble popping, but a market rationalising.

So what does this mean for general practice? Three thoughts.

First, your AI scribe. Tokens are the scribe vendors' main input cost, and one analyst this week suggested the big labs could cut prices by sixty per cent and still turn a profit. If token prices fall, the cost of running a scribe falls with them. Worth remembering at contract renewal: if the licence fee doesn't budge, the saving is going into the vendor's margin rather than the practice's budget.

Second, a corrective for the next ICB meeting. The NHS is routinely described as uniquely slow to adopt technology. But if the typical firm worldwide is spending eleven dollars and change per employee on AI, then most organisations everywhere have barely left the starting line. On this evidence the NHS is in the pack, not trailing behind it — which is a rather more useful starting point for planning than the usual self-reproach.

And third, workforce. Jeff Bezos claimed this week that AI shrinks the number of people needed tenfold while creating tenfold more opportunity. The same style of argument is beginning to surface in medical workforce planning. Today's episode offers the right reflex for both: before accepting any dramatic claim, ask what the data actually measures. That discipline served the token chart well this morning, and it will serve workforce projections just as well.
