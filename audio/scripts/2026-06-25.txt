David's AI brief for Thursday, the twenty-fifth of June.

Today's one idea cuts through a busy, no-single-story day: who owns artificial intelligence inside an organisation matters more than which AI you happen to buy. The accountancy and consulting firm KPMG has run a quarterly survey, and unlike most of the stale reports doing the rounds, this one was collected recently enough to actually mean something. Its standout finding is about accountability. Organisations with clear ownership of AI decisions were about three times more likely to report a return on their investment. And where the chief executive is personally on the hook, the numbers jump sharply — leaders reporting established returns rise from four percent to fourteen, and those reporting meaningful business value go from twenty-one percent to fifty-seven. Nathaniel Whittemore, who presents the show, reads this as a shift in what kind of problem AI really is. It has stopped being a question of which tool the IT department picks, and become a question of organisational design — and that, he argues, is leadership's job to own.

Now, the other side. Writing three weeks earlier, the critic Ed Zitron would say the whole premise is built on sand, because, in his view, the return on investment from AI cannot actually be measured at all. He points to a separate study of nearly a thousand executives whose own authors concluded, in his quote, "the technology worked, the value didn't arrive." His warning is sharper still: making AI use something a chief executive personally champions is precisely what manufactures rosy self-reports. People who have been trained to ignore the true, and now rising, cost of these tools will keep saying they see value, while nobody can point to a single measured pound coming back. Reported return, he would say, is sentiment dressed up as arithmetic.

For those of us in general practice, a few thoughts. First, that distinction between reported and measured value is the useful one. If you are weighing an AI scribe, insist on a real saving you can see in your own surgery — minutes saved per consultation, letters turned round faster, fewer results left unactioned — measured locally, not taken from a confidence survey or a vendor's slide. Second, watch the meter. Both sides agree on one uncomfortable fact: only about a third of organisations can fully see what their AI actually costs, and pricing is drifting from flat subscriptions towards paying by use. Know what a tool costs before you build your week around it, and check it still saves time once any introductory pricing ends. And third, the organisational instinct underneath all this is sound for the NHS whoever turns out to be right: decide who is accountable for each AI-assisted decision before you deploy it. Who signs off that a scribe's summary is safe? Who reviews the errors? That is simply good clinical governance — and it travels to a practice without needing to believe the headline figure at all.
