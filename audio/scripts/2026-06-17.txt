David's AI brief for Wednesday, the seventeenth of June.

The one idea today is that the artificial-intelligence race, which had felt settled into a comfortable agentic phase, has suddenly become dynamic again — and the force rewriting it is SpaceX. Three threads braid together. Anthropic remains stuck in a regulatory standoff, now in its fifth day, after the government forced it to shut its Mythos and Fable models for foreign nationals worldwide. OpenAI's leaked, audited financials landed and were widely misread. And Elon Musk turned a blockbuster SpaceX flotation into a sixty-billion-dollar swoop on the coding company Cursor. SpaceX closed up forty-nine per cent on its first day, valued at two-point-six trillion dollars — the fifth most valuable company on Earth — and used that paper wealth to make Musk, briefly, the world's first trillionaire. The host's connective tissue is this: infrastructure, frontier models, and even who is allowed to use them are all being pulled under national-security control, and the contest is far more fluid than anyone thought a week ago.

The financial sub-plot is the one I want to sit with. The numbers everyone is quoting — a thirty-eight-and-a-half-billion-dollar loss — came from a leak, and the host argues most people read it wrong. Most of that figure, he says, is a one-off, non-cash accounting charge tied to OpenAI converting into a for-profit. The overlooked detail is that, on selling tokens alone, OpenAI appears to turn a tidy profit — thirteen billion of revenue against seven-and-a-half billion of direct cost.

Now, the other side. The man who actually obtained and published those audited figures is Ed Zitron, and he reads them as terminal, not reassuring. To him the headline is the operating loss of nearly twenty-one billion dollars on thirty-four billion of spending, with research costs alone near nineteen billion — losses he says are mounting dramatically year on year, with no path to sustainability he can see. Tellingly, both men accept exactly the same audited numbers, and both even agree the inference line is real; they differ only on whether it matters. The signal, beneath their opposite instincts, is the question they share: strip out the one-off charge, and does the operating loss actually shrink? Worth saying plainly — the host leans anti-doom and reached for the cheerful read, while Zitron is bubble-certain and chose to publish the leak. Watch OpenAI's next audited disclosure.

What does any of this mean for general practice? The sharpest, most uncomfortable fact is that a frontier model was switched off by government order — the first kill switch of its kind. Most AI scribes sit on one of these big models, so it is worth asking your supplier plainly which model they depend on, and what the fallback is if access is ever restricted. Keep a manual note-taking habit alive, so a clinic is never left without documentation overnight. And when a scribe contract comes up for renewal, judge the unit cost of the service you actually use, not the vendor's headline valuation — a tool can be perfectly sustainable per consultation even if its parent is losing billions.
