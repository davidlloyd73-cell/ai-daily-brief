David's AI brief for Tuesday, the twenty-third of June.

The one idea today is that the fight over AI data centres needs a middle path - somewhere between rolling over and reaching for the pitchforks. Both sides of this argument, the brief contends, are wildly reductive. Take the frightening numbers about water and electricity: most of them simply do not survive contact with context. Amazon's entire global fleet of data centres used around two and a half billion gallons of water last year. That sounds enormous, until you learn that America's golf courses get through more than five hundred billion gallons a year - so Amazon's whole year is roughly a single day of keeping the fairways green. Leaky pipes across the United States waste about fifteen times more water than every data centre combined.

But, says Nathaniel Whittemore, communities are not powerless either. The real opportunity is neither to let big technology do whatever it likes, nor to stop everything - it is to negotiate for serious economic benefit. He points to a rural parish in Louisiana where a surge in tax receipts from a new Meta campus is funding fifty-thousand-dollar bonuses for hundreds of teachers. That, he argues, is what the how-everyone-wins phase looks like.

Now, the other side. The writer Ed Zitron would question whether this build-out is as large as everyone assumes. He spent a week hunting for big data centres that broke ground a couple of years ago and were actually finished and switched on, and came up, in his words, surprisingly empty-handed. Nobody, he argues, has yet completed a true one-gigawatt facility; the word operational too often describes a campus where only a few of the planned buildings hold any equipment at all. The fight over a data centre's water and power, on his telling, quietly assumes a scale the disclosures do not support.

For us in general practice, a few thoughts. First, the lesson about big numbers travels straight into medicine. A figure frightens precisely because we have no sense of scale - the same trick that makes a doubling of a tiny risk sound alarming, or a screening benefit quoted in relative rather than absolute terms. It is a useful discipline when explaining risk to patients. Second, the how-everyone-wins framing applies to AI in the surgery: when a supplier or care board brings a scribe or triage tool, refuse the binary of swallowing whatever is offered or rejecting it outright, and ask what the practice gets in return - training, data control, fair pricing. And holding Zitron's caution alongside, check that the promised benefit is real and measured in your own setting before you reorganise around it. If he is right, some of it is vapour.
