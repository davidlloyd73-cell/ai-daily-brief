David's AI brief for Thursday, the eighteenth of June.

The one idea today is that the Fable gap is forcing everyone to get clever about which models they actually use. A week on from the United States government effectively banning Anthropic's Fable and Mythos models, the fallout is splitting two ways. At the G7, the heads of the big labs turned up in force, and for the first time the rest of the world found itself reckoning with a simple fact: access to American frontier models can be switched off, by a government, overnight. Europe arrived braced for a fight about China and instead found itself quietly pleading for guaranteed access, while admitting it is badly behind on putting its own chips on racks.

But the more interesting half of the story is what enterprises are doing about it. Rather than wait, they have started filling the gap themselves. The lost model has become fuel for a shift that was already coming: away from bolting the single most powerful model onto every task, and towards open-weight models, blended setups, and smart routing that send each job to whichever model handles it best. The numbers are striking. One Chinese open model runs a task for about six cents where a top frontier model charges forty-nine, and, the show argues, you often cannot tell the difference. Inference efficiency, in other words, has quietly become a competitive advantage in its own right.

Now, the other side. Ed Zitron, writing on the fifteenth of June, argues this is a sideshow next to the real problem — that the labs' business models, in his blunt phrase, simply do not work. On his telling, the rush to cheaper models is not a market maturing but an "tokenomics" bubble bursting: enterprises were moved onto pay-as-you-go billing early this year, saw the true cost of the tokens they were burning, and revolted, forcing providers into drastic price cuts. Squeezing the same work out of six-cent models, he says, is a symptom of that revolt, not proof of a healthy alternative.

The thing to watch, where both men actually meet, is enterprise renewal rates over the next few quarters. If spending holds and grows, the optimists are right; if it falls, the sceptic is.

For our world, a few thoughts. The real lesson for a surgery is that the model sitting behind an AI scribe is now a swappable part, and a cheaper one. Worth asking your supplier which model they use, whether it can change without notice, and what happens to the price at renewal. The sovereignty point matters more for the NHS than the cost: a tool tied to a single American model could go dark with no warning, so favour suppliers that can fail over and that keep patient data within the United Kingdom. And cheaper is not the same as safe — a model that is cheap and scores well on coding is not automatically fit for clinical summaries, where a quiet omission matters far more. The clinician's check of the note remains the safeguard.
