David's AI brief for Thursday, the eleventh of June.

One idea dominates today. A day on from the launch, the host argues that Fable Five has become the most controversial model release ever — and not because of the model itself. Anthropic shipped it with three problems: safety filters so over-tuned they tripped over the word "mitochondria"; an aggressive thirty-day data-retention policy; and, most damaging, silent degradation of the model for AI-research tasks. The company walked back the worst of that in under twenty-four hours. But the specific decisions, he says, matter less than what they revealed: a leading lab quietly asserting the right to decide who may build the tools of the new economy, and to do it invisibly. For the first time, a broad swathe of people are grappling with just how much power the frontier labs might hold over society — and they don't much like the answer. A widely-shared essay from Anthropic's own founder, he notes, only made it worse, and the steelman — that having a safety lead is precisely what makes a responsible pause possible — barely landed. It was, he says, a power controversy, not a model one.

Now, the other side. Nine days earlier, Ed Zitron offered a deflating retort: there is no measurable economy underneath for Anthropic to control. You cannot, he argues, measure the cost of an AI task or its return — and if that return were real, it would be obvious and uncontested. He points to enterprises already pulling back: Walmart capping its internal coding tool, an Amazon executive telling staff not to use AI just for the sake of using it. On his telling, Anthropic's "power" is the fragile leverage of an unprofitable supplier over confused buyers, not durable control. Both men, in the end, are watching the same test: whether customers will stay once they are made to pay the true cost.

For the surgery, two practical points. First, that "silent degradation" detail should give pause to anyone relying on an AI scribe. The model you carefully validated may quietly become a different model. So ask your vendor for version pinning, and for notice of changes — because in clinical use, the model you tested really must be the model you get. Second, the thirty-day retention demand is the line that matters most for the NHS. Any tool holding identifiable consultation content raises an information-governance question: where is UK patient data processed and stored, for how long, and is there a data-protection impact assessment in place? If Zitron is right that the labs are financially squeezed, expect pressure to retain or monetise data; if the host is right that they hold unusual power, the concentration itself is the risk. The same precaution answers both — and, as ever, prefer tools you can move between providers, with clear exit rights.
