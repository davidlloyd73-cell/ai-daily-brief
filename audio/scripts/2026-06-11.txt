David's AI brief for Thursday, the eleventh of June.

The one idea today is that the row over Fable five was never really about the model; it was about power. Anthropic shipped its new flagship with three things that lit a fire: a data-retention policy holding prompts for thirty days with human review, classifiers so trigger-happy they locked out biomedical researchers, and, most damaging, a silent degradation that deliberately made the model worse at frontier AI research without telling anyone. It walked back the worst of it in under twenty-four hours, apologising for getting the balance wrong. But the specific decisions matter less than what they revealed: a single private lab asserting the right to decide, invisibly, who gets to build the tools of the new economy. For the first time a broad swathe of people are grappling with just how much power the frontier labs may hold, and they do not like it. Around this swirl the week's vast numbers: a five-hundred-billion-dollar OpenAI data-centre campus proposed on federal land in Ohio, Nvidia backstopping a data-centre deal for the first time, and Oracle's shares falling eleven per cent as its debt load passed a hundred billion dollars.

Now, the other side. Ed Zitron would challenge the very premise that Anthropic is so powerful. In a piece a few weeks back he painted a company propping up its numbers, arguing its single profitable quarter was an accounting artefact, timed to a heavily discounted compute deal and released on the day of Nvidia's earnings to keep the bubble inflated. On his reading, Anthropic's costs still rise in lockstep with its revenue, so the supposed gatekeeper is less an emperor than a heavily subsidised tenant that cannot yet stand on its own books.

So the buried question is whether Anthropic's power is durable or borrowed. Both men agree it is, for now, setting terms others must accept. What divides them is whether that leverage rests on self-sustaining economics or on cheap compute and bubble capital, something only audited accounts, and whether enterprise customers actually stay after this week, can settle. Both are polemicists with incentives: the host bullish and selling adoption, Zitron certain of a bubble and selling the autopsy. The signal, despite their opposite instincts, is that this week's behaviour looked like insecurity, not confidence. Watch enterprise retention, and Anthropic's first audited figures once it floats.

For general practice, the practical lesson is about data. The loudest objection was that thirty-day retention applied even to customers promised zero retention, and Microsoft restricted the tool within the hour. For us, under UK data-protection law and the duty of confidentiality, that is the whole game: never put identifiable patient information into a consumer AI, and make sure any clinical tool contractually guarantees no human review and no training on your inputs. And there is a quieter warning about dependence: if one shaky vendor underpins your letters, your triage and your scribe, a sudden policy change lands on you with nowhere to turn. Keep your data portable.
