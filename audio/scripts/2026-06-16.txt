David's AI brief for Tuesday, the sixteenth of June.

One idea anchors today — a long read, recorded before the Fable blow-up. The host's argument is bracing: the American economy now is the AI trade. Artificial-intelligence investment drives the bulk of economic growth, and that capital keeps flowing only as long as token consumption keeps rising fast. But enterprises have moved from comfortable assisted-AI budgets to the harder reality of agentic AI, and they are slapping on spending caps — Uber blew through its entire annual AI budget in four months, then capped staff at fifteen hundred dollars a month. The only force, he says, that can give the labs their endless token growth and give enterprises enough value to lift those caps is mass-scale, high-quality AI training — moving every knowledge worker from assisted to agentic AI. And right now, he admits, that training market is an abysmal failure.

Now, the other side. Writing the day before, Ed Zitron reads exactly the same facts as terminal. To him, usage-based billing has triggered a revolt in the industry's customer base, because what the tokens cost is far higher than the value they create. And the very move the host predicts — labs pouring money into training people to use more — Zitron treats as proof of desperation: manufacturing token burn to feed a circular economy with no honest demand beneath it. Both men, tellingly, fixate on the same enterprise caps and the same push to make people burn more tokens. The signal, despite their opposite instincts, is that both independently land on the same question — whether those caps reflect a fixable skills gap, or a permanent gap between what AI costs and what it is worth. One thing worth flagging plainly: the host himself sells training, so he has some skin in his own remedy.

What does this mean for general practice? The host's sharpest idea is what he calls the "known-ROI bias" — that spending caps quietly push people toward safe, do-today's-work-a-bit-faster uses, and away from the bolder experiments that create the next generation of value. The NHS, frankly, lives in that bias permanently, and for a GP that is not all bad: it means AI arrives conservatively, through proven jobs like documentation, rather than speculative autonomy. He also frames "managing agents" as a genuinely new skill, closer to management than to information technology. If that is right, the thing to build in a practice is supervision — deciding what an agent may do unsupervised, such as drafting recall letters, and what always needs a human signature. And a closing, practical note: watch your scribe's price, not its promises. When contracts renew, the pressure described here may show up as higher prices or tighter caps — so budget for it, and keep a manual fallback.
