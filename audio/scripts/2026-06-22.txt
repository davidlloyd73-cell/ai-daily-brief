David's AI brief for Monday, the twenty-second of June.

The one idea today is that a Chinese open-weight model called GLM five point two may finally have broken the so-called two-horse race in artificial intelligence. For a long time the pattern has been wearily familiar: a new Chinese model arrives, scores brilliantly on the benchmarks, everyone declares the gap has closed, and a fortnight later nobody is using it. GLM five point two appears to be different. Days after release, respected practitioners are still raving about it. It topped the rankings for building websites, beating even Anthropic's Fable five, and it has landed at a moment when the ban on Fable and a shortage of computing power already had companies rethinking which model they reach for.

Nathaniel Whittemore, who presents the brief, is careful about the lesson. He is not telling anyone to rush out and buy graphics chips. Running this model properly is expensive: roughly four hundred thousand pounds-worth of hardware, or twenty thousand pounds a month to rent. His point is gentler and, I think, wiser. The era of two or three dominant models is giving way to a flowering of many different architectures, and so every organisation should keep a corner free to experiment with the alternatives, optimising for speed, cost or quality as the task demands.

Now, the other side. The writer Ed Zitron would say that getting excited about another model topping a leaderboard is precisely the trap. His recent piece argues that artificial intelligence is, in his words, slowing down just as it needs to speed up. No amount of impressive benchmarks, he insists, changes the fact that you still cannot measure the cost of a task or its return on investment. The real story, he says, is large companies capping their spending because they cannot link the bills to any value, sitting atop an infrastructure that demands trillions in revenue he sees no way of earning.

For us in general practice, a few thoughts. First, the sensible posture is to keep a sandbox but judge any AI scribe on the consultation in front of you, not on this week's leaderboard. Trial it on real clinics, measure whether it genuinely saves time without adding a correction burden, and never rebuild your workflow around a model that could be withdrawn or repriced. Second, open models raise a real and welcome question for the Health Service: a model you can host on your own infrastructure could keep patient data inside the N-H-S rather than sending it to an American vendor. That is a genuine information-governance advantage, though the cost means it is a question for the integrated care board, not the front desk. And whichever man is right, watch the renewal price, not the demo, and make sure your transcripts and templates leave with you if you ever switch supplier.
