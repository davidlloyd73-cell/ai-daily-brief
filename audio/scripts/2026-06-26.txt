David's AI brief for Friday, the twenty-sixth of June.

Today's one idea comes from a new report by Glean and the Work AI Institute, and it has a memorable name: bot sitting. The picture is a paradox. Eighty-seven percent of digital workers now use AI, three quarters say it makes them more productive, and on average they save around eleven hours a week. And yet only thirteen percent say their organisation is performing significantly better as a result. Where does the time go? About six and a half hours of it goes straight back into what the report calls bot sitting: feeding the AI missing context, checking its work, debugging its mistakes, re-running prompts, and cleaning up the confident answers that turn out to be wrong. That is more than a third of all the time people spend with AI.

There is an uglier cousin, too, which the report bleeps as bot shitting: cognitively offloading so much that you ship the first output that looks good enough, without being able to explain or defend it. Tellingly, when AI-assisted work fails, forty percent of workers blame the machine and only twenty-nine percent own the mistake.

Nathaniel Whittemore, who hosts the Brief, partly disagrees with the report's explanation. His argument is that individual gains never automatically become organisational gains. You can save an hour each, and your company can still perform no better, unless there is a real mechanism to turn that saving into something. So the differentiator, he says, is not the AI you buy. It is the human infrastructure you build — the training, the governance, the accountability. The work of AI transformation, in his phrase, is transformation itself.

Now, the other side. Writing this week, Ed Zitron would say that the transformation story is itself a kind of ritual. He calls the tech industry the largest cargo cult of all time — a trillion dollars poured into AI, he argues, not because real value is arriving, but because the giants have run out of other ideas and dress the spending up as disruption. Where Whittemore sees a hard transition worth crossing, Zitron sees a movement going through the motions of innovation while the returns never actually show up. The honest thing is that both men agree on the awkward fact at the centre of today's report: individual AI use is not yet becoming organisational performance. They simply disagree on whether that gap closes or never existed.

A few thoughts for the surgery. Bot sitting has a direct analogue in the AI scribe: it drafts quickly, but you still read, correct and sign. The figure that matters is minutes saved per consultation after that supervision, measured on your own list, not the vendor's headline. Bot shitting is really a governance point: a letter you have not fully read is still your responsibility, and blaming the tool is no defence. And the adoption lesson is human — the colleague who quietly models good use moves a team far more than any mandate.

That's the brief — source, The AI Daily Brief.
