David's AI brief for Wednesday, the tenth of June.

The one idea today is Fable five. Anthropic has released the first of a new, higher class of model, above haiku, sonnet and opus, and the host calls it, fairly undisputedly, the best AI we have yet been able to use. The benchmark jumps are large enough to actually mean something: eighty per cent on a tough software-engineering test where rivals sit in the high fifties, and more than double the previous score on a new measure of whether code is genuinely good enough to merge into production. Stripe reportedly used it to perform a fifty-million-line code migration in a single day, a job that would have taken a team more than two months by hand. But the deeper point is what it asks of us. In a world where you pay by usage, we have to become efficiency optimisers, matching the right model to the right job. And we need a new skill the host calls task imagination: the ability to hand a capable agent days of responsibility, not minutes of small tasks. The frontier, he argues, has moved from answers, to tasks, to responsibilities, and most of us simply are not dreaming big enough to use it.

Now, the other side. Ed Zitron, writing two days earlier, says precisely the opposite: that AI is slowing down, not leaping. The booster excitement, in his telling, is a con built around a technology designed to ingratiate itself at the highest possible cost. A model that one-shots an impressive demo, he would argue, does nothing to close the only gap that matters: the roughly two trillion dollars a year that all this infrastructure must somehow generate by 2030 to justify what is being spent on it.

Strip away the theatrics and both men are really arguing about one thing: the rate at which real, economically useful capability is improving. The host sees one-shotting and hundred-hour runs as a genuine step change; Zitron sees clever demonstrations that do not pay the interest on the data centres. Both are polemicists with incentives, the host bullish and selling transformation, Zitron certain of a bubble and selling the autopsy. Tellingly, they agree on the test that counts: whether capability converts into the revenue it needs. Watch the big providers' AI revenue against their capital spending over the next two quarters.

For general practice, two modest notes. Those over-tuned safety filters are a real nuisance: Fable reportedly refused basic biology, flagging the word cancer as a security risk and pausing on mitochondria. If a general AI blocks a legitimate clinical query, that is the filter misfiring, not the medicine, so rephrase or use a properly clinical tool. And there is a safe, GP-sized version of task imagination: not diagnose this patient, but watch my normal results and draft the routine recall letters for me to check. Delegate bounded, auditable admin, with a clinician always signing off.

That's the brief. Source, The AI Daily Brief.
