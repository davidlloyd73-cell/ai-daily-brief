David's AI brief for Wednesday, the tenth of June.

One idea leads today. Anthropic has shipped Fable Five, its first Mythos-class model and, fairly undisputedly, the best AI any of us have used. The benchmark jump is large enough to actually mean something, but the standout is agentic coding — the model one-shotting work that used to take whole teams months. The host's real argument, though, is about us, not the machine. In a world where you pay by usage, he says, we all have to become token-efficiency optimisers, matching the right model to the right job. And we need a new skill he calls "task imagination" — the ability to hand an agent days of responsibility, rather than minutes of work. The frontier has moved from giving AI answers, to giving it tasks, to giving it responsibilities — and most of us simply aren't dreaming big enough to use it.

Now, the other side. Writing two days earlier, the critic Ed Zitron sees almost the reverse. To him, these models keep producing a great deal of impressive-looking but unused, insecure work; the benchmarks are rigged; and the only thing reliably accelerating is cost. He reads the very efficiency drive the host celebrates — Uber capping its staff at fifteen hundred dollars a month, firms throttling their spend — as a sign that AI is slowing down, because customers recoil the moment they see the true bill. Strip away the rhetoric and both men are watching one dial: whether all that token consumption reflects durable value, or merely subsidised activity. The thing to watch is simple enough — whether capped companies raise or cut their budgets next.

So what does this mean for the surgery? Two thoughts. First, the upgrade that matters in clinical work isn't raw power; it's that Fable, in the host's telling, disagreed with him and then changed its position on new evidence, rather than flattering him. A tool that simply confirms your working diagnosis is dangerous; one that pushes back can be genuinely useful. It is worth testing any clinical AI by giving it a deliberately wrong steer, and seeing whether it argues back. Second, "task imagination" is interesting, but a consulting room needs something narrower — a scribe that reliably does one bounded job, turning a ten-minute consultation into an accurate, editable note. Whichever man turns out to be right, the discipline is unchanged: read every word before it enters the record. And one practical note — usage-based pricing is the labs' problem quietly becoming the practice's. If your scribe contract is a fixed per-seat fee, you're insulated for now, but it's worth knowing what happens at renewal.
