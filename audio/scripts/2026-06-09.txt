David's AI brief for Tuesday, the ninth of June.

The one idea today is that the thing we lump together as artificial intelligence may be quietly splitting in two. On the same Monday that OpenAI filed confidentially to go public, it published a manifesto declaring its third phase: no longer just chasing raw capability, but making AI, in its words, abundant, affordable and useful for everyone. And on the very same day, Apple finally shipped a competent Siri, with message summaries, calendar actions and basic web search. Fine, but minimal, and with almost no bearing on the agentic systems now reshaping actual work. Put those two side by side and the host's conclusion is hard to dodge. The consumer chatbot that answers your questions, and the fleets of synthetic employees being handed days of responsibility, may no longer be the same technology at all, and perhaps it is time we talked about them as two separate things. Around this sit the week's eye-watering numbers: SpaceX's float two times oversubscribed, a hundred and fifty billion dollars of orders; Google ordering three million chips from Intel for 2028; and Wall Street now exploring ways to trade computing power like a commodity, much as it trades oil.

Now, the other side. Ed Zitron, writing a week earlier, would pour cold water on the whole celebration. His argument is blunt: this grand new phase rests on a business with no demonstrable return. Nobody, he says, can actually measure AI's return on investment, or even the cost of a single task, because billing by the token makes spending wildly unpredictable and the technology's habit of hallucinating makes its output unreliable. He points to Uber reportedly burning a whole year's token budget in four months, and one firm that accidentally spent five hundred million dollars in a single month.

The honest reading is that both men are circling the same question, whether work AI produces value that genuinely exceeds its cost, and neither has the answer yet. Both are practised polemicists with skin in the game: the host bullish and selling adoption, Zitron certain of a bubble and selling the takedown. The signal is that they now agree on the real problem, which is measuring the cost. Watch for the first clean enterprise figures once token-based billing has fully landed.

A couple of thoughts for general practice. The consumer-versus-work split maps neatly onto our world: a chatbot drafting a patient leaflet is a very different beast from an agent reconciling a discharge summary. The value for the NHS is far more likely to sit in that unglamorous back-office work than in any patient-facing chatbot. And watch the pricing model: pay-per-use behaves very differently from a flat licence when winter doubles your throughput, so ask any scribe vendor now how their costs scale with use.
