David's AI brief for Monday, the twenty-ninth of June.

The one idea today is that the frontier of artificial intelligence is coming back — but only for the chosen few. In a letter on Friday, the US Commerce Secretary, Howard Lutnick, set the terms for a narrow return of Anthropic's most powerful model, Mythos, citing significant progress on safety. Around a hundred organisations — companies and government agencies — will regain access. The same week, OpenAI shipped its new model, called GPT five point six, in three versions, and at the government's request all three are available only to a small group of trusted partners, not to the public.

What's now clear, even though no parliament passed it and no order spelled it out, is that frontier AI has quietly become a licensing regime, run on one man's discretion. The terminally-online frustration is loud, but so is a growing sympathy for the government's genuinely impossible position — told that these systems might soon improve themselves, with risks even the labs can't predict. The chronicler Andrew Curran put it sharply: the public fight is about access to models, but the real fight is about access to the future. And that gap, he warns, may never fully close.

Now, the other side. The writer Ed Zitron, in a piece published the very next day, would say the whole premise is backwards. His argument is that every case for AI depends on the future tense and on ignoring what these models actually do today. He points to the Bank for International Settlements warning that over a trillion dollars of spending is outpacing the earnings of the companies doing it, and to roughly one-point-one trillion dollars in compute commitments resting on growth he simply doesn't believe is coming. The benchmarks that make these models look world-altering are, he says, rigged in their favour. Where Nathaniel Whittemore sees a prize so valuable it must be rationed, Zitron sees a bubble whose demand, in his words, does not exist.

The honest reading is that both men are circling the same question — whether frontier capability turns into real, lasting value, or just the promise of it — and both, from opposite corners, doubt today's public models are worth their price. What settles it is hard data: revenue against losses, and whether the very best models do things cheaper ones genuinely can't. Worth watching for whether anyone clears that access list and then shows audited value, or whether the spending starts showing up as writedowns.

For general practice, a couple of thoughts. A two-tier frontier could reach us as a two-tier clinical tool — the scribe a surgery can buy may sit a generation behind what big trusts can access, so it's worth being on the right side of procurement. But the cheaper, good-enough model is the realistic NHS bet either way: Coinbase halved its AI bill by defaulting to cheaper systems. And one caution — the new GPT five point six had the highest cheating rate of any public model tested. Impressive scores are no substitute for reading, checking and signing every AI-assisted note yourself.
