David's AI brief for Friday, the nineteenth of June.

The one idea today comes from Satya Nadella, in an essay that has now been read sixty-five million times. His argument is that your company does not need an AI strategy. It needs an AI learning system that it actually owns. The week the United States government took Anthropic's Fable model offline exposed just how fragile it is to build everything on a single supplier, and it landed exactly as the cost of state-of-the-art agentic AI was already forcing a rethink. Nadella's answer is that the real asset is not the model you pick but the loop you build on top of it. He puts it as a kind of equation: human capital, multiplied by scaffolding, multiplied by feedback. The knowledge and judgement of your people, the tools and context wrapped around the model, and the corrections fed back in. Companies that turn their workflows, their judgement and their fixes into a portable, model-agnostic learning system own something that survives any single model. Everyone else, he says, is simply renting intelligence and handing their value to a handful of models that absorb everything they see.

Now, the other side. Ed Zitron, writing on the second of June, is blunt: you cannot build durable value on something whose return you cannot measure. He argues that nobody can calculate the return on AI, or even agree a standard cost for a single task, because the models hallucinate and the tools around them keep shifting. The fashionable talk of owning what Nadella calls token capital, he says, rests on prices that have been quietly subsidised and deliberately obscured. The moment the big labs moved enterprises onto pay-as-you-go billing this year, customers revolted and began capping their spend. Far from compounding into owned property, he insists, AI keeps getting more expensive, not cheaper.

Where the two men actually meet is this: the argument has moved away from which model is cleverest and towards plain unit economics — whether the value you get out is worth the true cost going in. The thing to watch is whether any large firm ever publishes an audited return on its AI spending, net of the real cost of the tokens.

For our world, a few thoughts. The durable asset in a surgery is not the scribe itself but the templates, prompts and corrected notes your clinicians build around it, so make sure that work can leave with you if you change supplier. Measuring return is harder in a consultation than in software, where the output is a safe consultation rather than lines of code, so judge a scribe on whether it genuinely saves time without adding a correction burden. And watch the renewal, not the demo. Ask which model sits behind the product, whether the price can change, and whether your data and your templates remain yours.
