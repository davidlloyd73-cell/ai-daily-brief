David's AI brief for Wednesday, the twenty-fourth of June.

Today's one idea is a quiet but significant shift in how we actually work with these tools. Anthropic has released something called Claude Tag, which drops the full power of its coding assistant straight into Slack — not as another window to type prompts into, but as a persistent teammate that sits in your channels, picks up the context of what the team is discussing, and gets on with things. You tag it with a request, and it breaks the job into stages, writes or merges code, runs an analysis, or chases an open task. Anthropic says its own internal version now produces about sixty-five percent of its product team's code, and one staffer claims he does over ninety percent of his work this way.

Nathaniel Whittemore, who presents the show, reads this as the third major redesign of how we deal with these systems — first it was a website you visited, then an app you downloaded, and now a standing colleague that works alongside you. He draws out five shifts: from special apps to the workplaces we already use; from a private chatbot to a shared teammate; from one person's context to the whole team's; from prompting to delegation; and from a handy personal tool to something the organisation actually depends on.

Now, the other side. Writing the day before, the critic Ed Zitron looked at the very same talk of moving "from prompting to loops" and saw something far less flattering — a self-interested push to get people burning ever more tokens, and so spending more money. He notes that the executives championing it are, in his phrase, allowed to burn a hundred and thirty thousand dollars a month in tokens, and he casts much enterprise adoption as a cargo cult: bosses demanding everyone "do AI" because everyone else is, without really knowing what it does. The number both men curiously agree on is this — the average company has spent around eleven and a half million on AI this year, and most cannot show a single dollar back.

For us in general practice, a few thoughts. The move from prompting to delegating is the natural next step for anyone already working with an AI scribe — but watch the meter, and confirm a tool still saves real clinician time once the introductory pricing ends. Second, one warning from the show travels straight to us: an assistant sitting in a shared channel reads everyone's messages. In a surgery that is an information-governance question, with patient-identifiable data and our usual duties of confidentiality, to be settled before the tool is dropped in, not after. And third, that shared scepticism is the useful one: demand demonstrated, audited time-savings in your own setting before you reorganise rotas around any tool. Measure it here, on your own patients, not on a vendor's slide.
