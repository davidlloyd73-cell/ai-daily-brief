## Fable 5 Shut Down by US Government

_A national-security directive forced Anthropic to abruptly cut off Fable 5 and Mythos 5 for all foreign nationals worldwide — the first time the US has hit the kill switch on a frontier model. The pretext is a thin jailbreak; the real story is a precedent that splits the world by model access and turns Anthropic's safety rhetoric into the rope it hangs by._

## The One Idea

### The Fable shutdown wasn't about a jailbreak — it was a kill switch, and Anthropic spent a year loading it.

The US government invoked national-security export controls to force Anthropic to disable Fable 5 and Mythos 5 for every foreign national on earth — including its own non-citizen employees — over a narrow jailbreak that surfaced already-public vulnerabilities. The industry's fury splits two ways at once: the government's pretext is cartoonishly thin and its export strategy incoherent, and Anthropic spent a year insisting its models were too dangerous for anyone but itself to be trusted with — then acted shocked when Washington took that premise literally. Either way, the precedent is the story: model access is now a sovereign weapon, and no lab's deployment is safe from a 5 PM Friday directive.

## The Other Side

The loudest economic claim in today's brief is NLW's — that the whole US economy now rests on Anthropic and OpenAI revenue rising and investors continuing to fund the buildout, which is why the Fable shutdown is so dangerous. Writing the day before, Zitron argues the dependency is the danger: both labs have just filed to go public precisely because they 'burn billions of dollars a year and have no path to profitability,' racing for what he calls 'exit liquidity' before the bill lands. The trillion-dollar valuations NLW says are 'now in question' were, on his telling, never sound to begin with. Where NLW sees a national asset a clumsy government must stop endangering, Zitron sees the bubble itself — and a government merely poking it a little early.

Source: Ed Zitron, "The Silicon Valley Bubble (Part 1)", 12 June 2026 — https://www.wheresyoured.at/premium-the-silicon-valley-bubble-part-1/

## The Truth Beneath

Strip the rhetoric and both men orbit one variable: whether the frontier labs have a path to profit or merely a path to more capital. They accept the same facts — Anthropic and OpenAI have both filed to go public, both burn billions a year, and trillion-dollar valuations now hang on events neither company controls. The empirical question that will resolve is whether the flotation filings show margins bending toward profit or losses widening; the audited prospectus financials are the observable that settles it. For NLW to be vindicated, revenue must scale and the buildout stay funded, so 'the economy rests on this' holds. For Zitron, the filings must expose the dog — no margin, endless burn, exit liquidity dressed as growth. Both are polemicists: NLW sells optimism and the anti-doom case, Zitron sells bubble-certainty and anti-hype. The signal is where they agree despite opposite priors — both now call the valuation precarious. Watch the prospectus.

## GP's Corner

_Claude's notes on what today means for UK general practice and the NHS - commentary, not from the podcast itself._

### A 5 PM Friday could take your scribe offline

The story here is a frontier model switched off worldwide overnight by a single government letter. Your AI scribe almost certainly sits on top of a US lab's model. If NLW is right that the economy now leans on these labs, that concentration is itself the systemic risk; if Zitron is right that they are unprofitable and racing to float, the more likely failure is the vendor folding or repricing once subsidies end. Either reading points to the same precaution: don't let a single foreign model become load-bearing in clinical workflow without a manual fallback and a clear answer to 'what do we do the morning it stops working.'

### Where does the patient data sit, and for how long

Anthropic argued it needs thirty days of retained customer data to catch jailbreaks. Set aside the politics: any tool that holds identifiable consultation content for a retention window is an information-governance question for the practice, not just the supplier. Ask your scribe vendor plainly where UK patient data is processed and stored, for how long, and whether a foreign government directive could reach it. The Fable episode shows those are no longer theoretical concerns.

### The case for sovereign or UK-hosted tooling just got stronger

The sharpest line in the brief was that model access is becoming a question of citizenship. For the NHS that is an argument — not yet urgent, but real — for preferring clinical AI that can run on UK or European infrastructure, or that is portable between models. It is the same procurement discipline as last week: shorter terms, exit rights, and the ability to move your templates and data if the ground shifts under a single provider.

## NLW's Takes

### This is Anthropic's 'Altman firing' moment with the government

NLW compared it to when Sam Altman was ousted and reinstated at OpenAI — the moment Microsoft began quietly building resilience outside OpenAI's models. Once a partner proves itself too capricious to trust, the relationship is permanently reshaped, even if it nominally continues.

### The whole US economy now rests on this relationship staying intact

NLW argued the American economy effectively depends on Anthropic and OpenAI revenue rising and investors continuing to fund the buildout. The damage from this move, he said, isn't just to Anthropic — it's hard to overstate the hit to the entire economy.
