CEO-Led AI Gets 3X the ROI

A no-single-story day where a million small stories become tea leaves: OpenAI tapes out its first in-house chip, Anthropic ratchets up its China-distillation rhetoric, Micron's blowout earnings nuke the bubble jitters — and a KPMG survey lands the punchline, that the CEOs who actually own AI strategy are seeing roughly three times the ROI.

Source: https://aidailybrief.ai/e/2026-06-25

▶ Listen — today’s brief (3 min)

The One Idea

Who owns AI matters more than which AI you buy.

KPMG's latest Quarterly Pulse Survey, run during the agentic period rather than the before times, shows AI confidence and strategic priority both climbing. But the standout finding is about accountability: organizations with clear ownership are 3x more likely to report ROI, and when the CEO is personally accountable, established-ROI rates jump from 4% to 14% and meaningful-business-value reports go from 21% to 57%. AI is no longer an IT tool-selection problem — it's an organizational design challenge, and leadership owns it.

The Other Side

The edition's lead is that CEOs who personally own AI strategy report roughly three times the ROI. Zitron's argument is that AI's return on investment cannot actually be measured at all — there is no standard way to price a task given hallucinations and an ever-shifting list of agentic harnesses — so "reported ROI" from a survey is sentiment, not arithmetic. He points to a Bain study of 951 executives whose own conclusion was, in his quote, "The technology worked. The value didn't arrive," and warns that firms self-funding the next wave from past savings are making "a circular bet with a structural leak." And he argues that making AI use a CEO-owned KPI is precisely what manufactures inflated self-reports: people conditioned to ignore the true, now-rising token cost keep claiming value while nobody can point to a measured dollar back.

Source: Ed Zitron, “AI Doesn't Have ROI”, 2 June 2026 (from 2 June’s edition)

The Truth Beneath

Both men orbit one variable: whether enterprise AI yields a real, measurable return once a firm pays its true, unsubsidised cost. Both accept that companies are spending heavily, that token-based pricing has now arrived, and that cost accounting is poor — this very edition notes only about a third of organisations can fully see their AI costs. The empirical question that will settle it is whether audited outcomes, not survey sentiment, show a positive net return; the observable that resolves it is hard productivity or profit data at real prices set against spend. The residue is pure worldview: Whittemore reads rising executive confidence and "reported ROI" as signal, Zitron reads the same self-reports as a con that dissolves the moment anyone measures. Whittemore is vindicated if owned, accountable AI shows durable audited gains; Zitron if "reported" value keeps failing to become a demonstrable dollar. Both are polemicists with incentives — NLW bullish and anti-doom, Zitron bubble-certain with a paid newsletter — and the signal is that both concede firms cannot yet measure the cost. Watch whether anyone publishes audited, priced-at-cost ROI once the subsidies end.

GP’s Corner

Claude’s notes on what today means for general practice and the NHS — commentary, not from the podcast itself.

"Reported ROI" is not measured ROI — hold AI scribes to the harder test

The headline finding is a survey one: where the CEO personally owns AI, far more leaders say it delivers meaningful value. That is sentiment, not arithmetic, and Zitron's point lands squarely — a Bain study he cites concluded "the technology worked, the value didn't arrive." For a surgery weighing an AI scribe, the translation is to insist on a measured saving in your own setting: minutes saved per consultation, letters turned round faster, fewer unactioned results — audited locally, not taken from a vendor's slide or a confidence survey. If NLW is right there is real value to capture; if Zitron is right it is mostly perception, the same local measurement protects you.

Watch the meter as token billing arrives

Both sides agree on one uncomfortable thing: only about a third of organisations can fully see their AI operating costs, and pricing is shifting from flat subscriptions toward paying per use. A practice or ICB adopting any AI tool should know what a unit of work actually costs before reorganising around it, and confirm a scribe still saves real clinician time once any introductory pricing ends. Cheap-feeling now is not the same as cheap when the subsidy stops.

Ownership and accountability are sound NHS instincts — regardless of who is right on ROI

Stripped of the hype, the edition's organisational point is reasonable for general practice: decide who is accountable for each AI-assisted decision before deploying. Who signs off that a scribe's summary is safe? Who reviews errors? That clarity is good clinical governance whether AI proves transformative or turns out to be an expensive disappointment, and it is the part of this survey that travels to a surgery without needing to believe the 3x figure.
