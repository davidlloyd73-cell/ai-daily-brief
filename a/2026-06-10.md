## Fable 5 Raises the Bar for AI Ambition

_Anthropic ships Fable 5 — the first Mythos-class model and, fairly undisputedly, the best AI we've ever used. But at this level, getting the most out of state-of-the-art isn't your old prompts with a new model: it's controversial guardrails, usage-based reality, and a new skill called task imagination._

## The One Idea

### Fable 5 doesn't just beat the benchmarks — it raises the bar for ambition.

Anthropic's first Mythos-class model is a genuine leap, especially on agentic coding, where it one-shots things that used to take teams months. But the real story is what it demands of us: in a usage-based world, we have to become token-efficiency optimizers who match models to use cases, and we have to develop "task imagination" — the ability to hand an agent days of responsibility, not minutes of tasks. The frontier has moved from answers to tasks to responsibilities, and most of us aren't dreaming big enough to use it.

## The Other Side

NLW's lead is that Fable 5 is the best model we've ever used and a genuine leap that should make us dream bigger and develop "task imagination." Writing two days earlier, Zitron argues almost the reverse: the models keep shoving out a great deal of "stuff" that is unused, insecure slopware, the benchmarks are rigged, and the only thing reliably accelerating is cost. He reads the very token-efficiency turn NLW celebrates — Uber's 1,500-dollar-a-month cap, firms throttling spend — as evidence that "AI is slowing down," because customers recoil the moment they see the true bill. Where NLW sees ambition we are too timid to use, Zitron sees an unprofitable product whose revenue growth is stalling precisely when it must speed up.

Source: Ed Zitron, "AI Is Slowing Down", 8 June 2026 — https://www.wheresyoured.at/ai-is-slowing-down/

## The Truth Beneath

Strip the rhetoric and both men orbit one variable: whether rising token consumption reflects durable value or merely subsidised activity. They accept the same facts — Fable can one-shot work that once took teams, enterprises like Uber are capping token spend, and billing moved to usage in early 2026. The empirical question that will resolve is whether heavier agent use yields output enterprises keep paying for at unsubsidised prices; the observable that settles it is whether capped firms raise or cut their token budgets next, and whether shipped features and revenue can be traced to the agents. The residue is pure worldview: is "task imagination" a real skill gap or a marketing frame to drive token burn? NLW is vindicated if caps lift and real output follows; Zitron if caps harden as true costs bite. Both are polemicists — NLW bullish and anti-doom, Zitron bubble-certain and anti-hype — and the signal is that both now fixate on the same Uber cap. Watch next quarter's enterprise token budgets.

## GP's Corner

_Claude's notes on what today means for UK general practice and the NHS - commentary, not from the podcast itself._

### Disagreement without flattery is the upgrade that matters clinically

NLW's standout was a model that disagreed with him and then updated on new evidence rather than telling him what he wanted to hear. For any clinical decision support that property matters far more than raw capability: a tool that sycophantically confirms your working diagnosis is dangerous. It is worth testing any clinical AI the same way — feed it a deliberately wrong steer and see whether it pushes back or simply agrees.

### "Task imagination" is interesting; bounded reliability is what a surgery needs

The episode urges us to hand agents days of responsibility, not minutes of tasks. In a consulting room the relevant test is narrower: does the tool reliably do one bounded job — turning a ten-minute consultation into an accurate, editable note? If NLW is right that the frontier has leapt, scribes will quietly absorb more (coding, referral drafting); if Zitron is right that the output is impressive-looking but unreliable, the risk is exactly the long-running autonomy he warns about. Either way the discipline is unchanged: read every word before it enters the record.

### Usage-based pricing is the lab's problem becoming the practice's

The real-world thread here is that token billing makes cost unpredictable. Practices on fixed per-seat scribe contracts are insulated for now, but the labs have moved to usage pricing and vendors may follow. Worth checking whether your scribe contract is a fixed per-seat fee or exposed to token-based pricing, and what happens at renewal.

## NLW's Takes

### The first model that disagrees — and updates without kowtowing

NLW's standout everyday win: in a strategic debate, Fable disagreed clearly, then updated its position on new information without collapsing into telling him what he wanted to hear. That alone is a massive upgrade for strategic ideation, a top real-world use case.

### NLW: Fable rebuilt three of his own products in single shots

In hours of unattended work, Fable rebuilt Superintelligent's audit input system on Whisper, the Agent Transformation Intensive site and platform, and turned AIDB's shareable-nuggets mockups into a real production pipeline. The takeaway: far less management, much bigger ambition.

### The new skill: task imagination

With models that can run for days, NLW argues we'll all have to up-level ambition — and become token-efficiency optimizers who match models to use cases. Nate B. Jones's framing: most of us have nothing that's ever taken even an hour on AI, so the scarce skill is imagining tasks worth handing to a model that works for days.
