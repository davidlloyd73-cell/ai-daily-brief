The Right Way to Deal With AI Data Centers

A slower news cycle gives NLW room to defuse one of AI's most contentious fights — data centers — by actually checking the water and electricity numbers. Plus a Mythos/NSA correction, OpenAI's cyber escalation, another SpaceX compute deal, and a $200B Google wobble over two departing researchers.

The One Idea

The data center fight needs a middle path between rollover and pitchforks.

Both sides of the data center debate are wildly reductive. The scary water and electricity numbers mostly don't survive contact with context — Amazon's entire global data center fleet uses a day's worth of US golf course watering. But communities aren't powerless either. The real opportunity isn't 'let big tech do whatever' or 'stop it entirely' — it's negotiating for serious economic benefits, like the Louisiana parish where teachers got $50,000 bonuses funded by Meta's campus. Move past the binary into the how-everyone-wins phase.

The Other Side

NLW wants communities to move past the binary and bargain hard with a real, valuable build-out. Zitron's prior question is whether that build-out is as real and as large as everyone assumes. He spent a week hunting for big data centres that broke ground in 2023 or 2024 and were actually finished and switched on, and came up, in his words, "surprisingly empty-handed." He argues nobody has yet built a true one-gigawatt facility, that "operational" routinely describes a campus where only a handful of the planned buildings have any gear inside, and that hyperscalers refuse to disclose how much capacity is genuinely online despite over eight hundred billion dollars of capex. On his telling, the fight over a data centre's water and electricity quietly assumes a scale that the disclosures do not actually support.

Source: Ed Zitron, “Where Are All The Data Centers?”, 12 May 2026 (from 12 May’s edition)

The Truth Beneath

Both men orbit one variable: how real and how large the AI data centre build-out actually is. Both accept that data centres exist, are being built and draw real water and power, and that reporting is muddy. The empirical question that will resolve it is how much capacity has been completed and switched on; the observable that settles it is disclosed, audited operational critical-IT load - finished megawatts and gigawatts, completion timelines, honest hyperscaler figures. The residue is pure worldview: Whittemore treats the build-out as a real, valuable thing worth negotiating local benefits over; Zitron treats much of the claimed capacity as phased public-relations vapour. Whittemore is vindicated if large finished capacity is verifiably online and communities win real concessions from real campuses; Zitron if gigawatt claims keep dissolving into a few populated buildings. Both are polemicists with incentives - Whittemore bullish and anti-doom, Zitron bubble-certain and anti-hype with a newsletter - so the signal is their shared distrust of headline numbers: he doubts the capacity figures, NLW the scary impact figures. Watch whether hyperscalers ever disclose real operational capacity.

GP’s Corner

Claude’s notes on what today means for general practice and the NHS — commentary, not from the podcast itself.

How big numbers mislead - a lesson that travels to health statistics

The edition's sharpest point is that astronomical-sounding figures grab attention precisely because most people have no sense of scale: Amazon's entire global data-centre water use for a year is roughly a single day of watering America's golf courses, and US leaky pipes waste about fifteen times all data-centre water combined. The same trick runs through health reporting - a 'doubling of risk' that moves a tiny absolute number, or a screening benefit quoted relatively rather than as numbers-needed-to-treat. If NLW is right, the antidote is always context and a denominator; if Zitron is right that headline numbers are often manufactured, the discipline is the same. It is a useful reminder for explaining risk to patients, and for reading the next alarming AI-and-NHS headline.

The 'how-everyone-wins' framing applied to AI in the surgery

NLW argues communities should stop choosing between rolling over and grabbing pitchforks, and instead negotiate concrete benefits - he cites a Louisiana parish where data-centre tax receipts funded fifty-thousand-dollar teacher bonuses. The parallel for general practice is to refuse the binary of either swallowing whatever a vendor offers or rejecting AI outright. When an integrated care board or supplier brings an AI scribe or triage tool, the practical questions are what the practice gets in return: training, data control, fair pricing, and a share of any efficiency, not just a licence fee.

But check whether the benefit is real before you bargain for it

Zitron's contrasting caution is worth holding alongside: he argues much of the claimed build-out is smaller and slower than the headlines suggest. The clinical translation is to verify that an AI tool's promised benefit is actually delivered and measured before reorganising around it. If NLW is right, there is genuine value to negotiate over; if Zitron is right, some of it is vapour - so insist on demonstrated, audited time-savings or safety gains in your own setting, not a vendor's slide.

NLW’s Takes

NLW sticks to his guns on overreacting to personnel moves

Citing Prime Intellect's Florian, NLW notes the recurring cycle: 'peak Google is done for' is always followed by Google releasing a new model and everyone deciding no one can match its TPUs and data. For now it's for markets to debate which side is right.

If you don't like what the water's used for, even one gallon is too many

NLW's core diagnosis of the water debate: big numbers are politically potent because most people have no sense of how much water we actually consume. The astronomical-sounding figures grab attention regardless of context.

Labor unions may be the key actor for finding the middle

NLW argues unions sit in both camps — sympathetic to community concerns that AI disproportionately benefits the rich, yet seeing firsthand how valuable the build-out is as demand for skilled blue-collar work climbs. They're positioned to broker the practical middle ground.
