Why Only AI Training Can Save the Economy

A Long Read recorded before the Anthropic-government-Fable blowup, NLW argues that: the American economy now is the AI trade, the labs need ever-more token consumption, enterprises are slamming on cost caps — and the only thing that squares the circle is mass-scale AI training.

Source: https://aidailybrief.ai/e/2026-06-16

▶ Listen — today’s brief (3 min)

The One Idea

Only AI training can square what the labs need with what enterprises will pay.

The American economy is now the AI trade — AI investment drives the bulk of GDP growth, and that capital keeps flowing only as long as token consumption keeps rising fast. But enterprises have moved from assisted-AI budgets to agentic-AI reality and are slapping on spending caps. The only force that can give labs their never-ending token growth and give enterprises enough value to lift those caps is mass-scale, high-quality AI training — moving every knowledge worker from assisted to agentic AI. And right now that training market is an abysmal failure.

The Other Side

NLW's lead is that the American economy now is the AI trade, that the labs need ceaseless token-consumption growth, that enterprises are slamming on spending caps, and that only mass-scale AI training can square the two by lifting people from assisted to agentic AI. Writing the day before, Zitron reads the identical facts as terminal: token-based billing has triggered "a revolt in the AI industry's customer base," because what the tokens cost is far higher than the value they create. He treats the very "train them to use more" move NLW predicts as proof of desperation — labs manufacturing token burn to feed a circular economy with no honest demand beneath it. Where NLW sees training as the bridge to value, Zitron sees an unprofitable product whose training costs alone drown out every dollar of revenue.

Source: Ed Zitron, “AI's Brokenomics”, 15 June 2026 (from 15 June’s edition)

The Truth Beneath

Both men orbit one variable: whether enterprise spending caps reflect a fixable adoption gap or a permanent gap between AI's cost and its value. They accept the same facts — the AI trade now drives much of US growth, Uber, Walmart and Meta have capped or restructured token spend, and the labs will push enablement, training and agent "loops" to lift usage. The empirical question that resolves is whether, after training, capped enterprises raise their budgets and show output worth the spend; the observable is token budgets rising post-enablement with attributable revenue, versus caps tightening regardless. The residue is worldview: does training create value or merely engineer demand? NLW is vindicated if enablement lifts caps and value follows; Zitron if training is pure demand-manufacture and ROI stays unmeasurable. Both are polemicists with incentives — Zitron anti-hype and bubble-certain; NLW anti-doom and, tellingly, himself selling training — yet both independently centre the same caps and the same "make people burn more tokens" turn. Watch next quarter's enterprise token budgets.

GP’s Corner

Claude’s notes on what today means for general practice and the NHS — commentary, not from the podcast itself.

The 'known-ROI bias' is already how the NHS adopts technology

NLW's sharpest point is that spending caps quietly push people toward safe, do-today's-work-a-bit-faster uses and away from bigger experiments. The NHS lives in that bias permanently — tight budgets, low risk appetite. For a GP that is not all bad: it means AI enters clinical work conservatively, through proven jobs like documentation, rather than speculative autonomy. The cost is that genuinely useful redesign — of admin, of triage — tends to be under-explored.

Managing agents, not prompting them, is the skill to build for practice admin

NLW frames 'managing agents' as a new primitive, closer to management than to IT training. If that is right, the practice skill to develop is supervision: deciding what an agent may do unsupervised (say, drafting recall letters) and what always needs a human signature. If Zitron is right that the value is not there, you have lost little by going slowly; if NLW is right, the early advantage is governance, not enthusiasm.

Watch your scribe's price, not its promises

The whole episode is about token costs colliding with budgets. The practical takeaway for a practice: when scribe contracts renew, the pressure described here — labs needing ever-more revenue and moving everyone to usage billing — may surface as price rises or tighter caps. Budget for it, and keep a manual fallback so a price shock never leaves the clinic without notes.

NLW’s Takes

Every AI business is now a token-efficiency business

2025 assisted-AI budgets are colliding with 2026 agentic-AI reality. Uber blew through its entire AI budget in four months and moved to a $1,500/month per-employee cap; Walmart did something similar. NLW's argument: for the foreseeable future every AI business is, in some form, a token-efficiency business.

IPOs will make the token-growth pressure brutal

Already, the relationship between lab revenue (token consumption) and available infrastructure capital is the defining one. Once Anthropic and OpenAI IPO, public-market pressure to show massive token-consumption growth every quarter will be relentless — NVIDIA already gets punished for merely beating estimates by too little.

Prediction: labs pour money into enablement training

Over the next 6–12 months, NLW expects dramatic increases in lab investment in enablement, training, and expanding usage depth. Even labs that don't believe everyone should be building agents will be forced to act as if it's true — because they can't hit quarterly token growth by giving leverage to only a select few.

Spending caps create a 'known ROI bias'

Caps don't just limit spend — they shape what gets attempted, pushing people toward basic productivity use cases and away from the big, unseemly experiments that create the next generation of value. NLW calls it the known-ROI bias: without permission, sandboxes and encouragement, people just do today's work a little faster.

Training is the only thing that solves both sides

The single thing that serves both the labs' need for token growth and enterprises' need for value, NLW argues, is AI training at mass scale and high quality — moving people from assisted to agentic AI and helping them uncover the use cases that make input costs seem negligible.

Managing agents is a new knowledge-work primitive

We're shifting from a paradigm where we do things to one where we oversee synthetic intelligences that do them for us. Prompting was a new skill but not a new primitive; managing agents is — and it's far closer to management training than to software training.
