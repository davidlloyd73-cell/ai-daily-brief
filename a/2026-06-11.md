## Why Fable 5 Is the Most Controversial AI Release Ever

_Anthropic walked back a Fable 5 policy in under 24 hours — but the silent nerfing, the data retention, and Dario's manifesto opened a much bigger wound: a lot of people just woke up to how much power one AI lab might have over who gets to build the future._

## The One Idea

### Fable 5 wasn't a model controversy — it was a power controversy.

Anthropic shipped Fable 5 with silent model degradation for AI research, an aggressive data retention policy, and over-tuned safety classifiers — and walked back the worst of it in under 24 hours. But the specific decisions matter less than what they revealed: a leading AI lab asserting the right to gatekeep who can build the tools of the new economy, and to do it invisibly. For the first time, a broad swath of people are grappling with just how much power the frontier labs may hold over society — and they don't like it.

## The Other Side

NLW's throughline is that Anthropic now holds an inherent power — potentially more control over people than any company in history — over who gets to build the tools of the new economy. Writing nine days earlier, Zitron's retort is that there is no measurable economy beneath it to control: you cannot measure the cost of an AI task or its return, and were the ROI real it would be obvious and uncontested. He points to enterprises already pulling back — Walmart capping its internal coding tool, an Amazon executive telling staff not to "use AI just for the sake of using AI." On his telling, Anthropic's "power" is the fragile leverage of an unprofitable vendor over confused buyers, not durable control over a thriving new economy.

Source: Ed Zitron, "AI Doesn't Have ROI", 2 June 2026 — https://www.wheresyoured.at/ai-doesnt-have-roi/

## The Truth Beneath

Beneath the heat both men orbit one variable: whether the labs' leverage is real structural power or the brittle pricing power of an unprofitable supplier. They accept the same facts — Anthropic can silently degrade or gate a model and dictate retention terms, yet it reversed a policy within a day under pressure, and enterprises are imposing caps. The empirical question that resolves is whether Anthropic keeps its customers and pricing once it must charge true costs around an IPO; the observable is switching behaviour — Microsoft trimming its Anthropic use, firms swapping in cheaper or Chinese models — versus genuine lock-in after price rises. The residue is worldview: is gatekeeping the future sovereign-level power or salesmanship? NLW is vindicated if buyers stay and the lab becomes a chokepoint; Zitron if they flee to substitutes once subsidies end. Both are polemicists — NLW anti-doom on the labs' significance, Zitron anti-hype and bubble-certain — and both now treat willingness to pay true cost as the decisive test. Watch switching after the next price change.

## GP's Corner

_Claude's notes on what today means for UK general practice and the NHS - commentary, not from the podcast itself._

### Silent model changes break clinical assurance

The detail that lit the fire was 'silent nerfing' — the model you validated may quietly become a different model. For an AI scribe that is a governance problem, not a curiosity: if the underlying model changes without notice, your local testing no longer guarantees how it behaves. Ask vendors for version pinning and change notification. As the episode put it, benchmarks assume the model you tested is the model you get — and in clinical use that assumption has to hold.

### Data retention is the line that matters most for the NHS

Anthropic's thirty-day data-retention demand is the thread to pull. Whatever you make of the politics, any tool retaining identifiable consultation content raises an information-governance question for the practice, not just the supplier. Confirm where UK patient data is processed and stored, the retention window, and that a DPIA is in place. If Zitron is right that the labs are financially squeezed, expect pressure to retain or monetise data; if NLW is right that they hold unusual power, the concentration itself is the risk. The same precaution answers both.

### Concentration is the real story — keep your options open

The 'power' debate is really about concentration in one provider. The practical response is ordinary procurement discipline: prefer tooling that is portable across models, with clear exit rights, so that one provider's abrupt policy swing cannot strand your clinic.

## NLW's Takes

### Texas could set the template for sane data center rules

Greg Abbott directed utilities to require new data centers to fully fund their own infrastructure so costs aren't passed to ratepayers, plus mandatory closed-loop cooling and water/electricity reporting. NLW: if you want to avoid moratorium-driven AI inequality while respecting communities, a popular destination like Texas leading on standards is "kind of optimistic."

### Fable 5 is the most controversial model launch ever

NLW argues the backlash to Anthropic's Fable 5 makes the GPT-5 / 4.0 deprecation drama look like nothing — and it took less than 24 hours of intense response for Anthropic to walk back a core policy.

### The real story is the power, not the policy

NLW's throughline: set aside the specific decisions and the clumsy communications — there's an inherent power in Anthropic's position that people are finally grappling with, one that could give a private corporation more control over people than any company has ever had.

### NLW's advice: fix the enterprise data retention policy fast

With the LLM research policy resolved, NLW says Anthropic should move quickly on enterprise data retention — because the corporate users who made Anthropic a juggernaut may not care about the research question, but won't stick around if their data is subject to Anthropic's whims.
