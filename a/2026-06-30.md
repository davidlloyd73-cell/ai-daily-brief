## How Big Is the AI Economy?

_OpenAI-style bubble talk is on a low ebb, so it's a good time to ask dispassionately: how big is the AI economy right now? Exponential View's new report says $175B annualized and growing 3x faster than any IT wave before it — while the headlines fill with identity checks, agent-neutrality bills, and a memory shortage nasty enough to earn the name Ramageddon._

## The One Idea

### The AI economy is real, big, and fast — more revenue-validated than any prior platform shift.

Exponential View audited over 1,000 AI companies and found demand backed by realized revenue, not just future promise: $110B banked over 12 months, a $175B run rate, and growth 3x faster than any IT wave before it. CapEx is enormous — $848B this year, $2T cumulative — but quarterly revenue now exceeds CapEx depreciation, older GPUs earn yields past their six-year life, and high-AI-spend companies have grown revenue 92% faster than non-adopters. The bubble question isn't settled, but the indicators are far more positive than the discourse suggests.

## The Other Side

This edition's lead rests on Exponential View's audit: $110bn banked over twelve months, a $175bn annualised run rate, quarterly revenue now exceeding CapEx depreciation — offered as proof that AI demand is real and realised, not merely promised. Zitron's piece attacks precisely that framing. The revenue, he argues, is largely circular: hyperscalers pour capital into their own unprofitable labs, OpenAI and Anthropic, which spend it straight back on compute, and only a fraction returns as "revenue" that is really venture subsidy recycled. He notes the hyperscalers won't disclose real AI revenues, hiding behind "vague run rates," and that their capex will outpace cashflow from Q3 2026. Citing the Bank for International Settlements, he warns the build-out serves demand that "does not exist outside of Anthropic and OpenAI" and rests on the hope that today's models will "turn into something completely different."

Source: Ed Zitron, "The AI Industry Is Losing", 30 June 2026 — https://www.wheresyoured.at/the-ai-industry-is-losing/

## The Truth Beneath

Both men orbit one variable: whether the vast AI spend is converting into real, self-sustaining demand and returns, or only the promise of it. Both accept the capex is enormous — roughly $848bn this year, $2tn cumulative — that headline revenue is climbing fast, and that the money flows mainly through a handful of labs. The empirical question is whether that revenue is durable, unsubsidised paying demand; the observables are hyperscaler capex against free cash flow, lab losses against revenue, and whether "run rate" figures survive as audited profit. The residue is worldview: Exponential View reads rising realised revenue and GPUs earning past year six as validation, Zitron reads the same numbers as recycled subsidy propping a bubble. Whittemore is vindicated if the run rate becomes self-funding profit; Zitron if capex outruns cashflow and surfaces as writedowns. Both are polemicists with incentives — NLW bullish and anti-doom, Zitron bubble-certain with a paid newsletter — and the signal is where they agree despite opposite priors. Watch whether Q3 2026 results show hyperscaler capex crossing above free cash flow.

## GP's Corner

_Claude's notes on what today means for UK general practice and the NHS - commentary, not from the podcast itself._

### Whether it's a bubble or not, budget for AI tools that outlast the hype cycle

The edition's central claim is that AI revenue is real and compounding; Zitron's is that much of it is subsidised spend propping up a handful of loss-making labs. For a GP surgery the practical reading is the same from either direction. If NLW is right and the economy keeps growing, prices for the scribes and admin tools we already lean on should keep falling as token costs collapse — the edition notes a blended price drop from roughly seventeen dollars to two per million tokens. If Zitron is right and the money is being withdrawn, the vendors selling cheap subsidised AI scribes today may raise prices sharply or fold, exactly as he warns happened when Copilot moved users onto token-based billing. Either way, a surgery should avoid deep dependence on a single cut-price vendor and keep a manual fallback for note-taking.

### Token-based billing is coming to clinical tools — watch the running cost

A recurring thread across the edition is the shift from flat subscriptions to per-token billing: Amazon lost its wholesale deal with Anthropic, and consumer tools are being repriced by usage. Agentic tasks, the edition notes, can burn around 1,200 times the tokens of a simple chat. For general practice this matters because an ambient scribe that transcribes and summarises a full consultation is far heavier than a one-line query. When a practice or PCN procures these tools, the question to ask is not just the monthly headline price but the cost per consultation at realistic volume, and whether that price is contractually protected against the memory-and-compute shortages the edition describes.

### The hardware shortage ('Ramageddon') could quietly raise the cost of NHS IT

Less about AI clinically and more about the practice's wider kit: the edition reports memory prices up sharply, with Apple, Microsoft and Lenovo all raising prices and Micron quadrupling memory costs in a year. That feeds through to the ordinary computers, servers and devices a surgery buys. It is worth factoring a real-terms price rise into any hardware replacement plan over the next year or two, rather than assuming the usual gentle decline in the cost of PCs and storage.
