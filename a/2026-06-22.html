Why AI Users Are Raving About GLM 5.2

GLM 5.2 just did what no Chinese open-weight model has done before — it survived first contact with real usage. As power users rave and a Nobel laureate flees DeepMind, NLW separates fact from innuendo on the Fable saga and asks whether the two-horse race is truly over.

The One Idea

GLM 5.2's DeepSeek moment shatters the two-horse race — for real this time.

Chinese open-weight models always benchmark well and then fade fast. GLM 5.2 is different: respected practitioners are raving days later, it beat Fable 5 on website design, and it's forcing companies rethinking their stack amid the Fable ban and compute crunch to take open models seriously. The lesson isn't to race out and buy GPUs — it's that the flowering of diverse model architectures means every organization should have a sandbox to experiment with the alternatives.

The Other Side

Zitron's answer to the GLM 5.2 excitement is that getting thrilled about another model topping a leaderboard is precisely the trap. His piece argues the opposite of a flowering: AI is, in his words, "slowing down just as it needs to speed up," and no amount of "rigged benchmarks and anecdotal examples" changes the fact that you still cannot measure the cost of a task or its return - open-weight or closed. The real story, on his telling, is enterprises capping token spend because they cannot link bills to value: Uber blew its annual budget in a quarter, Brex limits engineers to a few hundred dollars a week. And underneath sits an infrastructure build demanding trillions in annual revenue by 2030 he sees no path to, with barely any paying demand outside two loss-making labs. Whether GLM is good is, to him, beside the point.

Source: Ed Zitron, “AI Is Slowing Down”, 8 June 2026 (from 8 June’s edition)

The Truth Beneath

Both men orbit one variable: whether a fresh model release like GLM 5.2 is a signal that matters, or a distraction. Both accept the same facts - the benchmarks and practitioner raves are real, and enterprises are capping AI spend because cost-per-task and ROI are hard to measure. The empirical question that will resolve it is whether cheaper, diverse, open models convert into measurable, sustained value and a widening base of paying demand; the observable is enterprise AI spend, renewals, and whether real revenue emerges outside OpenAI and Anthropic. The residue is pure worldview: Whittemore reads proliferation as healthy maturation, Zitron as rearranging deckchairs on an unfinanceable build-out. Whittemore is vindicated if diverse models drive durable productivity and paying demand broadens; Zitron if spend stays capped and demand stays concentrated in two loss-makers, whatever the model. Both are polemicists with incentives - Whittemore bullish and anti-doom, Zitron bubble-certain and anti-hype with a newsletter - so the signal is their shared premise that ROI is currently unmeasurable. Watch whether profitable AI demand appears outside the two big labs.

GP’s Corner

Claude’s notes on what today means for general practice and the NHS — commentary, not from the podcast itself.

Keep a sandbox, but judge a scribe on the consultation, not the leaderboard

NLW's practical advice - that every organisation should have a corner free to experiment with alternative models - translates cleanly to a surgery curious about AI scribes and triage tools. The lesson is not to chase whichever model topped a benchmark this week. If NLW is right that capable, cheaper, open models are now real, a practice gains optionality and resilience against any one supplier. If Zitron is right that the gains are marginal and the economics shaky, the same caution holds: trial a tool on real consultations, measure whether it genuinely saves clinician time without adding a correction burden, and never rebuild your workflow around a model that may be withdrawn or repriced.

Open weights raise a real data-governance question for the NHS

The edition's sub-theme - that credible open-weight models let you run AI on your own terms - matters more in healthcare than in most sectors. An open model that can be hosted within NHS infrastructure could, in principle, keep patient data inside the organisation rather than sending it to a US vendor's servers. That is a genuine information-governance and DPIA advantage worth raising with any supplier. The caveat from Zitron's side: running these models properly is expensive (the edition cites roughly four hundred thousand pounds-worth of hardware or twenty thousand a month to rent), so for a single practice this is a question for ICB or national procurement, not the front desk.

Watch the renewal price, not the demo

Both men agree the real action is in unit economics, not which model is cleverest. For a practice that means the figure that matters is the renewal cost and whether your transcripts and templates leave with you if you switch supplier. Ask which model sits behind any scribe, whether the price can change at renewal, and what happens to your data and accumulated corrections if you move.

NLW’s Takes

Don't over-read any single career move

NLW cautions that behind-the-scenes reporting needs a grain of salt and that we tend to make too much of individual departures — humans have complex motivations. But two high-profile DeepMind exits in a week does start a pattern, and Google's slide in the coding and enterprise race is genuinely notable.

Being a few months behind the frontier now covers far more use cases

As the overall state-of-the-art rises, being three or six months behind has a lot more viable use cases than it did a year ago. Chinese models have steadily been integrated into the stack, especially for startups and smaller companies with fewer constraints on which models they can use.

Don't buy 8 H200s to try GLM 5.2 — use a router

Some are acting like the only way to run GLM 5.2 is locally — Itamar Golan estimates ~$400K to buy or ~$20K/month to rent the eight H200s needed. NLW's recommendation: rather than hacking complex physical infrastructure, just try it via a routing tool like OpenRouter or an open-source harness.

The two-horse race has been broken

NLW's bottom line: even if Fable returns this week, the idea that AI is a two-horse race between OpenAI and Anthropic (with an asterisk for Google) is over. Intensifying, costlier workloads plus government review of the most powerful models plus viable runner-up models point to a flowering of diverse architectures companies can optimize for speed, cost or performance.

Give part of your org a sandbox for alternative models

NLW doesn't think most companies need to race off their core subscriptions. But having some part of the organization with license to experiment with alternative model architectures is probably time and money well spent right now.
