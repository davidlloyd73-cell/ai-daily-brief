A Big Shift in the AI Race

Anthropic's Mythos/Fable shutdown drags into its fifth day with no resolution in sight — while Elon Musk uses SpaceX's blockbuster IPO to swallow Cursor for $60B and reshape the model race. The One Idea

The AI race just got dynamic again — and SpaceX is the one rewriting it.

Just as everyone settled into the agentic phase, the board reshuffled. Anthropic is stuck in a regulatory standoff, OpenAI's leaked financials reveal a profitable inference business hiding under accounting noise, and Elon Musk parlayed a 49%-pop SpaceX IPO into a $60B Cursor acquisition. The connective tissue: AI infrastructure, frontier models, and access are increasingly being placed under national security control — and the race is far more fluid than the 'settled' sentiment suggested.

The Other Side

This is the very leak NLW builds half his episode on — Zitron is the one who obtained and published OpenAI's audited 2025 figures, verified by the Financial Times. He reads them as terminal where NLW reads them as misunderstood. By his account OpenAI took $13.07 billion in revenue against $34 billion in total costs and a $20.92 billion operating loss, with research and development alone at $19.18 billion. He stresses that operating losses are 'mounting year-over-year at a dramatic rate' and says plainly he is 'not sure how this company finds a way toward any kind of sustainability or profitability.' Where NLW lifts out the inference line — $13 billion of revenue on $7.5 billion cost of revenue — as the overlooked good-news story, Zitron treats it as a rounding detail beside a loss from operations that dwarfs every dollar of revenue.

Source: Ed Zitron, “Exclusive: OpenAI Losses Increased Nearly 8X in 2025, With Spending Hitting $34 Billion”, 15 June 2026 (from 15 June’s edition)

The Truth Beneath

Both men orbit one variable: whether OpenAI's headline loss is a fundamentally unprofitable business or accounting noise sitting on top of a viable core. They accept the same audited facts — $13.07bn revenue, $7.5bn cost of revenue, $34bn total costs, a $20.92bn operating loss, and a one-time $41.55bn fair-value charge from the for-profit conversion that inflates the $38.5bn headline. The empirical question that resolves it is whether, once that one-off charge is stripped out, the operating loss narrows as inference gross profit scales faster than R&D and sales grow; the observable is OpenAI's next audited statement or IPO prospectus showing the ex-one-off operating-margin trajectory. The residue is pure worldview: is the gap between cost and value structural, or a phase of investment ahead of a real engine? NLW is vindicated if the inference profit scales and operating losses shrink ex the conversion charge; Zitron if losses keep mounting with no margin path. Both are polemicists with incentives — NLW anti-doom and reaching for the bullish read, Zitron bubble-certain and the man who chose to publish the leak — yet both accept the same numbers and even agree the $13bn-on-$7.5bn inference line is real; they differ only on whether it matters. Watch the next audited disclosure and whether the operating loss, stripped of the conversion charge, actually falls.

GP’s Corner

Claude’s notes on what today means for general practice and the NHS — commentary, not from the podcast itself.

The model under your scribe could be switched off by a government, not a vendor

The hardest fact in today's edition is not financial — it is that a frontier model was pulled worldwide for foreign nationals by government order, the first kill switch of its kind. Most AI scribes sit on top of one of these big models. For a UK practice that is a continuity question worth asking your supplier plainly: which underlying model do you depend on, and what is your fallback if it is suddenly restricted? Keep a manual note-taking habit alive so a clinic is never left without documentation if access changes overnight.

If Zitron is right, expect price rises; if NLW is right, expect more model churn

If Zitron's reading holds and OpenAI's losses really are structural, the bill eventually lands on customers — so a scribe contract renewal may bring higher prices or tighter usage caps; budget defensively. If NLW's reading holds and the inference business is genuinely profitable, the bigger disruption is competitive churn — cheaper rivals like the Cursor-style entrants undercutting incumbents — which means the scribe that is best value today may not be in a year. Either way, avoid long lock-ins and keep your data portable.

'Profitable on inference' is the number to watch, and it has a clinical analogue

NLW's overlooked point — that selling tokens may already be profitable even while the company loses money overall — is the right lens for judging any AI tool's durability: separate the running cost of the service you actually use from the vendor's investment burn. A scribe vendor can be sustainable on the per-consultation cost even if its parent is haemorrhaging cash on training. When assessing tools for the practice, ask for the unit economics of the service you consume, not the headline valuation.

NLW’s Takes

Managing Washington is now Anthropic's job — and they may not realize it

NLW's read: you can be mad at who's in power and at the government's lack of technical capacity, but a company seen as integral to the economy and national security doesn't get to fail at dealing with the government in power. The charitable interpretation is that Anthropic simply hasn't recognized that managing this relationship is now as important as anything they build in the lab.

"What the hell were you doing for the other hour and 15 minutes?"

NLW points to a pattern of slow Anthropic responses — days to deliver the Glasswing recipient list, and even the friendly counter-narrative that Dario took 'only' an hour and 15 minutes to call back on Friday night. When the government calls about something this mission-critical, the move is to pause everything and get on the phone.

We're in a very liminal moment for AI

NLW frames a broad realignment: companies grappling with what full agentic workloads will cost and racing to become token-efficient, a US economy whose GDP growth leans heavily on AI infrastructure build-out, and the political dimension all converging at once. The economics of mature agentic AI are starting to become clear.

The 'settled' agentic phase is anything but

NLW's wrap: just as it felt like the agentic phase had settled in, the race is clearly fast-moving. Things to watch — resolution of Anthropic's Fable issue, whether OpenAI avoids its own version of it, what SpaceX's public price does, what Cursor's next model reveals about strategy, and a conspicuously quiet Google that won't stay quiet for long.
