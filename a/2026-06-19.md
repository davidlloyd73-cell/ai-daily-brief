## Your Company Doesn’t Need an AI Strategy

_Fable's still offline, but the thaw between the White House and Anthropic may finally be starting — and in the vacuum it left, the enterprise conversation has shifted from "which model?" to something much bigger. Satya Nadella's viral token-capital essay crystallizes it: your company doesn't need an AI strategy, it needs a learning loop you actually own._

Source: https://aidailybrief.ai/e/2026-06-19

Audio: https://davids-ai-brief.netlify.app/audio/2026-06-19.mp3

## The One Idea

### You don't need an AI strategy. You need an AI learning system you own.

The Fable ban exposed how fragile single-vendor AI foundations really are, and it landed right as the cost of state-of-the-art agentic AI was already forcing a rethink. Satya Nadella's answer — read 65 million times — is that the real asset isn't the model you pick but the compounding loop you build on top of it: human capital times scaffolding times feedback. Companies that turn workflows, judgment, and corrections into a portable, model-agnostic learning system own IP that survives any single model. Everyone else is just renting intelligence and ceding their value to a few models that eat everything they see.

## The Other Side

Zitron's answer to the build-a-learning-loop gospel is blunt: you cannot build durable value on something whose return you cannot measure. He argues nobody can calculate the ROI of AI — or even a standard cost per task — because hallucination-prone models and an ever-growing list of harnesses make every interaction unpredictable. The fashionable talk of owning "token capital", on his telling, rests on subsidised and deliberately obfuscated token prices; the moment Anthropic and OpenAI moved enterprises to usage-based billing in early 2026, customers revolted and began capping spend. Far from compounding into owned IP, he insists, AI keeps getting more expensive, not cheaper, and much of the enthusiasm is written by people "conned into ignoring the true cost."

Source: Ed Zitron, "AI Doesn't Have ROI", 2 June 2026 — https://www.wheresyoured.at/ai-doesnt-have-roi/

## The Truth Beneath

Both men orbit one variable: whether a company can wring measurable, durable value from AI that exceeds its true, unsubsidised cost. Both accept the same facts — token prices were subsidised and obfuscated, and enterprises recoiled once moved to usage-based billing. The empirical question that resolves it is whether the private evals, captured workflows and feedback loops Nadella prescribes actually raise measured output by more than the real per-token bill; the observable is audited enterprise AI ROI, net renewal and spend over coming quarters, and whether such "token capital" survives a model swap. The residue is pure worldview: Whittemore sees a compounding asset a firm can own, Zitron sees unmeasurable, subsidised theatre. Whittemore is vindicated if firms show durable, portable gains net of rising prices; Zitron if spend keeps being capped with no measurable return. Both are polemicists with incentives — Whittemore bullish and anti-doom, Zitron bubble-certain and anti-hype, each with a newsletter to sell — so the signal is their shared premise that unit economics, not model choice, now decides. Watch whether any large firm publishes audited AI ROI net of true token costs.

## GP's Corner

_Claude's notes on what today means for UK general practice and the NHS - commentary, not from the podcast itself._

### Own the loop, not just the scribe

Nadella's idea translates neatly to a surgery: the durable asset is not the AI scribe itself but the accumulated templates, prompts and corrected notes your clinicians build around it. If NLW is right, a practice that captures and keeps that work owns something portable that survives a change of supplier. If Zitron is right, today's tidy price is subsidised and unproven, so the practical move either way is the same — make sure your templates and corrected transcripts can be exported, and never let a vendor lock that learning inside its own system.

### Measuring ROI is harder in a consultation than in code

Zitron's sharpest point — that nobody can measure AI's return or even a standard cost per task — bites harder in general practice, where the output is a safe consultation, not lines of code. If NLW is right, the gain is clinician time saved and better documentation; if Zitron is right, the honest answer is that no one yet has a clean cost-per-consultation or proven ROI. Either way, judge a scribe on whether it demonstrably saves time without adding a correction burden, and ask what it will cost once any free or subsidised tier ends.

### Watch the renewal, not the demo

Both men agree the action has moved to unit economics rather than which model is cleverest. For a practice that means the figure that matters is the renewal price and the per-seat or per-token cost, not the impressive launch demo. Ask your supplier which model sits behind the product, whether the price can change at renewal, and whether your data and templates leave with you if you switch.

## NLW's Takes

### NLW: read literally, this is nationalization of the AI industry

NLW argues you don't need to debate the merits — 50% of any company over $200M in revenue, in voting shares, amounts to de facto control of the whole sector. The more important read is how it sets the tent poles of where AI policy discourse is heading.

### Fable's outage made companies question their AI foundations

A week after Fable 5 went offline, NLW says many organizations are looking at their AI strategies and realizing how much they rely on one or a small handful of partners. The lesson: picking the right vendor is a very small part of real organizational change.

### NLW: the post is also Microsoft narrative-building

NLW reads Nadella's essay as a declaration of independence from Anthropic and OpenAI's growing power — and as positioning for Microsoft's own ecosystem play. The deeply enmeshed enterprise incumbent has a direct self-interest in the ecosystem approach over one-model-to-rule-them-all.

### NLW: design AI systems, not AI implementations

As token costs rise, NLW warns of the temptation to respond with strict spend limits and a bias toward known ROI. The most sophisticated organizations he talks to are instead shifting to designing AI systems — and if any good comes from the Fable ban, it's putting a fine point on that systems thinking.
