1. King's Speech delivers AI promise, not AI Bill

King Charles opened the new session of Parliament this morning with a speech the government had briefed heavily over the weekend. The AI line is the one Labour first used in 2024 — that they will "seek to establish the appropriate legislation to place requirements on those working to develop the most powerful artificial intelligence models" — followed by a consultation, not a Bill. The AI Growth Lab sandbox is confirmed, the Cyber Security and Resilience Bill is carried over and continues its passage, and a Digital Information and Smart Data Bill is in the briefing pack. No standalone AI Bill. No statutory regulator for clinical AI.

The view from the surgery: This is what NHS clinical-AI procurement looks like for another year — governed by MHRA discretion, the DUAA, the GMC's evolving guidance, and whatever each integrated care board decides counts as assurance. The contrast with the EU AI Act's 2 August enforcement date is sharper this morning than last. If you are a GP being asked to consent to a scribe pilot, the chain of accountability did not get clearer today.

2. UCLA puts ambient scribes through the first proper randomised trial

A UCLA Health randomised trial published this week put two market-leading ambient scribes — Microsoft DAX and Nabla — through their paces across 238 physicians, 14 specialties and roughly 72,000 patient encounters. The Nabla arm delivered a ~10% reduction in documentation time versus usual care; the DAX arm did not reach statistical significance on the primary endpoint. Both showed modest improvements in self-rated burnout. This is the first rigorous RCT of ambient scribes in real-world deployment — pitched against a market that has been quoting 86% time savings for two years.

Why it matters: The hype-to-evidence gap on scribes has been embarrassing, and a clean RCT was overdue. Ten percent is real and worth having — about three minutes off a thirty-minute clinic of admin — but it is not the headline any of the vendor decks have been waving. The honest reading is that scribes save real time on letters and discharge summaries, less on the in-consult narrative most of us already touch-type. Useful figures to keep handy next time a salesperson offers you an hour back.

3. NEJM: the digital divide is coming for clinical AI

David Blumenthal, Kevin Bennett and John McDonough published "Overcoming the Digital Divide in Health Care AI" in NEJM on 9 May. Their argument is straightforward: the populations most likely to benefit from clinical AI — older adults, patients with limited English, those with lower digital literacy — are precisely those least likely to be enrolled in the training data, least likely to use AI front doors, and most likely to be served by systems unable to deploy the tools in the first place. The piece calls for AI safety, equity and digital-infrastructure assessments to be coupled rather than separated, and is short enough to read in a coffee.

The view: Worth a read before the next discussion of practice digital-front-door upgrades. For Harrow demographics specifically — multigenerational households, multiple first languages, a long tail of patients who still want to come in — the equity question is not abstract. The patients who would gain most from an AI triage front-end are the ones it is most likely to fail. Worth raising at the next PCN meeting.

4. Chatbots, psychosis, and the gap between model generations

A joint CUNY–King's College London team published a clinical safety evaluation this week comparing five frontier chatbots against a simulated user designed to test responses to delusional and suicidal content. GPT-4o, Grok 4.1 and Gemini 3 Pro were classified "high-risk, low-safety" — GPT-4o particularly so, with a strong tendency to affirm delusional content. GPT-5.2 and Claude Opus 4.5 performed substantially better, more often declining to validate and more often signposting professional help.

The view: Two takeaways. First, model generation matters: the gap between GPT-4o and GPT-5.2 is not cosmetic, it is clinically meaningful — and the older models remain on free tiers used by exactly the patients you would worry about. Second, patient choice of chatbot is now a relevant clinical variable, particularly for younger patients with emerging psychosis or affective instability. Next time a younger patient mentions they have been "talking to" a particular bot, it is worth asking which one. The answer will increasingly shape the trajectory.

5. Anthropic rents the Colossus

In a deal confirmed on 6 May, Anthropic has signed to take effectively the entire compute capacity of SpaceX's Colossus 1 data centre in Memphis — over 300 megawatts and 220,000 Nvidia GPUs. The arrangement is striking on two counts: Colossus was built to power xAI's Grok, now merged into SpaceXAI; and Musk simultaneously confirmed Anthropic has "expressed interest" in orbital compute capacity. Anthropic's stated reason is more prosaic — fixing the rate-limit complaints from Claude Pro and Max users.

Why it matters: Strip away the orbital flourish and what remains is the most striking commercial fact of the spring: Musk is now selling industrial-scale compute to a competitor while running the competing model. The hyperscaler market is consolidating in odd directions, and the geographical concentration of frontier compute around Memphis and the Texas grid is becoming a national-policy story. For NHS digital procurement, the sovereignty question — which jurisdictions does my clinical model train in? — is no longer hypothetical.

From the Eye

Three the wires skipped this week. Microsoft, Google and xAI confirmed on 5 May that they will join OpenAI and Anthropic in granting the US Commerce Department's Center for AI Standards and Innovation pre-release access to frontier models for security evaluation — quietly turning what was a voluntary OpenAI gesture into an industry baseline, and one the UK AI Safety Institute now needs to mirror or risk losing access to. Microsoft's State of AI Diffusion report (7 May) puts global AI adoption at 17.8% of the working-age population in Q1 2026, up 1.5 percentage points in three months — the steepest quarterly jump since the index began; if the figure holds, the productivity puzzle of the next decade arrives before the labour-market data catches it. And King's College Hospital NHS Foundation Trust rolled out Abbott's Ultreon 3.0 AI imaging system for coronary angioplasty on 11 May, expected to support around 500 patients a year — a small deployment but a precise one, the kind of clinical-AI integration that earns no headline yet steadily shifts care quality. Worth quoting next time the conversation drifts to AGI.
