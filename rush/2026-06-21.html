THE MORNING RUSH

A short brief on AI in medicine — and the world it's moving through. Read it before the 8am phone lines open.

Sunday 21 June 2026 · ~5 min read · Issue 053

On the line today: An OpenAI reasoning model has cracked 18 rare-disease cases that had defeated the specialists — a genuinely useful result, with a sober denominator attached. The MHRA and the FDA have quietly tied themselves together with reciprocal "liaison officers," AI top of the agenda. Closer to home, an indemnity provider reports patients firing off AI-drafted legal letters at GP practices — seven pages of arguments that "maybe don't exist." Brussels has picked its champion to build a sovereign European frontier model in all 24 languages, and fresh UK figures show the graduate job ladder being sawn off near the bottom. Plus From the Eye — deepfaked doctors, a trillion-dollar firm's guilt money, and a patient photo that never was.

1. The model that solved 18 cases the specialists couldn't

Researchers at Boston Children's Hospital, Harvard and OpenAI took 376 paediatric cases that had already been through genetic testing and expert review and remained unsolved, and ran them back through OpenAI's o3 "Deep Research" reasoning model. After expert review, further testing and clinical confirmation, physicians reached a diagnosis in 18 of them — an extra diagnostic yield of 4.8%, across neurodevelopmental disorders, rare neuromuscular disease, sudden unexpected death in paediatrics and early-onset psychosis. The work was published in NEJM AI on 18 June. The mechanism is mundane and powerful at once: new gene-disease links appear in the reference databases constantly, and the machine is tireless about re-checking old cases against new knowledge.

The view from the surgery: Note the honest framing — 18 out of 376, not "AI diagnoses rare disease." But for the families of those 18, after years of nothing, 4.8% is everything. This is the kind of AI that earns its place: not replacing the workup, but doing the one thing humans do badly, which is patiently re-reading the cold case the day the textbook changes.

2. The MHRA and FDA quietly move in together

At the DIA annual meeting in Philadelphia, MHRA chief executive Lawrence Tallon and FDA deputy commissioner Grace Graham announced a new "liaison programme" — reciprocal officers embedded in each agency to share expertise day-to-day, with medical devices, AI and innovative medicines named as the focal areas. Both sides stress they keep full regulatory independence and will make their own calls; the pitch is smoother passage across the Atlantic in both directions and less duplicated effort on emerging questions. It follows an April commitment to deepen device-regulation cooperation.

Why it matters: This is plumbing, but the right plumbing. AI tools don't respect borders, and a UK-approved scribe or triage model shouldn't need two entirely separate regulatory rituals to reach patients on both sides. The watch-point is drift: "alignment" can quietly mean importing whoever has the lighter touch. Worth seeing whose standard the joint thinking actually converges on.

3. Patients are now sending you AI-written legal letters

Medical Protection's deputy medical adviser Dr Sarah Townley told Pulse Live Birmingham that the indemnity provider is seeing a surge of patients using AI to generate complaint and legal-threat letters to GP practices — some running to 25 pages, packed with legal arguments "that maybe don't exist." What once took a determined complainant a weekend now takes seconds, and it's no longer confined to complaints: it's increasingly threats of litigation. The finding sits in a white paper on complaints and the regulatory process from Cogora.

The view from the surgery: The asymmetry is the problem. A patient can now generate a confident, citation-laden, wholly synthetic case in the time it takes the kettle to boil, and a real human has to read every page and take it seriously. Most of it will be hallucinated nonsense dressed as Latin — but you can't ignore it, because buried in page 19 might be a genuine point. Practices will need a calm triage routine for this, the same way we learned to handle the printout from "Dr Google." It is here now.

4. Europe picks its champion for a sovereign frontier model

The European Commission has named the EUROPA consortium — led by the Italian firm Domyn — as winner of its Frontier AI Grand Challenge, with a remit to build an open-source frontier model covering all 24 official EU languages. The brief called for more than 400 billion parameters, and the winner gets a year's access to EuroHPC supercomputing to train it. The stated aim is bluntly geopolitical: build advanced AI on European infrastructure, in European languages, rather than renting it from American and Chinese labs. The announcement landed on 19 June.

Why it matters: "Sovereign AI" is the phrase of the year, and language is a sharp way in — a model fluent in Maltese and Estonian is something the big US labs have little commercial reason to build. Whether a grant-funded consortium can actually keep pace with firms spending billions a quarter is the open question. But for the NHS, the principle travels: depending on a single foreign supplier for a clinical tool is a strategic choice, not a neutral one.

5. The UK job ladder is losing its bottom rungs

Fresh UK labour analysis makes uncomfortable reading: graduate roles are down sharply year on year, with a large share of employers explicitly hiring fewer graduates because of AI, and roughly one in six firms expecting AI to shrink their workforce over the next twelve months. The bite is heaviest at the entry level — the two-to-five-years-experience posts that used to be where people learned the trade. UK net AI-linked job losses are running ahead of comparable economies.

The view: I keep coming back to this because medicine isn't exempt. The whole NHS pitch for AI is "free the clinician for higher-value work" — but the economy-wide pattern is that the junior, learn-by-doing role goes first. Automate away the boring jobs the F2, the trainee and the new HCA cut their teeth on, and you bank a saving this year and lose the consultant you needed in fifteen.

From the Eye

Three the front pages didn't run. Item one — the elder-fraud beat nobody's covering properly: scammers are now using AI to clone the voices of Medicare-type officials and distressed relatives, and to forge doctors' signatures, lab reports and imaging to support bogus claims. Your older patients are being phished with synthetic clinical documents that look exactly like the real thing — worth a word with the ones who manage their own affairs. Item two — tucked inside Dario Amodei's much-quoted "exponential" essay was a quieter line: Anthropic is pledging $350m to soften AI's labour-market damage — a $200m Economic Futures Research Fund and a $150m fellowship. Read it as you like — conscience, or a near-trillion-dollar firm pre-buying the moral high ground before the layoffs it's accelerating arrive. Item three — the New England Journal of Medicine retracted a clinical case study after the authors admitted the patient image had been altered with AI. A doctored photograph of a real condition sailed through peer review at the most prestigious journal in the field. If the picture can't be trusted, that's a different order of problem from a dodgy citation.
