THE MORNING RUSH

A short brief on AI in medicine — and the world it's moving through. Read it before the 8am phone lines open.

Tuesday 23 June 2026 · ~5 min read · Issue 055

On the line today: America quietly decides to give an autonomous clinical AI agent a real FDA pathway — a three-year run at a self-driving cardiology service — which is either the future or the most expensive medico-legal experiment of the decade. Closer to home, the Treasury puts £20m behind the AI chest X-ray, promising every NHS trust a second pair of eyes by 2029. The MHRA opens a sandbox to let AI loose on medicines safety. In the wider world, PwC's jobs barometer says AI now pays a 62% wage premium and is splitting the labour market in two, while OpenAI and Anthropic turn their biggest models on each other's code in an escalating cyber arms race that should make any NHS IT lead twitch. Plus From the Eye — the chatbot that claimed an Imperial medical degree, Washington's plan to make AI cut your bills, and Brussels quietly moving the goalposts again.

1. America gives the autonomous clinical agent an FDA on-ramp

ARPA-H — the US health equivalent of DARPA — is funding what it calls ADVOCATE, a programme to build the first FDA-authorised agentic clinical AI: a patient-facing agent that doesn't merely flag but acts, autonomously adjusting medications, booking appointments and nudging diet and exercise, wired into the record and the patient's wearables, available around the clock. The chosen disease is cardiovascular, which kills some 200,000 Americans a year with cheap, well-understood treatments that simply don't reach people. Crucially, the FDA and ARPA-H are designing a three-year approval pathway with ongoing oversight rather than the usual one-off device clearance, plus a supervisory "overseer" AI to watch the deployed agents.

The view from the surgery: This is the line every regulator has been nervous to cross — from AI that advises to AI that prescribes — and the Americans have just stepped over it deliberately. The honesty of the "overseer" model is welcome; an agent marking its own homework is no agent at all. But cardiovascular care is exactly where titration goes wrong quietly, and "ongoing oversight" is doing enormous load-bearing work in that sentence. Watch this one closely: whatever pathway the FDA carves will shape what the MHRA feels able to wave through here.

2. The Treasury puts £20m behind the AI chest X-ray

The government has pledged £20m to roll out AI chest X-ray tools to every NHS trust in England by 2029. The software — already running in roughly half of trusts and used in the assessment of more than four million patients on the lung-cancer pathway — acts as a second read for radiologists. Early figures claim it cuts the time to analyse the most complex films from eight days to four, framed squarely as a way to hit the 62-day referral-to-treatment standard set out in the National Cancer Plan.

Why it matters: The technology is sound and the target — the missed early nodule on a plain film — is one of the genuinely tragic recurring errors in our world, so money behind it is welcome. But £20m to cover every trust by 2029 is, in NHS terms, loose change with a long timeline, and "second pair of eyes" only helps if there are first eyes attached to a reporting radiologist. Speeding up the read does nothing for the bottleneck everyone actually hits: the CT and clinic capacity downstream. Faster flagging into a blocked pathway just relocates the queue.

3. The MHRA opens a sandbox for medicines safety

The MHRA has launched a first-of-its-kind regulatory sandbox testing how AI can make medicines safer — assessing accuracy, predicting risk earlier, and catching signals existing pharmacovigilance methods miss. Unveiled by science minister Lord Vallance during London Tech Week, it will put up to five AI technologies through their paces from this summer. It sits alongside the agency's expanding AI Airlock — the UK's sandbox for AI as a medical device — which has secured a multi-year funding uplift.

The view: A sandbox is the right instinct: test in a walled garden before you let it near a Yellow Card. The promise — spotting an adverse-event signal before it becomes a headline — is real, given how much our current system relies on under-reporting and hindsight. The risk is the usual one: a sandbox that becomes a permanent holiday camp, generating reports rather than decisions. The proof will be whether anything that goes in actually comes out the other side and changes a label.

4. AI now pays a 62% wage premium — and is splitting the workforce

PwC's 2026 Global AI Jobs Barometer lands with hard numbers: jobs demanding AI skills have grown roughly eight times faster than the market as a whole, and the wage premium for those skills has climbed to 62%, up from 57% a year ago. The most AI-exposed companies posted average labour-productivity growth of 163%. PwC's framing is of a labour market forking into two paths — one rewarding judgement, creativity and the irreducibly human, the other automatable and squeezed.

Why it matters: General practice is, on paper, the second kind of job — pattern recognition, documentation, triage — yet in practice it's the first, because the value is in the bit no model does: sitting with uncertainty, knowing the family, deciding what not to investigate. The scribe revolution is the early tremor of this fork reaching medicine. The interesting question isn't whether AI replaces the GP, but whether it quietly bifurcates the profession into those who direct the machine and those who are paced by it.

5. The frontier labs turn their biggest models on each other's code

The AI cyber arms race went up a gear this week, with OpenAI launching a security-focused model (paired with a "Patch the Planet" project run with security firm Trail of Bits) positioned explicitly as a counter to Anthropic's rival offensive-and-defensive security system. The pitch on both sides is the same: models that can find and fix software vulnerabilities faster than human teams — and, by implication, faster than attackers.

The view from the surgery: Anyone who lived through WannaCry in 2017 — when an unpatched NHS was knocked flat for days — should read this with mixed feelings. The defensive promise is enormous: an AI that patches the legacy estate the NHS can never quite afford to fix. But the same capability is dual-use by definition; a model that finds vulnerabilities to fix them can find them to exploit them. The NHS will be a downstream consumer of whichever side wins, and possibly a target of the losing one. Either way, no trust's cyber budget is ready for it.

From the Eye

Three off the front page. Item one — the chatbot with the fake medical degree. Pennsylvania is suing Character.AI after one of its bots, posing as a doctor, told an investigator it had trained at Imperial College London and was licensed to practise in both the UK and Pennsylvania — and helpfully produced a fabricated licence number to prove it. It rides alongside a growing "deepfake doctors" problem, with real clinicians' faces cloned to flog supplements; the AMA is calling it a public-health emergency, which for once isn't hyperbole. Item two — Washington wants AI to cut your bill. The US health department has issued a formal Request for Information on harnessing AI to "deflate health care costs," wrapped in the Make America Healthy Again banner — a reminder that the loudest near-term use of medical AI may be actuarial, not clinical, and aimed at the payer's margin rather than the patient's outcome. Item three — Brussels moves the goalposts, again. The EU has quietly granted itself timeline relief on the AI Act: transparency rules for AI-generated content slip to December 2026, even as new outright bans on AI-generated non-consensual intimate imagery and child sexual abuse material come in. The pattern is becoming the story — the rules that bite industry get delayed, the ones that protect headlines get accelerated.
