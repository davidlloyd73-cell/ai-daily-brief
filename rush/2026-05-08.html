THE MORNING RUSH

A short brief on AI in medicine — and the world it's moving through. Read it before the 8am phone lines open.

Friday 8 May 2026 · ~5 min read · Issue 010

On the line today: The EU AI Act Omnibus deal closed yesterday — and medtech is the one sector that didn't get the simplification everyone else did. OpenAI takes the wraps off "OpenAI for Healthcare" with GPT-5.2 already inside Cedars-Sinai, MSK, UCSF and Stanford. Pulse tells GP practices they need a trained Clinical Safety Officer before plugging in an ambient scribe. Stanford and NEJM AI publish MedAgentBench — and Claude is top of the class on virtual EHR work. And OpenAI's Washington policy blueprint asks for the floor and the ceiling at the same time. Plus From the Eye — Beth Israel's working rule for ChatGPT use, the NHS GitHub deadline that lands Monday, and the misattribution machine.

1. The EU AI Act Omnibus deal lands — and medtech is left walking with two crutches

Council and Parliament reached provisional political agreement on the Digital AI Omnibus yesterday, 7 May, a week earlier than expected. High-risk Annex III obligations slip to 2 December 2027, AI in regulated products (Annex I) to 2 August 2028. For machinery and connected vehicles, that is a clean win — DIGITALEUROPE estimates €600,000 of duplicative Year-1 compliance lifted off the typical SME. Medical devices got nothing. The Commission's December proposal to fix the MDR/AI Act overlap was dropped to get the file across the line. MedTech Europe — 95% SMEs — calls it "a major win for Europe's manufacturers, but a missed opportunity for medtech innovators."

The view from the surgery: The EU is already not the first-launch market for new medical devices, and yesterday widened that gap. CE-marked devices coming out of Europe will still carry a heavier regulatory load than US-cleared kit. Expect Lyrebird-class scribes and ArteraAI-style prognostics to keep eyeing the FDA before Brussels — and MHRA's International Reliance Framework to start mattering more than its own conformity rules.

2. OpenAI for Healthcare goes live — with GPT-5.2 inside the academic majors

OpenAI announced ChatGPT for Healthcare yesterday alongside GPT-5.2 — a HIPAA-aligned enterprise tier already rolling out at AdventHealth, Baylor Scott & White, Boston Children's, Cedars-Sinai, HCA Healthcare, Memorial Sloan Kettering, Stanford Medicine Children's Health and UCSF. OpenAI is publishing GPT-5.2 scores against its own HealthBench rubric (5,000 multi-turn cases, 262 physicians) and claims the model now beats human baselines on every role tested.

Why it matters: The launch partners read like the US clinical AI A-list, and the framing has shifted decisively — this is OpenAI selling like Epic. The UK question is whether HCA Healthcare's roll-in pulls HCA UK (Harley Street, the Princess Grace, the Wellington) into the same deployment, and what that does to data flows for shared-care NHS patients. "HIPAA-aligned" is not DPA 2018 / DTAC compliant — and the gap will need MHRA and CQC eyes before any UK private wing turns it on.

3. Pulse says: no AI scribe without a trained Clinical Safety Officer

Pulse this week reported that GP practices are now being told — NHS England guidance, reinforced by the BMA — to appoint a Clinical Safety Officer with at least two days' formal training before deploying any ambient voice product. The CSO must be a clinician who understands clinical risk and DCB0129/0160. New procurement frameworks are landing alongside, intended to make the AVT registry less of a free-for-all.

The view: Two days' training and a named accountable clinician is, by NHS standards, light-touch. By GP-practice standards — already running QOF, CQC, IG, fire and DSP toolkit single-handed — it is another straw. Federations and PCNs will share CSOs across practices, and the medico-legal trail when a scribe-induced error reaches the GMC will run through whichever name is on the form. Worth knowing whose name that is before someone else writes it in for you.

4. NEJM AI publishes MedAgentBench — and Claude tops the EHR class

Stanford HAI and Stanford Medicine published MedAgentBench in NEJM AI — a virtual FHIR-compliant EHR loaded with 100 patient profiles and 300 physician-authored tasks, testing whether an LLM agent can actually do the EHR work clinicians do (place orders, document vitals, retrieve labs). Claude 3.5 Sonnet v2 topped the board at 69.67%, GPT-4o at 64.0%, DeepSeek-V3 at 62.67%. The headline number is that even the best model gets a third of routine EHR tasks wrong.

Why it matters: This is the first benchmark that measures the agent layer where the next wave of "AI does the documentation" claims will land. A 70% success rate on the easy, scripted work suggests the autonomous-EHR-agent market is not where its marketing slides say it is. Useful counterweight when a vendor next offers to "let the AI close the loop."

5. OpenAI tells Washington it wants the safe-harbour and the speed limit

STAT, 6 May, on OpenAI's new health-policy blueprint: the company is asking US regulators for federal pre-emption to override state-level AI rules, liability protection for clinicians using AI tools, reimbursement codes for AI-assisted care, and a fast-track FDA pathway for adaptive models. STAT's framing — "they want to have their cake and eat it too" — is the blunt version of a longer concern: OpenAI is now the one player whose products are used widely enough that regulatory structure follows lobbying preferences, not the other way round.

The view from the surgery: Where US payers go on AI reimbursement codes, NHS commissioning eventually follows — usually three years later, in a worse-funded form. The company asking for safe-harbour also runs 230 million weekly health questions. That is a lot of triage already happening outside the regulated system.

From the Eye

Three quieter items. Adam Rodman at Beth Israel Deaconess offered a working rule in the Harvard Gazette this week: a chatbot is most useful "before you go and see a doctor — or after," never during an emergency. About 32% of US adults have now consulted one for medical advice; cyberchondria is the new presenting complaint, and patients arriving with a Claude-printed differential of glioblastoma multiforme is now a documented Wednesday morning. The NHS England GitHub close-source deadline lands Monday 11 May — three days from now. The FSFE's open letter has crossed 1,200 signatures, the Engineering Board has not yet blinked; expect a quiet extension or a noisy U-turn early next week. And the misattribution machine is now scaling: Aaron Parnas reported that within an hour of the White House Correspondents' Dinner shooter being named, AI-generated accounts had falsely linked dozens of public figures to him — Tom Hanks, Sydney Sweeney, Pierre Poilievre, Barack Obama. The tech that two years ago made deepfake videos now makes fake witness lists before the police press conference.
