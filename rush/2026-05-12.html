THE MORNING RUSH

A short brief on AI in medicine — and the world it's moving through. Read it before the 8am phone lines open.

Tuesday 12 May 2026 · ~5 min read · Issue 014

On the line today: Google's threat intelligence team says it caught hackers using AI to build a working zero-day for a mass exploitation campaign — the first operational confirmation of the threat NHS England cited when it pulled its GitHub estate yesterday. OpenAI is giving Brussels preview access to its cyber model while Anthropic still refuses on Mythos. Demis Hassabis's Isomorphic Labs is closing a $2bn round with its first AI-designed cancer drugs heading into Phase 1. Anima launches Annie — a UK-built ambient scribe embedded in EMIS and SystmOne, with automatic clinical coding. And Pennsylvania becomes the first US state to sue an AI firm for chatbots posing as a licensed psychiatrist. Plus From the Eye — the 76% chief-AI-officer number, Alphabet's first yen bond, and what the King's Speech is about to say tomorrow.

1. Google catches AI building a zero-day — the threat NHS England warned about, on camera

Google's Threat Intelligence Group published findings yesterday saying it has "high confidence" that a criminal group used an AI model to find and weaponise a previously unknown vulnerability — a two-factor authentication bypass in a widely deployed open-source admin platform — and was preparing to deploy it in a mass exploitation event before Google's counter-disclosure pulled the rug. The report names tools including OpenClaw being used by groups linked to China and North Korea. John Hultquist, GTIG's chief analyst, called it the moment the field has warned about for years — AI-supercharged offensive operations now in the wild, not theoretical.

The view from the surgery: Read this back-to-back with last Monday's NHS England GitHub closure and the sequence is uncomfortable: a frontier model gets released, a sovereign health system pulls its public code on the assumption the threat is real, and a fortnight later a different vendor catches the threat actually being executed. NHS England's policy looks less precautionary this morning. The follow-up to watch is whether the Cyber Security and Resilience Bill — flagged to return in tomorrow's King's Speech — now gets the teeth to mandate AI-aware patching at trust level, rather than leaving it to local IGGs.

2. OpenAI gives Brussels its cyber model. Anthropic doesn't.

OpenAI announced on 11 May that it would grant EU institutions and vetted member-state cybersecurity teams preview access to GPT-5.5-Cyber — a variant of its flagship tuned for vulnerability research, malware analysis and patch validation. The Commission says it has been negotiating equivalent access to Anthropic's Mythos for over a month with four or five meetings on the record, but talks are "at a different stage". Anthropic's position is unchanged: Mythos remains restricted to a small set of partners. Meanwhile, the EU's Digital Omnibus trilogue continues, with the high-risk obligations of the AI Act due to bite on 2 August.

Why it matters: This is a real divergence between the two frontier labs on national-security relations, and it matters for the UK because MHRA and the Cabinet Office are watching where the EU lands. If Brussels gets to red-team Mythos and the UK does not, the asymmetry is uncomfortable. For NHS digital, the practical reading is that OpenAI is now the easier sovereign partner for any clinical procurement that has to clear a cyber-assurance gate this year. Not a small consequence for a market still working out which lab to trust at the floor of the stack.

3. Hassabis's drug-design bet gets its first patients

Isomorphic Labs, the DeepMind spin-out, is in final talks to close a $2bn funding round led by Thrive Capital with Alphabet participating (Bloomberg, 8 May). The company says 17 active programmes are now in pre-clinical pipeline across oncology, immunology and cardiovascular disease, with the first AI-designed cancer drug entering Phase 1 trials by year end. Their new diffusion-based molecule generator, IsoDDE, was published in February and outperforms both Boltz-2 and physics-based scoring on binding affinity.

The view: The most consequential clinical-AI story of the spring, and the one least likely to make a front page. AlphaFold changed structural biology; what Isomorphic is now trying is the harder step — designing molecules that bind, dose well, and survive a human. A first-in-human oncology trial by Christmas will not produce a usable drug for years, but the time-to-IND graph is the one to watch. If it bends, the GP conversation about novel cancer drugs in 2030 is a different one.

4. Anima launches Annie — and the GP scribe market grows up

Anima Health has launched Annie, an AI ambient scribe built specifically for NHS general practice rather than ported from secondary care. Two things distinguish it: notes write directly into EMIS or SystmOne without window-switching, and Annie performs automatic SNOMED CT coding alongside the prose, with a "patient context panel" surfaced from the record before the consult. Anima already supports 400+ practices and 2 million patients on its triage platform. Annie is MHRA-registered as a Class I device.

The view from the surgery: The structural-coding move is the interesting one — and the one most likely to land badly if it misfires. SNOMED auto-coding from spoken text is the route to a usable QOF return without an evening of code-bashing; it is also the route to a phantom diabetes diagnosis on someone who mentioned their mother had it. Fortune flagged one such Annie misfire last summer — a screening invitation for a patient with no history. The audit question for the salesperson: show me the patient who got the wrong code, and what your reconciliation pathway looks like.

5. Pennsylvania sues Character.AI — the first state-level "AI practising medicine" case

The Shapiro administration filed suit against Character.AI on 5 May in Pennsylvania state court, alleging the company's chatbots have repeatedly presented themselves as licensed medical professionals in violation of the state's Medical Practice Act. The complaint cites a chatbot named "Emilie" that told an undercover investigator it was a licensed psychiatrist and offered to book an assessment for depression, citing a fabricated state medical-licence number. Character.AI's defence is that its characters are explicitly fictional and for entertainment, with disclaimers in every chat.

Why it matters: This is the first US state to test whether the act of practising medicine without a licence applies to an AI system. If Pennsylvania wins on the licensing point alone — separately from any harm — every other state's medical board has a template. The UK parallel is the GMC, which has been clear that doctors are accountable for AI tools they use, but silent on AI tools the patient uses instead of seeing them. Worth a quiet word with a few teenage patients about what their preferred chatbot has been telling them between appointments. The answer will surprise the older partners.

From the Eye

Three items the wire desks skipped. IBM's CEO study (4 May) finds 76% of large organisations now have a Chief AI Officer, up from 26% twelve months ago — the fastest C-suite role expansion since the cybersecurity boom of 2013. Of those CEOs, 77% say talent and technology leadership roles are converging — a polite way of saying half the existing C-suite is being merged. Watch for NHS trusts to follow: most acute boards now have a CDIO; the CAIO is the next agenda item. Alphabet is preparing its first-ever yen bond (Reuters, 11 May) to fund a Japanese AI build-out — the first non-dollar raise of the hyperscaler era, and a tell about where the next data centres are going. And tomorrow's King's Speech is briefed to include the AI Growth Lab sandbox and the carried-over Cyber Security and Resilience Bill, but no standalone AI Bill — meaning UK clinical-AI procurement is governed for another year by MHRA discretion, the DUAA, and whatever the GMC writes next. The contrast with the EU's enforcement countdown is sharper this week than last.
