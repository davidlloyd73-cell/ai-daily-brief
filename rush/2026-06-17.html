THE MORNING RUSH

A short brief on AI in medicine — and the world it's moving through. Read it before the 8am phone lines open.

Wednesday 17 June 2026 · ~5 min read · Issue 050

On the line today: The Lancet wants AI training made compulsory for GPs just as ministers promise the "most AI-enabled workforce in the world" — but the curriculum is a blank page. A BMJ Open audit finds the chatbots your patients already consult get health advice wrong roughly half the time. America's hospitals get a governance rulebook and a certification badge while we get a registry. In Brussels the Parliament has voted to push the workplace-AI rules back to 2027, and Microsoft has decided it would rather not depend on OpenAI after all. Plus From the Eye — why the weather fills your waiting room, the scribe that examines patients it never heard, and a Danish vaccine algorithm collecting silverware.

1. The Lancet wants AI on the curriculum

A new piece in The Lancet Primary Care, "The mandate for clinical artificial intelligence education in primary care," makes a blunt argument: with 28% of UK GPs already using AI tools for documentation, triage, audit and decision support, leaving training to chance is no longer defensible. The authors want structured, assessed coursework rather than the current learn-as-you-go drift. It lands against the government's stated ambition — buried in the 10 Year Health Plan — for the NHS to become "the most AI-enabled workforce in the world by 2035."

The view from the surgery: Hard to argue with the principle — you don't hand someone a new diagnostic instrument and hope they read the manual. But "mandatory AI training" is easy to announce and hard to write. Who sets it, who examines it, and who keeps it current when the tools change every quarter? A module that's out of date by the time it's accredited is worse than honest ignorance. Get the content right before you make it compulsory.

2. Half the time, the chatbot is wrong

An audit published in BMJ Open put ChatGPT, Gemini, Meta AI, DeepSeek and Grok through ten questions across cancer, vaccines, stem cells, nutrition and athletic performance, and graded the answers. Just under half — 49.6% — were problematic, with roughly one in five rated "highly" so; Grok came bottom of a poor field. It arrives as the patient-safety group ECRI names AI chatbot misuse the single biggest health-technology hazard of 2026, citing breakneck adoption and an almost total absence of oversight.

Why it matters: This is the consultation happening before yours — the patient who's already asked the machine and half-believes its answer. Half wrong is not a rounding error when millions are asking; it's a steady drip of confidently-phrased nonsense entering the room ahead of the history. The "Dr Google" problem with a far smoother bedside manner and no accountability.

3. America issues the rulebook; we issue the registry

Across the Atlantic the governance machinery has clicked into gear. The Coalition for Health AI has published eight detailed governance "playbooks" — built with 150-plus leaders across 100-plus health systems — covering everything from procurement to post-deployment monitoring, and in tandem the Joint Commission has launched a Responsible Use of AI in Healthcare certification that hospitals can actually be accredited against. It is, for all its American bureaucratic heft, a concrete answer to "how do we deploy this safely at scale."

The view: Contrast with home. We have NHS England's ambient-scribe supplier registry and a clutch of guidance documents, but no certification a trust can hold up and say "we meet this standard." Registries tell you what you're allowed to buy; certification tells you whether you're using it safely. The Americans have built the second thing. We are still, characteristically, perfecting the procurement list.

4. Brussels blinks on workplace AI

On Monday the European Parliament approved a provisional agreement to delay the AI Act's high-risk rules for AI used in employment decisions — hiring, firing, monitoring — until December 2027, a year and a half later than planned. It still needs Council sign-off, and the Cypriot presidency is trying to close the file before its term ends on 30 June. The week before, the Commission had quietly published a voluntary Code of Practice on labelling AI-generated content, the disclosure plumbing for the transparency rules.

The view from the surgery: The pattern is now unmistakable — Europe writes the strict law, then extends the deadline every time it approaches, while the labelling regime arrives "voluntary" and toothless. For UK readers this is the third way by accident: we never wrote the hard law, so we have nothing to climb down from. Whether that's pragmatism or drift depends entirely on what the promised NHS AI regulatory framework actually contains when it finally appears.

5. Microsoft would rather not need OpenAI

On 2 June Microsoft unveiled its own family of in-house AI models, explicitly pitched to "lessen reliance on OpenAI and lower costs for developers" — a notable shift for the company that bankrolled OpenAI's rise. It was one move in a crowded month: Anthropic, Google, xAI and OpenAI all shipped in June, and the commentary, The AI Daily Brief included, read it as the platform war turning openly competitive rather than cosily aligned. The era of one model to rule them all is over before it began.

Why it matters: This churn isn't abstract for anyone signing NHS software contracts. The AI tool a trust procures today sits on a foundation model that may be deprecated, repriced or quietly swapped within eighteen months. Lock-in to a single vendor's model is a real risk, and the scribe and decision-support products riding on these platforms inherit every wobble upstream. Buy the capability, not the brand, and read the exit clause before the demo.

From the Eye

Three the front pages didn't run. Item one — actuaries at Discovery Health have crunched the numbers and confirmed what every duty doctor suspects: both very high and very low temperatures drive measurable spikes in GP visits, emergency attendances and hospital admissions. File under "data science discovers the weather," but it's the kind of signal that should be feeding surge planning rather than surprising us every August and January. Item two — a review of twenty ambient AI scribe systems found every single one produced at least one significant inaccuracy, with nine inventing outright clinical detail; the chilling example is a note reading "no masses found" when no examination ever took place. A phantom physical, transcribed straight into the permanent record — worth a thought next time you sign off the AI draft without rereading the examination section. Item three — Evaxion, a Danish biotech, has walked off with the 2026 Prix Galien UK award for best digital health solution for an AI platform that designs vaccine targets; a reminder, amid the chatbot hand-wringing, that the quietly useful end of medical AI is busy winning prizes nobody reports.
