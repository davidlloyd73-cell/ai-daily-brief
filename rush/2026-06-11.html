THE MORNING RUSH

A short brief on AI in medicine — and the world it's moving through. Read it before the 8am phone lines open.

Thursday 11 June 2026 · ~5 min read · Issue 044

On the line today: A new public dialogue says the NHS is rolling out ambient scribes faster than the public even knows they exist — and wants national oversight before trust runs out, with a sharp warning about recording the wrong consultations. New US data finds 8.2 million young people now ask a chatbot for mental-health advice, just as the trials catch up with what works and what doesn't. The MHRA's chief sketches his regulatory philosophy — a hurdles race, not a single high wall — which tells you how the rules heading your way will actually behave. On the broader beat, AI overtook every other cause of US job cuts this year, and Anthropic filed confidentially for an IPO while still blacklisted by the Pentagon it fell out with. Plus From the Eye — chatbots citing retracted science, the layoffs the figures can't see, and a £900m shopping trolley.

1. The scribe is already in the room — the public just found out

A public dialogue commissioned by AI firm Newton's Tree, reported this week, reached a blunt conclusion: ambient scribes are spreading through the NHS faster than patients realise, and the rollout needs national oversight — checks on data security, staff training and the accuracy of AI-generated notes — before trust erodes. Awareness among the 41 participants was strikingly low, and several were frustrated to learn the technology was already listening in some settings. Notably, they wanted clinicians to keep discretion over when to switch it on, citing domestic abuse and other highly personal disclosures as consultations where a transcript should never exist.

The view from the surgery: That last point is the one I'd nail to the consulting-room door. The efficiency case for scribes is fine; the governance instinct here is finer. A patient disclosing abuse, an immigration fear, a suicidal thought — those are exactly the moments where "it's all being recorded" changes what gets said, and where the off switch matters more than the time saved. Telling patients the machine is on, and being willing to turn it off, isn't a courtesy. It's the consent.

2. 8.2 million young people are taking their feelings to a chatbot

A nationally weighted US survey now estimates 19.2% of young people — around 8.2 million — have sought mental-health advice from an AI chatbot, up from 13.1% a year earlier. Girls, older teens and Black youth were the heaviest users; 43% engage at least monthly, and nearly 92% found the advice helpful. Meanwhile a randomised trial in NEJM AI showed a purpose-built generative chatbot genuinely reduced symptoms of depression, anxiety and eating disorders — yet every head-to-head study against human therapists for moderate-to-severe illness still favours the human, and a separate test of two dozen chatbots found none responded adequately to someone at risk of suicide.

Why it matters: Both halves are true, which is the trap. For a mild low mood on a waiting list measured in months, a free, patient, 3am chatbot may be the only thing that answers — and we shouldn't be precious about that. But "helpful" is what the user feels, not what's safe, and the gap between those two words is widest exactly where the risk is highest. When a young patient mentions their chatbot, the useful question isn't whether they should use it, but what it's been telling them.

3. The MHRA's man tips his hand: a hurdles race, not a wall

Lawrence Tallon, chief executive of the MHRA, has been setting out how he wants AI regulation to work — and the design choices are revealing. Rather than one high barrier to market, he favours a "hurdles race": lighter entry, then continuous post-market surveillance on real-world data, with the rules looking not just at the device but at the professionals and institutions using it. The overarching framework, informed by the National Commission into the Regulation of AI in Healthcare, is still promised this year.

The view: This is the right shape, and an honest one — an adaptive tool that changes after approval can't be policed by a one-time gate. But notice where the weight lands. "Post-market surveillance" means the real-world data being watched is your patients, in your consultations; and "the professionals using it" means some of the regulatory burden is quietly being parked on the clinician, not just the vendor. A hurdles race is fairer than a wall — provided someone's actually standing at the hurdles. Post-market monitoring is only as good as the body resourced to do it.

4. AI is now the leading stated reason for US job cuts

The labour figures have crossed a line. AI is now the single most-cited reason for US job cuts in 2026, with AI-linked layoffs already exceeding the combined totals of 2024 and 2025. Employers announced over 97,000 cuts in May — the worst May since 2020 — and tech bore the brunt. The subtler signal sits underneath: economists keep finding AI's real bite is in hiring, not firing, with graduates aged 22–27 facing notably higher unemployment as the bottom rung of the ladder quietly disappears.

Why it matters: I read this straight back into medicine. The argument for AI in the NHS is "free the clinician for higher-value work" — but the economy-wide pattern is that the junior, entry-level role is what gets thinned first. In our world that's the F2, the trainee, the new HCA learning the craft by doing the boring bits. Automate away the apprenticeship and you save money this year and lose the consultant you needed in fifteen. Worth keeping in view as the scribes and copilots arrive.

5. Anthropic files to go public — while still on the Pentagon's blacklist

Anthropic, maker of the Claude models, confidentially filed a draft IPO prospectus with the SEC on 1 June, days after a funding round valuing it near $965bn. The oddity sits in the footnotes: the same company is blacklisted by the Pentagon, after its CEO refused to let the models be used for mass surveillance of Americans or fully autonomous weapons, and the President threatened to ban its software across government. It's chasing a near-trillion-dollar listing on a $47bn run-rate while one of the largest customers on earth has shown it the door.

The view from the surgery: I file this beside every NHS AI business case. The clinical tools we're adopting run on exactly these platforms, owned by firms valued at a trillion dollars before profit and capable of falling out with a government overnight. That's not an argument against the tools — it's an argument for not betting a care pathway on a single supplier whose finances, and politics, can turn on a sentence from its founder.

From the Eye

Three the front pages didn't run. Item one — a quiet but unnerving study tested ChatGPT-4o, DeepSeek and Grok 3 on stem-cell research questions and found they happily cited retracted papers as if sound, with no flag that the science had been withdrawn. The patient who arrives with a confident chatbot summary may be quoting a study the field has already binned — and the model has no idea. Item two — the layoffs in item four are almost certainly undercounted: roughly three-quarters of laid-off Americans never file for unemployment, so the official data that policymakers steer by is structurally blind to the scale of AI's labour effect. We are flying this transition on a broken altimeter. Item three — barely covered, NHS Shared Business Services has stood up a £900m "Healthcare AI Solutions" framework, a national buying route covering diagnostics, predictive analytics, robotics and consultancy. The procurement plumbing is being laid before the regulatory framework it's meant to run under even exists — order of operations worth noting.
