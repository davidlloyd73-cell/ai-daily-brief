A short brief on AI in medicine — and the world it's moving through. Read it before the 8am phone lines open.

Monday 25 May 2026 · ~5 min read · Issue 027

On the line today: The Pope publishes an encyclical on artificial intelligence this morning — and presents it alongside a co-founder of Anthropic, which tells you something about who the moral authorities now are. Closer to home, the National Commission examining how to regulate health AI lands on an unfashionable conclusion: the binding constraint isn't the technology, it's trust. Anthropic and the Gates Foundation put $200m behind AI for the diseases the market forgets. A big US health system claims it has cut admin time by four-fifths with ChatGPT — believe the direction, not the decimal. And the chip wars reach the layer underneath your pilot. Plus From the Eye — a machine settles an eighty-year-old maths problem, "indispensable" agents nobody is measuring, and the credentials hiding in your infusion pump.

1. The Pope writes an encyclical on AI — and shares the stage with Anthropic

This morning at 11:30 in the Vatican's Synod Hall, Pope Leo XIV presents Magnifica Humanitas, his first encyclical, on "safeguarding the human person in the time of artificial intelligence." He signed it on 15 May — pointedly, the 135th anniversary of Leo XIII's Rerum Novarum, the document that founded modern Catholic social teaching during the upheavals of industrialisation. The framing is deliberate: AI as a labour-and-dignity question, not a gadget question. Breaking with precedent, the American-born Pope will speak at the launch himself, with doctrine chief Cardinal Fernández presenting and a co-founder of Anthropic on the platform beside him.

The view: Set aside your theology — the choreography is the story. When the oldest institution in the West writes its first major teaching on a technology and invites the technologist to stand next to it, that's a frontier lab buying moral legitimacy and a church buying relevance, in one handshake. Worth reading for the questions it raises about work and human worth, which land squarely in a consulting room.

2. The health-AI commission decides the real barrier is trust, not tech

The National Commission into the Regulation of AI in Healthcare, advising the MHRA, held a public webinar last week ahead of its final recommendations this summer. The headline from Professor Henrietta Hughes — England's Patient Safety Commissioner and chair of its Health Systems working group — is a useful reframing: the primary barrier to adoption in the NHS is not compute, budgets or even accuracy, but trust. That means trust from clinicians being asked to stake their registration on a tool's output, and from patients being asked to let it into the room. The Commission took some 770 submissions to its call for evidence.

Why it matters: This is the right diagnosis. We've spent two years arguing about model accuracy when the thing actually throttling deployment is that no one has resolved who carries the can when an ambient summary quietly drops a red-flag symptom. Get indemnity and accountability clear and adoption follows; leave them fuzzy and the best model in the world sits unused.

3. Anthropic and the Gates Foundation put $200m behind the neglected diseases

Anthropic and the Gates Foundation announced a four-year, $200m partnership — grant funding, Claude credits and technical support — aimed at global health, life sciences, education and economic mobility in low- and middle-income countries, where some 4.6 billion people lack reliable access to essential health services. The health strand is the largest, with named first targets that are revealing: polio, HPV and pre-eclampsia/eclampsia. The pitch is to speed vaccine and therapy development and help health ministries actually use the data they already hold.

The view from the surgery: Eclampsia on the list is the tell. This is being aimed, at least in part, at the unglamorous killers of mothers in places with no scribe shortage to solve — a more defensible use of frontier AI than another note-summariser. Whether grant credits survive contact with weak local infrastructure is the open question, but the direction is sound.

4. A US health system says ChatGPT cut its admin time by 80%

AdventHealth, a large American not-for-profit system, has rolled out "ChatGPT for Healthcare" — OpenAI's regulated, compliance-wrapped version launched in January — across clinical, IT, finance and HR functions. The reported figure is an 80% reduction in time spent on administrative tasks, with physician advisors using it to build structured chart summaries and draft initial clinical rationales. Their stated strategy is the interesting part: treat adoption as the product, on the view that the hard problem isn't the model but getting humans to use it safely and consistently.

Why it matters: Take the 80% with a fistful of salt — it's a vendor case study, not a trial, and "time on admin tasks" is a rubber ruler. But the underlying insight is sound and applies to Harrow as much as Florida: the bottleneck is behaviour and governance, not capability. The systems that win won't have the cleverest model; they'll have figured out how to make ordinary clinicians trust it on a Tuesday.

5. The chip wars reach the layer underneath your pilot

Anthropic is reportedly in talks to run some of Claude on Microsoft's own Maia 200 chips — specifically for inference, the bit that answers queries rather than trains the model — following Microsoft's $5bn investment and Anthropic's $30bn Azure commitment last November. Nadella claims the Maia 200 offers over 30% more "tokens per dollar" than its current silicon. The strategic logic is reducing everyone's dependence on Nvidia's perpetually short H100/H200 supply.

The view: This sounds like plumbing because it is — but it's the plumbing your future EHR assistant runs on. Inference cost is what decides whether the triage tool or ambient scribe a trust pilots this year is still affordable when scaled to every clinician next year. The race has quietly moved from who has the cleverest model to who can run a good-enough one cheaply enough to put it everywhere.

From the Eye

The machine that did the maths. Largely lost amid the louder product launches, OpenAI said an internal reasoning model autonomously disproved a case of the Erdős unit-distance conjecture, a discrete-geometry problem first posed in 1946 — not retrieving a known answer but generating a genuine counter-result. File under "narrow but real," and a useful corrective next time someone tells you these systems only ever remix. Indispensable, unmeasured. A new industry survey found 94% of healthcare organisations now call AI agents "indispensable" to operations — while only 59% have any standard process for tracking whether they actually work. The gap between reverence and measurement is precisely where bad tools hide; if you can't say what it's doing, you can't say it's safe. The credentials in the cupboard. A security report this week found that roughly 74% of compromised healthcare devices were holding live credentials for EHR systems — the connected pumps, monitors and scanners quietly storing the keys to the record. As trusts bolt AI onto every device, the attack surface is no longer the laptop; it's the kit on the ward.
