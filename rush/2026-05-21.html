THE MORNING RUSH

A short brief on AI in medicine — and the world it's moving through. Read it before the 8am phone lines open.

Thursday 21 May 2026 · ~5 min read · Issue 023

On the line today: The AMA stops telling patients to avoid chatbots and starts coaching them how to use one — the first real attempt to manage rather than scold. New survey data shows the governance curve trailing the adoption curve badly: most organisations can't tell you whether their deployed AI still works. The FDA opens a real-time, AI-monitored clinical-trial pilot with a comment window closing in a week. Commure raises at a $7bn valuation, confirming that the money in clinical AI is flowing to the back office, not the diagnosis. And the Commons workforce inquiry quietly buries the government's old "pro-innovation, hands-off" line. Plus From the Eye — the AI Daily Brief on the end of the subsidy era, a fake eye disease that chatbots diagnosed to thousands, and a sobering diagnostic-benchmark number.

1. AMA stops warning patients off chatbots — and starts coaching them

The American Medical Association on 20 May released a patient-facing infographic with recommended prompts and explicit cautions for using AI chatbots on health questions, framing the tools as a complement to — not a replacement for — a doctor. It is a notable shift in posture: the same body that last week called doctor deepfakes a public-health crisis is now accepting that patients will type symptoms into ChatGPT regardless, and trying to shape how. More than 40 million people now use ChatGPT daily for health information.

The view from the surgery: Useful, because it is the first time a major medical body has tried to teach patients how to use these tools rather than simply telling them not to. The framing is worth borrowing in the room — "bring me what the chatbot told you" is a far more workable consultation than pretending the search didn't happen.

2. The governance curve is trailing the adoption curve — badly

Survey data flagged in Healthcare IT Today on 17 May lands an uncomfortable point: only 59% of healthcare organisations have a standardised process to track the performance of the AI agents they have already deployed, 68% of nurses feel insufficiently trained to use them, and 60% lack confidence in their organisation's AI oversight. This against a backdrop where roughly four in five doctors now report using AI at work for notes, correspondence and research.

Why it matters: Adoption has comfortably outrun assurance. We are now running clinical-adjacent tools that a large share of organisations cannot confirm are still behaving as validated at the point they were procured. Model drift is not hypothetical, and "we turned it on in 2025" is not a monitoring strategy. If your PCN has a scribe live, ask who is watching its output quality — the honest answer is often nobody.

3. FDA opens a real-time, AI-monitored clinical-trial pilot

The FDA has announced a pilot using AI and cloud computing to monitor clinical-trial data in real time, alongside a separate AI-Enabled Optimization of Early-Phase Clinical Trials programme. The agency's pitch is that real-time surveillance could strip 20–40% off overall trial timelines and sharpen early go/no-go and dose-selection calls. Comments on the early-phase request for information are due by 29 May. It sits within a broader FDA shift toward accepting a single pivotal trial rather than two.

The view: Faster trials are welcome if the monitoring is genuinely better and not merely cheaper. But real-time AI surveillance of safety signals raises the same accountability question as everywhere else in this field — who carries the can when the model under-calls a signal it should have flagged, or floods reviewers with noise it shouldn't have.

4. Commure raises at $7bn — the money is in the back office

Commure, a healthcare-operations AI company, has raised $70m in fresh financing at a $7bn post-money valuation, to expand its platform for automating administrative work — the coding, scheduling and correspondence that costs US providers an estimated $1 trillion a year. The round continues a clear pattern: investors are funding the unglamorous plumbing, not the headline diagnostic models.

Why it matters: The near-term return on clinical AI is in the letters, the coding and the rota — not the clever differential-diagnosis engine. The NHS lesson is the same one Heidi's documentation data has been quietly making for a month: the savings that actually materialise are administrative. Worth keeping in mind when a board paper promises diagnostic transformation but the business case rests entirely on time saved typing.

5. Commons workforce inquiry buries the "hands-off" line

The Commons Business and Trade Committee is preparing its report from the inquiry "Artificial Intelligence, business and the future of the workforce" (evidence closed 3 April), examining bias, explainability, accountability and the rise of AI-generated workplace grievances. Lawyers reading the runes call it the end of the government's earlier "pro-innovation, non-statutory" stance on workplace AI. In parallel, the ICO is consulting on draft guidance for automated decision-making until 29 May.

The view from the surgery: Relevant to GPs twice over — as employers running practices, and as professionals whose own appraisal, prescribing and performance data increasingly passes through automated systems. The ICO consultation closing on 29 May is the one to actually respond to if you have a view on how automated decisions about clinicians get made and contested.

From the Eye

Three the front pages didn't run. Item one — The AI Daily Brief (Nathaniel Whittemore, 14 May) ran a deep dive on Anthropic's Claude pricing changes and "the end of the subsidy era" — the quiet structural story that frontier-model access has been sold below cost, and the bill is now arriving. That repricing, not any benchmark, is what will reshape clinical-AI procurement maths over the next 18 months; the vendor quote in your 2025 pilot may not survive contact with 2027 economics. Item two — a researcher in Gothenburg invented a fake eye condition called "bixonimania" (eyelid discolouration and sore eyes, supposedly from screen blue light), uploaded two fabricated preprints, and watched ChatGPT and other chatbots confidently diagnose it to users. The cleanest demonstration yet of the preprint-to-chatbot pipeline laundering fiction into "fact" — and a reminder that "the AI cited a paper" is not the reassurance patients think it is. Item three — a medRxiv benchmark of 16 leading models across 200 vignettes covering 20 frequently-misdiagnosed conditions found the best performer corrected the misdiagnosis only 55% of the time. A sobering figure for anyone tempted to treat the chatbot as a reliable second opinion on the hard cases — which are precisely the ones it gets sent.
