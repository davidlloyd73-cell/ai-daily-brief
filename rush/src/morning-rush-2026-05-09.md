## 1. King's Speech on Wednesday — and the AI Growth Lab is the headline

The State Opening of Parliament is **Wednesday 13 May**. The Lords Library briefing confirms a Digital Information and Smart Data Bill, a Cyber Security and Resilience Bill, and — the eye-catcher — formal legislation for the **AI Growth Lab**, the cross-economy regulatory sandbox DSIT consulted on through January. Healthcare is one of four named target sectors. The Lab is not a regulation-off zone; it would set strict, time-limited carve-outs from specific rules under licence, with consumer protection, fundamental rights and IP all explicitly excluded. The comprehensive AI Bill on safety and copyright is still not ready, and remains punted to a later session.

**The view from the surgery:** Healthcare in the sandbox sounds promising on a press release and quite different on the ground. The likely first uses are AVT-and-EHR write-back, autonomous triage agents, and generative discharge summaries — all things MHRA, GMC and the DPA would otherwise want a slower look at. Worth watching whether RCGP gets a seat at the Lab, or whether the pilots are designed by people who have never read DCB0129.

## 2. Anthropic rents the Colossus — and floats data centres in space

Anthropic announced on **6 May** that it has taken all the available compute capacity at SpaceX's **Colossus 1** in Memphis — more than **300 megawatts** running on **220,000+ NVIDIA GPUs**. Pro and Max five-hour rate limits on Claude Code have already doubled, and the peak-hour throttle is gone. Buried in the same release: Anthropic and SpaceX have "expressed interest" in jointly developing **multiple gigawatts of orbital AI compute** — data centres in low earth orbit, cooled by vacuum.

**Why it matters:** The compute crunch Azeem Azhar wrote about earlier in the week has its first concrete fix, and the politics is unusual — Musk's xAI is a direct Claude competitor; Anthropic is now, effectively, his tenant. For NHS digital procurement the practical effect is that headroom on whichever Claude tier a trust uses just trebled. Orbital compute is a decade-plus from procurement reality, but it's where the question of who runs the most capable clinical models — and where the data sits — eventually goes.

## 3. A New York judge rules ChatGPT-flagged grant cuts unconstitutional

US District Judge Colleen McMahon ruled on **7 May** that DOGE's cancellation of **1,400+ National Endowment for the Humanities grants — over $100m — violated the First and Fifth Amendments**. DOGE staffers, the court found, used ChatGPT to scan grant applications for "DEI", "LGBTQ", "equity" and similar terms, and pushed flagged grants onto a kill list. One terminated project: an anthology of short fiction by Jewish writers from the Soviet Union, titled *In the Shadow of the Holocaust*. McMahon's line is the bit that will travel: **"DOGE's use of AI to identify DEI-related material neither excuses presumptively unconstitutional conduct nor gives the Government carte blanche to engage in it."**

**The view:** The first US court ruling to address an arm of government using a chatbot as a decision tool. The case isn't about the model's accuracy — it's about whether handing a viewpoint-discrimination job to ChatGPT is a defence. Read the relevance for UK procurement straight: anyone in NHS or DH considering an LLM in a benefits, eligibility or rationing pathway has now seen what the audit trail looks like in court.

## 4. Doctolib buys Medicus — and commits £100m to UK general practice

French digital-health giant **Doctolib** announced its acquisition of **Medicus** this week, alongside a **£100m UK investment** over five years, **150 new hires** and a London R&D centre focused on primary care. The combined platform pitches appointment booking, video consultation, ambient scribing and patient-facing triage in one stack — head-to-head with Accurx and the EMIS-Optum bundle.

**Why it matters:** Continental capital arriving on the GP side of the NHS is genuinely new. Most UK ambient-scribe spend so far has gone to Australian (Heidi, Lyrebird) or US (TORTUS, Microsoft Dragon, Corti) suppliers; the AVT registry has nineteen on it. A French entrant the size of Doctolib changes the data-residency conversation — Doctolib hosts in the EU, a different DTAC story from a Sydney or San Francisco product. Worth watching whether the £100m goes to NHS-pricing procurement or a private-GP play.

## 5. *npj Digital Medicine*: clinical AI needs flight rules, not autopilot

A new piece in *npj Digital Medicine* — **"Flight rules for clinical AI"** — argues the best mental model for ambient AI in medicine is the way aviation handled cockpit automation, not the way medicine has handled past tools. The authors propose **scenario-based training**, **clinician benchmarking against unaided performance** and a **minimum unaided practice** requirement after AI deployment — the medical equivalent of pilots being required to keep manual flying hours. The core risk they name is the **automation paradox**: operators lose the skills to intervene exactly as the systems doing the work get better.

**The view from the surgery:** The most useful frame I've read for explaining to a registrar why I still want them to compose the differential before the scribe writes the letter. Two practical takeaways: log a few unaided consultations a month, and keep a record of where the AI draft and your own note diverge. That second thing is the medico-legal evidence base nobody is yet collecting.

### From the Eye

Three items the front pages mostly missed. **Mythos is reportedly splitting the White House** — the *Washington Post* on **8 May** describes "the secretive AI hacking tool fracturing Trump's White House", with NSC, OSTP and Hassett's NEC running parallel responses and briefing against each other; the FDA-style pre-release review for frontier models is now moving on a fortnight timeline. **The AMA's 2026 physician AI survey** lands at **81% awareness or use** — up from 66% in 2024 — with the biggest growth in administrative use; 38% of US physicians say a colleague has shown them an AI tool that worried them, and nobody is publishing the equivalent UK figure. **And the largest clinical-summarisation hallucination dataset to date** — 12,999 clinician-annotated sentences in another *npj Digital Medicine* pipeline study — finds a **1.47% hallucination rate and 3.45% omission rate**. Not bad arithmetic; absolutely catastrophic if the omitted sentence is "penicillin allergy". The open framework, CREOLA, runs on local models — useful for any practice CSO to have on the desk before signing the next AVT contract.
