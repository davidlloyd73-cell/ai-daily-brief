## 1. Nvidia moves into the consulting room

On Wednesday Nvidia announced it is co-building a dedicated healthcare model with **Abridge**, the Pittsburgh ambient-documentation firm now valued at $5.3bn. The model will sit on Nvidia's open **Nemotron** family — weights *and* training data published — trained on de-identified consultation audio using Blackwell hardware, and will stretch beyond note-taking into clinical decision support. Deployment is slated for the back half of this year. Nvidia already holds a stake in Abridge, so it now sells the chips, owns a slice of the customer, and helps build the product.

**Why it matters:** The open-weights, open-data choice is the interesting bit — it means an NHS trust could, in principle, inspect what the thing was trained on rather than taking a black box on faith. That's genuinely better than the closed scribes most of us are being sold. But watch the vertical integration: when the silicon vendor, the shareholder and the model-maker are the same outfit, "independent evaluation" gets harder to come by exactly as these tools start nudging clinical decisions, not just transcribing them.

## 2. South-west London bets 20,000 clinicians on one scribe

Four trusts — St George's, Epsom and St Helier, Croydon, and Kingston and Richmond — are rolling out ambient scribing from **Lyrebird Health** to up to 20,000 clinicians, starting at around 10,000 and described as the largest ambient-voice deployment in NHS history. The business case is the familiar one: documentation time reclaimed, capacity returned, clinicians looking at patients rather than screens.

**The view from the surgery:** I'm all for getting the keyboard out from between me and the patient. But twenty thousand clinicians on a single supplier is a procurement decision with a long tail. If the notes drift, the pricing jumps at renewal, or the firm gets acquired, you don't have a pilot to pause — you have a region that has forgotten how to write its own records. Scale is the selling point and the risk in the same sentence.

## 3. Philips puts a number on the time AI is saving

Philips' **Future Health Index 2026**, out this week, reports that 65% of clinicians have increased their use of employer-provided AI, and that those using it claim average time savings of around **132 hours a year** — with half saying they now have capacity for roughly eight more patients a week. It's the cheeriest headline figure the sector has produced in a while.

**The view:** Treat the number as a vendor-flavoured estimate, because it is — self-reported, by the enthusiasts, in a survey from a company that sells the kit. The honest question isn't whether AI saves time; it's where the saved time *goes*. If those 132 hours become eight more patients a week, that's not relief for a knackered workforce — it's the same workforce running hotter. Time saved is only a win if someone decides, deliberately, not to spend it.

## 4. The EU AI Act's teeth — and the dentist's quiet retreat

Mark **2 August 2026**: the date the bulk of the EU AI Act becomes enforceable, when the AI Office gains real powers over general-purpose model providers — documentation demands, evaluations, even source-code access for the likes of GPT, Claude and Gemini. Yet in the same breath, a provisional **Omnibus** deal pushes the Act's toughest *high-risk* obligations back — standalone systems now to 2 December 2027, embedded ones to August 2028.

**Why it matters:** This is the regulatory weather heading for medical AI, and it's blowing two ways at once. Brussels is arming itself against the foundation-model giants while quietly granting the high-risk crowd — which includes a great deal of clinical software — another year or two of grace. For us, it means the diagnostic and triage tools arriving in surgeries will run ahead of the hard rules meant to govern them. Useful to know which promises have a deadline and which have a postponement.

---

## 5. OpenAI files to go public, a step behind

OpenAI confidentially submitted a draft **S-1** to the SEC on 10 June, days after Anthropic did the same — two of the biggest names in AI now queuing for the public markets within a week of each other, amid what analysts are calling the most active model-release month of the year. The same week, OpenAI cut a deal to distribute its models and Codex tool through Oracle's cloud.

**The view from the surgery:** I keep a quiet tally of who owns the engines under our clinical tools, and the answer is increasingly "shareholders." Once these firms are public, the pressure shifts from research to quarterly numbers — and the roadmap for the model your scribe depends on starts answering to an earnings call. Not a reason to down tools, but a reason to ask any NHS supplier what happens to their product if the platform beneath it has a bad quarter.

### From the Eye

Three the front pages didn't run. **Item one** — fraud researchers describe scam "compounds" that, when a suspicious victim demands a video call, hire *real human beings* to sit on camera while deepfake software remoulds their faces into the fictional love interest. We have reached the point where the cheapest way to fake a person convincingly is to rent an actual one and paint over them. **Item two** — a survey out this week finds nearly **85%** of adults can no longer reliably tell real content from AI-generated, up from 66% a year ago; one deepfaked video call already relieved engineering firm Arup of $25.6m. The line between evidence and confection has moved faster than anyone's defences. **Item three** — a quieter structural fact: of the 1,300-odd AI devices the FDA has now authorised, roughly **1,039 — about 80% — are in radiology**. The "AI revolution in medicine" is, for now, overwhelmingly a revolution in reading scans; the GP's consulting room remains the frontier the approvals haven't reached.
