## 1. Streeting out, Murray in — and the Bill that carried the AI plan

Health Secretary **Wes Streeting** resigned on **Thursday 14 May**, citing "lost confidence" in Keir Starmer's leadership after Labour's local-election results. The timing was excruciating: his resignation landed twenty-four hours after the **King's Speech** introduced his **NHS Modernisation Bill** — the legislative vehicle for abolishing NHS England, the Single Patient Record by 2028 (maternity and frailty first), the neighbourhood-health-centre plan, and most of the AI-and-data agenda the 10-Year Plan promised. **James Murray**, MP for Ealing North and previously chief secretary to the Treasury, was confirmed in the brief on Thursday evening.

**The view from the surgery:** The Bill's chief political author is gone before second reading. Murray comes in as a Treasury technocrat, not a health-policy figure, and his first decision is whether to defend the SPR clauses as drafted — including the powers letting DHSC override GP data controllers — or open them to amendment. The BMA's May collective action is already squarely focused on data-sharing agreements; the new Secretary of State has both a structural fight and a contractual one in week one. The Treasury instinct in this seat will be to push delivery; the GP instinct will be to push consent. Whichever wins decides the shape of NHS AI for the rest of this parliament.

## 2. The Lancet audits 2.5 million papers — and finds AI hallucinations in the references

A letter in *The Lancet* on **7 May** by **Maxim Topaz** (Columbia Data Science Institute) and colleagues reports the first systematic audit of fabricated citations across PubMed Central's Open Access subset — **2.47 million biomedical papers, 97.1 million references**, scanned by a bespoke verification system. The numbers: **4,046 fabricated references in 2,810 papers**, prevalence up **twelvefold since 2023**, and in the first seven weeks of 2026, **one in every 277 published papers** cited a study that does not exist (up from one in 458 last year and one in 2,828 in 2023).

**Why it matters:** The audit dates the sharpest inflection to mid-2024 — exactly when generative AI moved into mainstream academic writing — and is unsubtle about the route. The clinical-guideline implication is plain: a NICE working group quoting a systematic review that quietly cites a fabricated trial is now a plausible failure mode rather than a theoretical one. The fix — automated reference verification at journal submission — is procedural and trivially affordable. The delay in implementing it is the actual scandal.

## 3. Mpathic's chatbot benchmark — the models pass the obvious, fail the subtle

**Mpathic**, a Seattle clinician-led benchmarking outfit, published on **12 May** the first peer-relevant evaluation of six major language models on **300 ten-to-fifteen-turn role plays** designed by fifty licensed clinicians, on suicide risk and eating disorders. The headline: **Anthropic's Claude Sonnet 4.5** scored highest on combined safety and helpfulness; **OpenAI's GPT-5.2** scored highest on harm-avoidance alone. The honest finding, per Mpathic's Danielle Schlosser: "Almost all the models struggled with more nuanced risk signals" — subtle comments about food, withdrawal, hopelessness, or beliefs that escalate slowly across a long conversation.

**The view:** GP-relevant in three ways. Patients are already using these tools as triage. The failure mode is exactly the one we worry about in five-minute appointments — explicit cues are easy, subtle cues are the case. And multi-turn drift is not measured by the FDA, the MHRA or any UK regulator; we lean on the vendors to publish, which Anthropic has now done and OpenAI has not. Last month's AMA call for federal limits on mental-health chatbots is starting to look like the floor, not the ceiling.

## 4. Orgvue puts a number on the AI rehire — almost a third of firms have reversed course

Workforce-planning firm **Orgvue**'s research, refreshed this week, finds **32% of organisations** that made redundancies tied to AI deployment have since had to **rehire**, because the technology could not perform the work as advertised. The most cited case remains **Klarna**, whose CEO Sebastian Siemiatkowski admitted earlier this year that letting AI replace 700 customer-service agents pushed satisfaction and resolution scores low enough to reopen the headcount. Orgvue's broader number: companies spend roughly **$1.27 for every $1** saved by AI-linked workforce cuts, once severance, unemployment and rework are accounted for. Goldman Sachs's January frame still stands — AI investment contributed "basically zero" to US economic growth in 2025.

**Why it matters:** The reversal does not mean the displacement story is wrong — it means it is more uneven and slower than vendor decks claim. The NHS parallel is direct. Trusts cutting administrative posts on the assumption a scribe replaces the role rather than the task are signing themselves up for a Klarna in eighteen months. The few moving in earnest are quietly building hybrid teams. Worth re-reading any business case that lists a workforce-headcount reduction in year two.

## 5. Maryland's deepfake law lands — and the Electoral Commission runs its UK pilot

**Maryland** on **14 May** signed into law a statute prohibiting AI-generated deceptive media in political advertising — the **thirtieth US state** to do so — effective **1 June** ahead of the autumn midterms. The **UK Electoral Commission** has separately opened a **deepfake-detection pilot** for the **7 May English and Welsh local elections**, working with two UK fact-checking labs. Across the Atlantic, the National Republican Senatorial Committee has already deployed an AI-generated video of Texas Democratic Senate candidate **James Talarico** "reading" his own old social-media posts, with the disclaimer in microscopic type.

**The view from the surgery:** UK and US are converging on a labelling regime, and labelling is the regulatory equivalent of cycling lanes painted onto motorways — it does not stop the cars. The clinical analogue is the disclosure question coming for AI-summarised hospital letters. A "this letter was AI-summarised" footer is fine for governance; it will not protect the patient who reads the wrong dose at home.

### From the Eye

Three quieter items working their way through. **Stanford's "natural Ozempic"** — a *Science Daily* piece flagged on **12 April** that researchers had identified a peptide active in mouse and human cells that triggers GLP-1-style appetite suppression without the gut side effects of injectable semaglutide; the clinical lead is years away and the pharmaceutical incentive to discredit it is not. **OpenAI's DeployCo**, the firm's new enterprise-deployment arm, has closed seed funding at a **$10bn pre-money valuation** backed by **Bain & Company, Capgemini and McKinsey** — three of the consultancies that for thirty years told the NHS what to buy now have skin in the game on what gets sold, and the conflict-of-interest disclosures for the next big NHS digital procurement will repay careful reading. And **Optura** closed an **$11m Series A** on **14 May** with the strapline: *"healthcare does not have an AI-adoption problem, it has an AI-results problem"*. The pitch deck argues the average trust has bought six AI tools and measured none of them. Hard to disagree.
