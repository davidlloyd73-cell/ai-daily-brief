## 1. Heidi's UK report — 15 million visits, 86% off documentation, and a two-minute discharge letter

Ambient-scribe vendor **Heidi** published a UK report on **8 May** covering more than **15 million NHS patient visits**. The headline claims: an **86% reduction in documentation time**, **4 million hours of clinical capacity returned**, **burnout improvement reported by 95% of clinicians**. Site-level numbers are the bit worth reading: at one emergency department, **discharge letter processing dropped from 9.03 days to 2.6 minutes**; another trust cleared a **2,700-letter clinic backlog in four months**; and **Dudley Group NHS Foundation Trust** reports **£95,200 of annualised capacity savings** on a single same-day emergency care pilot. Heidi says it now handles **half a million UK patient interactions a week**.

**The view from the surgery:** A vendor-published number, with a vendor-flattering denominator. Eighty-six per cent off documentation time isn't 86% off the GP day — most of us aren't documenting eight hours a day in the first place. The interesting figure is the discharge-letter delta, because slow discharge letters are a real GP harm — patients turning up at the surgery before the post arrives, with no plan, no medication and no continuity. If Heidi has compressed that cycle to minutes at scale, it's a different conversation from the time-saving sales pitch. The right question to ask the salesperson: show me the unedited "before" letter and the unedited "after" letter, on the same patient.

## 2. Anthropic teaches Claude to "dream" — and a medical document firm reports a 50% time cut

Anthropic on **6 May** released a research preview of **"dreaming"** for its managed-agent platform — a scheduled offline process that reviews an agent's past sessions, extracts patterns and writes back as plain-text playbooks the next session can read. It does not retrain the underlying model. It is closer to a registrar going home, doing the post-clinic reflection nobody pays for, and coming in on Monday with a tidier checklist. Anthropic's named case studies are **Harvey** (legal AI, ~6× task-completion improvement) and — more relevant here — **Wisedocs**, a medical document-review firm reporting a **50% reduction** in document review time after enabling dreaming.

**Why it matters:** Medico-legal document review is exactly the work that practices, federations and ICBs are queueing agentic vendors up for — subject access requests, complaints bundles, coronial summaries. A 50% reduction at a US firm with no NHS context is a marketing number, but the architectural shift is real: agents that get measurably better between Friday and Monday change the deployment story. The procurement question is whether the dreams are auditable. A regulator will eventually want to read the playbook the agent wrote about your patients.

## 3. Google's AI Health Coach lands on 19 May — Fitbit app becomes Google Health

Google announced on **7 May** that the **Fitbit app becomes the Google Health app on 19 May**, with a **Gemini-powered AI Health Coach** at **$9.99 a month** as the headline feature: personalised weekly fitness plans, sleep coaching, AI summaries of any health records the user chooses to share, and a chatbot pointed at the user's own fitness and medical data. UK pricing has not yet been published. A new screenless **Fitbit Air** wristband ships alongside. Google's careful framing — "not intended for medical use" — is the same disclaimer Microsoft pinned to **Copilot Health** in March.

**The view:** The disclaimer is the only thing keeping the regulator off the lawn. In the surgery, the practical effect is a different shape of Monday morning: not "Dr Google said it might be cancer", but "the Google Health Coach has flagged that my resting heart rate is up, and asked me to come and see you". A few of those will be useful. A great many will be a paid algorithm seeing pattern in noise. Worth knowing what the patient has already been told before re-explaining it from scratch.

## 4. Trump's AI security order — without the mandatory model tests

**Bloomberg**, **8 May**: the White House is preparing an AI security executive order that would **direct US agencies to partner with frontier-AI companies to defend networks** against AI-enabled cyberattack — but will **stop short of requiring government pre-deployment approval for new frontier models**. NEC director **Kevin Hassett** has been comparing the proposed framework to the FDA. The FDA does not let drugs ship before review.

**Why it matters:** Hassett's analogy is the policy ask; the order is the political compromise. The Mythos hysteria of the past fortnight is the backdrop — the case for pre-release testing has never been stronger, and this order ducks it. The UK reading is straightforward: any safety floor for frontier-clinical models will not come from Washington in this administration, and the EU AI Act path now matters more for what eventually reaches the MHRA's desk.

## 5. Box Elder County, Utah: 40,000 acres, 9 gigawatts, and a referendum

Utah's **Box Elder County Commission** voted unanimously on **4 May** to approve the **Stratos Project** — a **40,000-acre AI and cloud-computing campus** near the Great Salt Lake, backed by Kevin O'Leary. Larger than twice Manhattan; projected to draw up to **9 GW of electricity** (roughly double the state's current consumption) and over **10,000 acre-feet of water**. **More than 1,000 residents** attended the meeting; commissioners adjourned to a private room and approved by livestream. By **9 May**, residents had filed an application for a **November ballot referendum** to overturn the decision.

**The view from the surgery:** Every compute story has a power-and-water story underneath, and that is now civic politics. Most NHS conversations about Anthropic, OpenAI and Google deals do not include the question of which county's water table is being asked to keep the model fluent. They will, before long — particularly when "sovereign UK compute" gets to procurement, and Sizewell C's neighbours notice.

### From the Eye

Three less-trodden items. **A 29-chatbot suicide-screening study** in *Scientific Reports* tested 24 mental-health-branded products and five general-purpose ones against vignettes including suicidal ideation, and **none provided an adequate response** — 14 ranked inadequate, 15 marginal. Sit that next to **RCPsych's February warning** that half a million children are on English mental-health waiting lists, and you have the safeguarding question no GP federation has yet written an SOP for. **The deepfake-doctor scam ecosystem keeps scaling**: *Axios* on **6 May** describes AI-generated videos cloning real physicians' faces and voices to push unregulated supplements — and US pharmacists are now reporting **AI voice-cloned refill calls** purporting to be from a known prescriber, used to authorise controlled-drug refills. Worth a quiet check on how the practice would spot one. **And Aaron Parnas** reports AI-generated attacks targeting him personally are now a daily backdrop — the unglamorous tail of the same generative-fakes pipeline that put Tom Hanks on a fake witness list at last week's WHCD. The tech that two years ago made bad campaign ads now makes weekly weather around any public-facing voice.
