## 1. LMCs ask the Treasury who pays when the scribe is wrong

Pulse reported this week that GP leaders have written to DHSC asking that **state-backed indemnity (CNSGP) be formally extended to cover failures of AI triage, ambient scribes and clinical-decision-support tools** used in NHS-contracted work. The submission says current partner exposure to "a wide range" of personal liabilities from technology the NHS itself is encouraging is "unsustainable". The trigger is Wes Streeting's repeated framing of AI as the productivity solution, paired with NHSE's 31 March guidance that puts accuracy responsibility squarely on the clinician.

**The view from the surgery:** Every partner who has signed an ambient-scribe contract this spring is quietly asking the same question. Medical Protection's line is they will support claims where a human retained final decision-making — but that is not the same as cover, and the contracts being signed bury the indemnification clauses on page eleven. If you're about to sign, read it with the LMC in the room.

## 2. Maryland fines Cigna for letting an AI mark down the bills

The Maryland Insurance Administration has **fined Cigna $80,000 and ordered an immediate halt to "Policy R49"**, the insurer's AI-driven downcoding system that since October 2025 has been knocking high-complexity E/M claims down a level without physician notification or chart review. A separate suit filed 6 April by Philadelphia's Jefferson Health is challenging Aetna's parallel "level-of-severity" inpatient policy. STAT's 8 April investigation has insurers and providers in agreement that ambient AI scribes are nudging coding upward — and now the AI-coded counterpunch from the payer side is hitting the regulator.

**Why it matters:** This is the new shape of the documentation arms race — clinicians' AI summarising consultations into higher-acuity codes, payers' AI quietly overruling at claim time, and adjudication moving into state insurance regulators. It is dollars rather than pounds, but the NHS is not immune: PCN activity-based payments, RTT pathway coding and ICB outcome contracts all sit on documentation nobody is auditing for AI drift.

## 3. Anthropic floats a $900bn valuation as the LLM revenue ranks flip

Bloomberg and CNBC reported on **29 April that Anthropic is reviewing preemptive offers of around $50bn at a valuation between $850bn and $900bn** — more than double its February valuation of $380bn, and ahead of OpenAI's $852bn close. Counterpoint Research separately had Anthropic on a 31.4% Q1 share of LLM revenue versus OpenAI's 29%, despite 134m monthly users to OpenAI's 900m — the per-user economics are a different shape. Google had already committed up to $40bn on 24 April, of which $10bn lands now.

**The view:** A frontier lab tripling its valuation in three months while its main rival's CFO publicly worries about funding compute contracts tells you the markets are no longer distinguishing between "successful AI company" and "essential infrastructure". For NHS procurement watchers, the labs you are licensing clinical AI from this year may have £700bn-class price tags — and pricing power to match — by next.

## 4. OpenAI ships the cyber model it said no one should have

On **29 April Sam Altman announced GPT-5.5-Cyber**, available within days through the company's Trusted Access for Cyber programme to "critical cyber defenders" only — government, critical infrastructure, security vendors, cloud platforms, financial institutions. TechCrunch's headline framed it neatly: a fortnight after publicly chiding Anthropic for holding back Claude Mythos, OpenAI is now restricting its own most cyber-capable model. The functional difference is that Mythos is gated behind a partnership with Apple, Microsoft and Cisco; Cyber is gated behind identity verification.

**Why it matters:** Frontier labs are converging on the same answer to the most-dangerous-capability problem — restrict by user, not by model. NHS trusts are not on any of the trusted-access lists. If a Mythos-class capability ends up in the hands of an organised group targeting hospital middleware before the patches land, the question of who in the NHS holds the relationship with the lab is awkward and unanswered.

## 5. The AMA asks Congress to draw a real line round mental-health chatbots

The American Medical Association formally **called on Congress this month to pass federal safeguards for AI mental-health chatbots**, after Q1 saw 36 US states introduce more than 70 bills on the theme. Illinois and Nevada have banned AI for behavioural health outright; New York and Utah require chatbots to disclose non-human status and refer apparent self-harm to crisis lines. The trigger is a mounting reporting trail — a *Scienceline* analysis last month found 14 of 29 chatbots gave "inadequate" responses to suicidal ideation — and at least one US homicide case in which the suspect's ChatGPT history is now a prosecution exhibit.

**The view from the surgery:** The patients telling us they "talk to ChatGPT before they ring" are not a small group anymore — and the cohort doing it most are the young adults IAPT services have been losing to waiting lists. The RCPsych said something useful here in February. It is past time the GMC matched it with something practical for primary care.

### From the Eye

Three things humming under the front pages. **The Electoral Commission has quietly switched on its first deepfake-detection pilot**, running April to June ahead of the **7 May locals** — built with the Home Office's Accelerated Capability Environment, monitoring social platforms for fake candidate-withdrawal videos and forged audio, with human analyst review before any takedown request. First UK election where the regulator is running its own technical trip-wire; worth noting if anything weird turns up between now and Thursday week. **Microsoft Agent 365 goes generally available today, 1 May** — a corporate runtime for AI agents bolted onto the M365 estate, with a security suite shipped in preview yesterday; expect NHS digital teams to start fielding "can we use this?" questions by Monday, ahead of the answer being ready. **And the JAMA multi-centre ambient-scribe study** quietly published this month found total EHR time fell by **13.4 minutes a day** across five academic systems, with documentation down 16 — modest, inconsistent, and varying widely between clinicians on the same product. Worth holding next to the vendor-deck claim of "two hours a day back" before the next procurement round.
