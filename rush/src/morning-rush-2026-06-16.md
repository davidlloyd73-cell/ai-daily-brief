## 1. Palantir's NHS data deal gets a second opinion

MPs on the Science, Innovation and Technology Committee question the Federated Data Platform rollout today, two weeks after their 70-page report urged the government to trigger the February 2027 break clause and either build in-house or find a UK-owned supplier "more compatible with UK values." The timing is awkward for Palantir: analysis by Foxglove, reported yesterday, found that a third of the trusts using the FDP's inpatient scheduling tool are now doing *fewer* operations than before they adopted it, and that Chelsea and Westminster alone accounts for 84% of the headline fall in outpatient waiting. Health ministers say they will decide "later this year" whether to extend.

**The view from the surgery:** "Largest healthcare data platform" turns out to mean one good hospital and fifteen treading water. That isn't an argument against data platforms — it's an argument against believing the aggregate number before you've seen the spread. We learned this lesson with every QOF dashboard: a national mean hides the trust that's quietly going backwards. If the gains are real, they should survive scrutiny; if they're one outlier wearing a national costume, better to know now than in 2027.

## 2. Pushy chatbots drive women away from screening

A University of Surrey study, published in *Lingua*, looked at "Asa" — a generative-AI receptionist that invites women to book cervical screening over WhatsApp — and found something every receptionist already knew: tone is the whole game. Patients warmed to language that was "friendly," "kind" and "not forceful," and that fitted their existing routines. But over-messaging, imperative phrasing and a blurred line between human and bot actively put people off, especially the vulnerable groups screening most needs to reach. It lands as UK cervical screening uptake has fallen 5.3% in a year, with ethnic-minority women persistently under-represented.

**Why it matters:** This is the rare AI paper that should change behaviour on Monday. We are bolting chatbots onto recall and screening invitations precisely because they nudge relentlessly — and the relentlessness is the bug, not the feature. A bot that hectors a hesitant woman into ignoring her smear invitation has done real harm with perfect efficiency. Whoever configures these systems is making a clinical decision about engagement, and should be held to a clinical standard.

## 3. A stethoscope that triages heart failure

Queen Mary and Barts Health have been awarded £1.3m by the NIHR's i4i programme to develop iHeF — an Intelligent Heart Evaluation Framework that reads an ECG-enabled stethoscope examination and flags who genuinely needs an echo for heart failure with reduced ejection fraction. The pitch is screening *out* as much as screening in: spare the worried-well a scan, fast-track the people whose hearts are actually failing, and unclog the diagnostic queue. Three years of testing and regulatory work means clinical use no earlier than late 2028.

**The view:** This is the shape of AI that helps a GP — cheap kit at the point of care, a clear triage question, and a number that tells you who to push up the list. The honest part is the timeline: £1.3m and three years just to *try* to prove it, which is what proper evaluation actually costs. Compare and contrast with the scribe market, where the same rigour arrived years after the product. If iHeF works it's a genuinely useful tool; the discipline of finding out first is the point.

## 4. Washington asks frontier labs for a sneak preview

Trump's Executive Order 14409, signed 2 June, sets out a notably hands-off federal approach: rather than binding rules, it asks developers of frontier AI to *voluntarily* give the government up to 30 days' early access to new models before wider release, alongside hardening federal cyber-defences and steering enforcement toward criminal misuse of AI. Concrete detail is promised within 60 days. The framing is explicit — America leads by refusing to "stifle innovation" with heavy regulation.

**Why it matters:** Put this beside Brussels delaying its AI Act and you can read the whole geopolitical weather in one frame: Europe writes hard law and then flinches at the deadline, while Washington declines to write it at all and asks nicely instead. "Voluntary 30-day preview" is the regulatory equivalent of marking your own homework with a teacher looking over your shoulder if you fancy it. For UK readers with neither an AI Act nor an executive order — just MHRA guidance — it's a reminder that "light touch" is a choice with consequences, not a neutral default.

## 5. The year the agents went to work

The enterprise mood has shifted, and the AI commentariat — Whittemore's *AI Daily Brief* among them — has clocked it: June 2026 is the month firms stopped asking whether AI "agents" are real and started asking which department gets automated first. Industry surveys now claim a majority of large companies have agents — systems with goals, memory and some autonomy to act, not just chat — running in production, handling support, admin, research and coding. Take the percentages with salt, but the direction is not in doubt.

**The view from the surgery:** The vendors selling "agentic AI" to hospitals are selling this same shift — software that doesn't just draft the letter but sends the referral, books the slot, chases the result. That's exactly where the medico-legal ground gets soft. An agent that *acts* has crossed from decision-support into something closer to practice, and the question "who is accountable when it acts wrongly" has no good answer yet. Useful for admin; treat anything touching a clinical decision as guilty until proven safe.

### From the Eye

Three the front pages didn't run. **Item one** — meet bixonimania, a disease that does not exist, confidently described to thousands of users by ChatGPT, Gemini, Copilot and Perplexity alike; it even surfaced as a citation in a peer-reviewed *Cureus* paper before the hoax was rumbled and the paper retracted. Confabulation doesn't just invent facts, it launders them into the literature. **Item two** — the MHRA, with NHS England (London) and the capital's health innovation networks, has quietly opened "London Region I," a regulatory sandbox to test AI-enabled medical devices in real-world NHS settings before full authorisation; sensible, under-reported, and the sort of plumbing that matters more than the press releases. **Item three** — buried in the bixonimania research is the detail that should chill every scribe enthusiast: hallucination rates *rise* when the input "looks professional and written as a doctor writes." The more clinical your prompt, the more confidently the machine makes things up — precisely the opposite of what you'd hope from a tool you're feeding consultation notes.
