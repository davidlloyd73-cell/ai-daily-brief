## 1. The biggest UK ambient-voice deal lands in South West London

The **South West London Acute Provider Collaborative** — St George's, Epsom and St Helier, Croydon Health Services, and Kingston and Richmond — has signed with **Lyrebird Health** to roll out ambient voice technology to **20,000 clinicians**, the largest UK AVT contract to date. Lyrebird is integrated with the trusts' shared **Oracle Cerner Millennium** EPR, so the scribe writes directly into the record rather than into a holding tablet. The plan is 10,000 clinicians in year one, scaling to the full 20,000 over four years.

**The view from the surgery:** Lyrebird against Heidi, Tortus and Nabla is one of the genuinely competitive segments left in the NHS AI market, and EPR integration is what makes the difference — anything that ends a consultation with a clinician still copy-pasting has missed the point. Worth watching how four boards reconcile clinical governance: one contract, four medical directors and a shared EPR is the arrangement that exposes whose DPIA is actually current.

## 2. NEJM AI: AI-standardised OSCE training beats the old way

Published today in *NEJM AI*, a two-site single-blind RCT of **247 early-clerkship students** finds **AI-standardised clinical examination training** outperforms standard OSCE prep — median score **11.4 vs 10.7** (P = 0.02), with absenteeism essentially gone (0.8% vs 4.9%). Postintervention surveys show better emotional readiness and lower stress; **74%** of users said the AI patient felt real, **67%** said the feedback felt like real teachers'.

**Why it matters:** The substitution effect worth watching isn't AI marking the OSCE — it's AI replacing the simulated patient. SP programmes are the costliest and most logistically fragile part of clinical examination training; if software can do it as well, the budget will move. A 0.7-point score effect does not redraw the map, but a four-percentage-point absenteeism drop and the cost differential together will. UK deaneries should be reading this paper today.

## 3. Anthropic closes $965bn — and ships Opus 4.8 the same day

**Anthropic** today confirmed a **$65bn** round at a **$965bn post-money valuation**, co-led by Altimeter, Dragoneer, Greenoaks and Sequoia — eclipsing OpenAI's $852bn for the first time. The lab paired the financing with the release of **Claude Opus 4.8**: SWE-bench Pro 69.2, fast mode three times cheaper than 4.7, and a "dynamic workflows" feature spinning up hundreds of parallel subagents. Anthropic also said it has built "stronger safety safeguards" that will let it ship **Mythos**-level cyber capability to all customers within weeks.

**The view:** The flip matters for procurement leverage, not bragging rights. The wider-release Mythos signal is the consequential bit — defensive cyber capability at frontier-model scale, available to ordinary customers, will move into baseline expectations for healthcare CISOs faster than the current contracts assume. Trusts renewing this summer should be asking what model floor the vendor will support by Q4.

## 4. Stanford brings patients into the AI procurement room

**STAT's AI Prognosis** reports that **Stanford Health Care** has formalised **patient panels** as part of how the system decides which AI tools it actually deploys — alert fatigue, missed diagnoses, overnight blood draws among them. One panellist is **Eric Gries**, who cared for his wife through LVAD and transplant; his job is to flag the workflow consequences clinicians miss because they're not on the receiving end.

**Why it matters:** PPI in the NHS is meant to do this already and frequently doesn't — packs go out, response rates are low, the procurement decision is already made. Stanford's setup is paid, structured and given veto-shaped influence. If NHS commissioners want a defensible answer to *who was in the room when this was bought*, the model is worth borrowing — particularly before the £900m framework below starts releasing call-offs.

## 5. FDA buys itself more time on AI-enabled trials

The **FDA** has extended the comment window on its **AI-enabled optimisation of early-phase clinical trials** pilot from 29 May to **29 June** — quietly, on 27 May, one day before the cutoff. The pilot, due to launch this summer, uses AI and cloud monitoring to read trial data in real time; the agency reckons it could "shave years" off approval timelines. Two proof-of-concept real-time trials are already running.

**The view from the surgery:** Two readings. The optimist: the regulator wants more input because it is taking the design seriously. The realist: extending a window the day before it closes is what regulators do when the responses so far don't add up. Either way, real-time AI trial monitoring would land in the UK via the MHRA's international-reliance pathway — operational consequences a couple of years off, but the architecture is being set now.

### From the Eye

**The £900m tender that quietly opened.** **NHS Shared Business Services** published its **Healthcare AI Solutions** framework on 11 May — eight lots, £900m including VAT, contract life to 2035 — with the **23 June** submission deadline now under four weeks out. It is the single biggest forward AI procurement vehicle the NHS has issued, covering radiology, predictive analytics, virtual and robotic health, and consultancy. The framework will route most trust-level AI spend for the next eight years; worth knowing who is bidding. **The Uber engineering ledger that detonated.** Uber's CTO confirmed this week the firm burned through **its entire 2026 AI coding tools budget in four months** — at six other enterprises analysed by the *AI Daily Brief*, the AI bill became the second-largest engineering line item inside ninety days. Agents do not consume tokens the way chatbots do; NHS scribe contracts now landing assume a usage profile that may not hold once agentic capability is switched on. **The ECRI top hazard isn't a device.** ECRI's 2026 **Health Tech Hazard Report** has **chatbot misuse** — ChatGPT, Gemini and Copilot used unwisely in healthcare settings — as the **single biggest health technology hazard** of the year, ahead of the usual infusion-pump and imaging-software entries. It is the first time an unregulated general-purpose tool has topped that list. Worth raising at the next significant-event meeting.
