# THE MORNING RUSH

*A short brief on AI in medicine — and the world it's moving through. Read it before the 8am phone lines open.*

**Wednesday 1 July 2026 · ~5 min read · Issue 063**

---

**On the line today:** The government and Eli Lilly put £85m into an obesity programme whose front door is an AI triage bot — sensible plumbing, awkward paymaster. A Lancet letter warns the celebrated mammography AI may be catching cancers that never needed catching. The radiologists' census finds AI booming everywhere except the admin that's actually driving people out. The medical defence bodies want the law to decide who carries the can when your scribe invents a line. And Brussels quietly waters down its own AI rulebook before most of it takes effect. From the Eye: deepfake doctors flogging shilajit, chatbots in the therapy room, and the vanishing bottom rung of the jobs ladder.

---

## 1. Lilly's £35m and the NHS's new front door for obesity

The government and Eli Lilly have put £85m — £50m public, £35m from Lilly — behind the Obesity Pathway Innovation Programme, and the technology is the headline. In Norfolk, Suffolk and north-east Essex, people worried about their weight will fill in an online health check that AI-triages them to a dietitian or a specialist clinic; in Kent, up to 3,300 families will get round-the-clock advice on eating, sleep and activity by WhatsApp. It runs to March 2029.

**The view from the surgery:** Two things at once. Triage-from-home is genuinely useful — much of what lands in my Monday list could have been sorted before it reached me. But look who's paying. Lilly makes tirzepatide, and a pathway part-funded by the manufacturer that ends in a prescribing decision is a conflict you can see from space, however clean the governance paperwork. Watch whether the AI front door quietly widens the funnel into the drugs its sponsor sells.

## 2. The Lancet letter that says the mammography AI may be finding too much

MASAI, the large Swedish randomised trial, showed AI-supported screen-reading lifting cancer detection (sensitivity 80.5% versus 73.8%) while roughly halving the radiologists' reading workload, and its authors concluded it "may be considered for implementation." A correspondence in *The Lancet* on 28 June pushes back: that recommendation rests on intermediate performance endpoints, and the extra cancers caught raise the old spectre of overdiagnosis — treating tumours that would never have troubled the patient. To be fair to MASAI, there was no rise in low-grade DCIS, which is where you'd expect the overdiagnosis to show.

**Why it matters:** The same lesson as this week's lung X-ray study, from the other end. More detected is not the same as more lives saved. Screening AI will always look handsome on a sensitivity slide; the number that actually matters — deaths prevented, minus the harm from overtreatment — takes years to read out, and nobody selling the software wants to wait for it.

## 3. AI everywhere in radiology except where it would actually help

New workforce census data from the Royal College of Radiologists land an awkward finding: AI adoption is racing ahead in diagnostics and cancer work, but it has barely touched the administrative grind — the letters, the coding, the chasing — that quietly eats reporting time. Meanwhile more than a third of departments froze radiologist hiring last year, close to double the year before.

**The view:** Familiar to any GP. We bought the clever kit for the interesting bit and left the drudgery — the thing that's actually driving people out of the job — completely untouched. If there's a real productivity dividend in AI, it's sitting in the admin, not in second-guessing a scan. That's where the ambient-scribe crowd should be pointing next, and mostly aren't.

---

## 4. The medico-legal hole under your scribe

Medical defence organisations are pressing the government to classify AI systems as products subject to strict liability, warning of an "AI liability gap." When an ambient scribe drops a negative, or confidently invents a line, and harm follows, it is genuinely unclear who carries the can — the vendor, the trust, or the doctor who signed off the note. The GMC's position hasn't shifted an inch: you remain responsible for any decision AI influences.

**Worth a phone call:** If you're running a scribe, ring your indemnifier and ask, in writing, whether that specific product is covered under your existing arrangement. "The AI got it wrong" is not a defence any UK court has yet tested, and you really don't want to be the case that tests it.

---

## 5. Brussels blinks on its own AI rulebook

The European Parliament has voted to soften the AI Act. The toughest obligations on high-risk systems — health among them — slip from this August to December 2027, and the lighter-touch regime now reaches firms of up to 750 staff. It did add teeth in one place, extending prohibitions to "nudifier" apps that fabricate intimate images without consent. Formal Council sign-off is expected by 2 August.

**The long view:** The world's flagship AI law, diluted before most of it ever bit. Read it as the regulatory mood everywhere just now — build first, write the rules later. For medical AI that cuts both ways: fewer hoops for a good tool to clear, but also fewer guarantees that the thing on your desktop was ever independently checked before it started drafting your notes.

### From the Eye

**Deepfake doctors** are having a moment nobody ordered — investigators have logged well over 100 AI-generated videos of "physicians," several cloned from real, named doctors who never consented, flogging shilajit and probiotics as Ozempic-beaters to menopausal women; the tools cost about $11 a month and the fakes pass a first glance, which is all a supplement scam needs. **In the therapy room**, the American Psychological Association's latest survey finds 77% of psychologists say their patients now report using AI, and Connecticut has become one of the first states to legally require chatbot operators to detect suicidal ideation and respond with real resources — regulation arriving, as ever, one tragedy behind the product. **The vanishing bottom rung**: fresh Dallas Fed and US labour figures show under-25s in the most AI-exposed occupations have seen employment fall about 13% since 2022, with entry-level postings down roughly a third — the grunt work that used to be how you learned a trade is exactly what the machines took first, and nobody has yet worked out where the next generation of seniors is meant to come from.
