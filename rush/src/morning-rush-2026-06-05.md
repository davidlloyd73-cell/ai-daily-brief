## 1. CMA forces Google to give UK publishers an AI opt-out — a world first

On **3 June** the **Competition and Markets Authority** announced it is ordering **Google** to allow UK publishers to opt out of having their content used in AI-generated search summaries, without losing their place in ordinary Google Search. Google has nine months to comply and must publish regular compliance reports. The order also gives publishers a separate switch to refuse training and fine-tuning use, and bars Google from down-ranking anyone who opts out. The CMA has framed it as the first conduct requirement under its new Strategic Market Status powers.

**Why it matters:** This is the first AI-specific competition remedy from a major Western regulator that actually has teeth — and the precedent travels. Medical publishers (BMJ, The Lancet, Nature, the royal colleges) are quietly the most exposed: their content is what GPs and patients land on when they ask Google or Gemini a clinical question. Watch over the next quarter to see whether *they* opt out, opt in, or strike paid licensing deals. The architecture being built here is also what will eventually arbitrate whether NHS-authored clinical guidance can be hoovered into private foundation models without consent.

## 2. James Murray becomes data controller for the Single Patient Record

In the Commons on **1 June** — and reiterated in detail on **3 June** — new health secretary **James Murray** confirmed that, under the Health Bill currently in committee, the Secretary of State will become the formal **data controller** for all NHS information shared through the planned Single Patient Record. GP surgeries and trusts retain control of their own source records, but the moment data flows into the SPR, the controller is the politician. Murray promised "the highest levels of security" and an audit trail; the **BMA** has already lodged its concern.

**The view from the surgery:** Read alongside Wednesday's *Register* piece, this is not a procurement story — it is a constitutional one. The accountable party for a leak, a Subject Access Request, or a hostile FoI now sits in Whitehall rather than at the practice door, which on paper helps GPs. The harder question is what happens at the next election: a database of 65 million people that changes controller every time the cabinet reshuffles is not a stable governance model, and the indemnity actuaries at **MDU** and **MPS** are already pricing the volatility.

## 3. OpenAI fuses GPT-5.5 into GPT-Rosalind for life sciences and biodefence

**OpenAI** on **4 June** announced a major upgrade to **GPT-Rosalind**, the life-sciences model launched in April. The new version embeds **GPT-5.5**'s agentic coding and tool-use, posts a 21.6% accuracy on the **GeneBench** end-to-end genomics benchmark while using 31% fewer tokens than base GPT-5.5, and ships plugins for evidence retrieval and bioinformatics workflows. The bigger move is access: OpenAI is sponsoring vetted developers and extending access to "select US government and allied public-health partners" for **biodefence** work — outbreak modelling, diagnostics, and medical countermeasure design.

**The view:** The dual-use framing is the bit to watch. A model good enough to accelerate drug discovery is also good enough to accelerate something nastier, and OpenAI has chosen to court the **BARDA**/**Wellcome Leap**/**UK Biosecurity** axis explicitly. For UK readers, the question is whether **UKHSA** and the **Office for Life Sciences** end up inside the access ring — or whether sovereign capability work shifts to DeepMind and a future NHS-owned model along the Mayo lines. The lobbying on this in Whitehall over the next three months will be intense and almost entirely below the waterline.

## 4. NHS Online: ex-Tesco chief John Browett takes the chair

The new **NHS Online** trust — the "virtual hospital" that will deliver app-based specialist consultations once the Health Bill passes — has named its first chair: **John Browett**, the former boss of Dixons, Tesco and (briefly) Apple Retail. The announcement on **3 June** confirmed Browett will oversee a service delivered "through the NHS App and video consultations", with the AI workforce plan to be published later in June reportedly contemplating roles that AI can "completely substitute" — language the **BMA** has already pushed back on.

**Why it matters:** A retail-and-logistics chair signals the operating model — high-volume, app-first, KPI-driven. The substantive question for primary care is whether NHS Online absorbs the simple-condition presentations that currently fill morning surgery (UTIs, pill checks, repeat sick notes) or whether it competes for them. The former relieves general practice; the latter cannibalises the funded list. The detail will live in the contract specification, not the press release.

## 5. EU Code of Practice on AI-content labelling lands this month

The European Commission has confirmed its **Code of Practice on marking and labelling AI-generated content** will publish in **June 2026**, six weeks before the **AI Act** becomes fully applicable on **2 August**. The Code is voluntary but will set the default expectation for watermarking, provenance metadata and disclosure obligations on every generative AI provider operating in the EU. The Commission has also stood up a 60-expert **Scientific Panel** for cross-border enforcement and a separate **Advisory Forum**.

**The view from the surgery:** The clinical limb of this is small but real. AI-generated patient information leaflets, AI-drafted referral letters and AI-summarised consultation notes are all in scope once Article 50 transparency rules bite in August. Expect your ICB to circulate a notice on documentation labelling before the summer recess — the boring kind of compliance email that turns into a Datix incident six months later if ignored.

### From the Eye

Three the front pages didn't run. **Item one** — **Phil Hammond**'s **MD column** in the *Private Eye* issue out next Thursday delivers the only honest scorecard on **Wes Streeting**'s tenure: **677 days** in post against a career average of **820**, inheriting a service in dire shape and leaving it with the Health Bill drafted but not enacted. The interesting line is that Streeting departed citing **Starmer** rather than NHS performance — confirmation that the political horsepower behind NHS reform sits somewhere else now, which matters for every AI procurement decision **James Murray** has to underwrite over the next twelve months. **Item two** — **Maryland**'s **HB 1563**, in force from **1 June**, now requires every health insurer using AI in claims adjudication to file **quarterly reports** to the state Insurance Commissioner listing the number of adverse decisions. It is the first US disclosure regime that actually generates auditable numbers rather than vibes — and it is the framework the **AMA** and **BMA**-equivalents will lift wholesale for prior-authorisation cases here within eighteen months. **Item three** — Brussels has appointed **Jim Hagemann Snabe**, ex-chair of Siemens and Maersk, as the EU's first **Special Envoy for Industrial AI**, alongside the new **Cloud and AI Development Act (CADA)**. The under-reported wrinkle: CADA explicitly targets healthcare data sovereignty, and the directional signal is that any future European foundation model trained on EU clinical data will need an EU-jurisdiction compute home. The UK's post-Brexit position on this — neither in nor fully out of the regime — is the bit nobody at DHSC seems willing to articulate.
