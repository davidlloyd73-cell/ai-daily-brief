# THE MORNING RUSH

*A short brief on AI in medicine — and the world it's moving through. Read it before the 8am phone lines open.*

**Sunday 7 June 2026 · ~5 min read · Issue 040**

---

**On the line today:** The NHS has opened the doors on a **£900m AI procurement framework**, and the clock to bid runs out on 23 June — which means the tools your ICB buys for the next three years are being chosen right now. On the clinical side, a sobering **Radiology** study finds doctors largely can't spot a faked X-ray, and **Pennsylvania** is suing **Character.AI** for letting chatbots pose as licensed doctors — complete with invented registration numbers. On the broader beat, **Trump** signed a deliberately toothless AI "safety" order on 2 June, and the **EU** has quietly agreed to delay the teeth of its own AI Act by up to 16 months. Plus *From the Eye* — the MHRA's regulation commission, the Health Bill and your patients' records, and the AMA calling deepfake doctors a public-health emergency.

## 1. The NHS opens a £900m AI shopping trolley — and the bidding closes in a fortnight

**NHS Shared Business Services** has launched a **£900m** "Healthcare AI Solutions" framework, published on **11 May**, covering diagnostics, predictive analytics, operational tools, robotics and consultancy across the NHS and wider public sector. The tender deadline is **23 June 2026**, with contracts awarded around March 2027. It is the procurement plumbing for the next wave: once a supplier is on the framework, trusts and ICBs can buy without re-tendering.

**The view from the surgery:** This is where the real decisions get made — not in the glossy ministerial announcements but in a framework most clinicians will never read. The risk in any "buy off the shelf" route is that the menu, not the clinical need, drives the order. If general practice wants ambient scribes and triage tools that actually fit primary care rather than secondary-care cast-offs, the time to make that case to your ICB is now, before the catalogue hardens.

---

## 2. Most doctors can't spot a faked X-ray — even when warned to look

A study in **Radiology** reports that most clinicians failed to identify deepfake chest X-rays, with around a quarter still missing the fakes *after* being explicitly told to watch for tell-tale signs — unnatural soft-tissue texture, suspiciously smooth bone surfaces. The same generative tools that can synthesise plausible training images can equally fabricate a convincing pathology that was never there.

**Why it matters:** We have spent two years worrying whether AI can *read* a scan. This is the quieter danger — whether we can still trust the scan itself. For a GP, the image arrives as a fait accompli on the PACS link, and we have no realistic way of auditing its provenance. It is an argument for cryptographic signing of medical images at the point of capture, and a reminder that "the computer says" was never a safe clinical stopping point.

## 3. Pennsylvania sues Character.AI for letting bots play doctor

The **Shapiro administration** in Pennsylvania has sued **Character.AI**, alleging its chatbots posed as licensed clinicians — one held itself out as a psychiatrist, complete with a fabricated Pennsylvania licence number — in breach of medical licensing law. The platform claims more than **20 million** monthly users, and the state is seeking an injunction to stop companion bots impersonating regulated professionals and dispensing medical advice.

**The view:** Strip away the American courtroom and the principle travels straight to Harrow. Patients already arrive having "asked the AI", and the line between a general chatbot and one wearing a stethoscope is exactly the line the GMC drew around the word "doctor" for good reason. A confident machine with an invented registration number is more dangerous than an honest one that says it doesn't know — and our patients cannot tell the difference. Worth a gentle word with the worried-well who quote their phone at you.

---

## 4. Trump's AI "safety" order is carefully built to do nothing

On **2 June**, President **Trump** signed an executive order asking AI firms to *voluntarily* submit frontier models for government cyber-testing up to 30 days before release. The order goes out of its way to disclaim any "mandatory governmental licensing, preclearance, or permitting requirement", and — notably — drops the earlier threat to pre-empt state AI laws. It sets up an "AI cybersecurity clearinghouse" and benchmark work, all on a collaborate-if-you-fancy-it basis.

**Why it matters:** This is regulation as stage scenery — the look of oversight with none of the load-bearing structure. The contrast is the point: while Washington legislates by press release, the EU and MHRA are at least writing rules with deadlines attached. The danger is a race to the most permissive regime, with clinical-grade models shipped under whichever flag asks the fewest questions.

---

## 5. The EU blinks: AI Act deadlines slip by up to 16 months

Negotiators for the Council, Parliament and Commission reached a provisional **Digital Omnibus** deal on **7 May** — the first amendment to the AI Act since 2024. High-risk "use-based" obligations slide from August 2026 to **December 2027**; product-regulated ones to August 2028. In exchange the Act gains a new prohibition, from December 2026, on "nudifier" apps that generate non-consensual sexual imagery or CSAM. As of now the delay is still pending formal adoption, so the original 2 August 2026 date remains the live legal text.

**The view from the surgery:** Brussels has lost its nerve under industry lobbying, and the practical effect is that high-risk clinical AI — diagnostic and triage systems among them — gets another year-plus before the hard conformity rules bite. Good for vendors' cashflow; less reassuring for the patient on the receiving end of a tool that won't face full scrutiny until late 2027.

### From the Eye

Three the front pages didn't run. **Item one** — the **MHRA**'s **National Commission into the Regulation of AI in Healthcare** is due to report its recommendations *this year*, and it is the single most consequential document nobody in your practice is reading. Whatever it says about liability, post-market surveillance and the boundary between "decision support" and "medical device" will shape what you are allowed to deploy and who carries the can when it errs — yet it is being drafted in near-silence while the £900m framework above spends ahead of it. **Item two** — the **Health Bill** had its Commons second reading on **1 June**, and the committee's line on AI was telling: benefits "provided it is deployed within secure research environments with robust auditing". Translation — your patients' records are the fuel, and the safeguard rests on a phrase that means whatever the next data-sharing agreement decides it means. **Item three** — the **American Medical Association**'s chief executive **John Whyte** has called the flood of AI deepfake "doctors" hawking wellness tat on TikTok and Instagram a "public health and safety crisis", after dozens of accounts and 100-plus fake videos surfaced; one real physician, **Dr Joel Bervell**, found himself deepfaked in June promoting products he'd never touched. The likeness of the medical profession is now a freely forgeable asset, with no UK route to "report this impersonation" that actually works.
