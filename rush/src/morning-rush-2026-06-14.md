## 1. The public's verdict on health AI: help the doctor, don't replace them

The MHRA published two reports on 11 June summarising the biggest engagement exercise it's run on AI in healthcare — a call for evidence drawing 760 submissions from patients, the public, clinicians and industry. The headline is reassuringly sane: support is real but conditional, hinged on transparency, accountability and human oversight, and people are far more comfortable with AI lightening the admin load than making high-stakes calls on its own. The findings feed the National Commission into the Regulation of AI in Healthcare, whose recommendations are due later this year. The day before, the regulator opened "London Region I," a sandbox letting AI medical devices be deployed in live NHS settings under close supervision.

**The view from the surgery:** This is the public telling us, politely, exactly where the line sits — and it's where most of us would draw it too. "Assist, don't decide" is a decent design brief for the whole sector. The sandbox matters more than the reports, mind: rules written in a consultation room are theory; rules written from watching a device misfire on a real London patient are evidence.

## 2. Washington pulls the plug on Anthropic's best models

On 12 June the US government handed Anthropic an export-control directive ordering it to suspend access to its two most capable models, Fable 5 and Mythos 5, for any foreign national "whether inside or outside the United States," citing national security. Unable to reliably tell foreign users from domestic ones in real time, Anthropic simply disabled both models for everyone — a frontier lab dark-firing its flagship overnight to comply. The firm's read is that officials believe someone found a way to jailbroke Fable 5; it's calling the whole thing a misunderstanding and racing to restore service.

**Why it matters:** Strip out the spy-thriller framing and this is a glimpse of the new weather. The most powerful medical-reasoning tools coming our way sit on models a single government letter can switch off without notice or appeal. Sovereignty isn't an abstraction when your decision-support tool goes dark because of an export row you had no part in. Worth remembering before the NHS leans its weight on any one American frontier model.

## 3. The deepfake doctors selling cures that don't exist

A swelling trade in AI-generated "doctors" is pushing scam health products across social media — fabricated clinicians in white coats hawking miracle fixes for diabetes, weight and chronic disease. Real physicians are finding their own faces and names cloned without consent: one US paediatric specialist discovered an AI video of himself flogging vitamin supplements he'd never touched. Detection is now its own arms race, with fraud-analytics firms building tools to spot synthetic medical claims and fake clinical documentation before money or trust changes hands.

**The view:** This is the misinformation problem with a stethoscope on. Our patients increasingly arrive having "seen a doctor explain it online," and that doctor may be a convincing fiction built to sell them something useless or harmful. The countermeasure isn't technical, it's relational — the trusted GP who'll tell them, kindly, that the man in the video isn't real and the cure isn't either.

---

## 4. Who carries the can? The medico-legal warning gets louder

The Medical Protection Society has warned that doctors risk becoming a "liability sink" — the place blame settles when a clinical AI tool errs — unless UK law is reformed to share responsibility across developers, deployers and clinicians. As scribes, triage models and decision-support tools multiply across the NHS, the legal architecture underneath them hasn't moved: sign the note, own the note, including whatever the machine quietly invented in it.

**Why it matters:** This is the question that should keep every practice manager awake, and it's been criminally under-discussed amid the productivity cheerleading. We're being asked to adopt tools at speed while bearing personal medico-legal risk for their failures, with no settled doctrine on apportioning fault. Anyone who's lived through indemnity reform knows the law arrives years after the technology — and the gap is where careers get ruined.

---

## 5. The money taps open — and health AI is downstream of it

SpaceX, having folded xAI into the group, floated on Nasdaq on 12 June and closed up nearly 20% on debut — the year's marquee AI-adjacent listing. Both OpenAI and Anthropic have now confirmed confidential IPO filings, and a successful SpaceX debut is widely read as clearing the runway for their own mega-listings as they scramble for the colossal capital their ambitions demand.

**The view:** This feels remote from a Harrow surgery, but it isn't. The tools arriving on our desktops are built by firms now answerable to public shareholders and quarterly numbers — which sharpens every incentive to expand into healthcare's vast, sticky, recurring revenue. When your scribe vendor has an earnings call, "more features" and "more data captured" stop being clinical decisions and start being financial ones. Follow the money; it's heading our way.

### From the Eye

Three the front pages didn't run. **Item one** — a London patient was invited to diabetic screening after an AI tool, Anima Health's "Annie," fabricated a diabetes diagnosis and suspected heart disease in his record, complete with an invented "Health Hospital" at "456 Care Road, Health City" and a made-up postcode. Comic until you realise it reached a real person on a real recall list — the hallucination didn't stay in the chatbot, it walked into the pathway. **Item two** — the cross-party Commons Science and Technology Committee has urged ministers to use a February 2027 break clause to exit Palantir's £330m NHS Federated Data Platform contract, with Amnesty backing the call and some NHS staff already boycotting the software. The biggest data deal in the NHS may be unpicked not on technical grounds but political ones. **Item three** — when OpenAI rolled its new "Library" feature into Healthcare workspaces this week, it shipped with automatic cross-referencing switched *off* by default. Read that again: the company building the tool doesn't fully trust it to connect clinical documents unsupervised. When the vendor hedges, so should you.
