# THE MORNING RUSH

*A short brief on AI in medicine — and the world it's moving through. Read it before the 8am phone lines open.*

**Sunday 28 June 2026 · ~5 min read · Issue 060**

**On the line today:** The most awkward paper of the month says the general-purpose chatbots now beat the purpose-built, FDA-cleared clinical tools at answering doctors' real questions — which rather undercuts the whole idea of a regulated "medical AI". The MHRA wants every GP to Yellow Card their scribe's fabrications, and so far almost nobody has. In the States, Medicare's AI prior-authorisation experiment has earned its contractor a formal slap from the federal government for leaving seniors waiting weeks. On the wider stage, PwC says AI is splitting the labour market into two tracks, and Azeem Azhar's first proper tally puts the generative-AI economy at $110bn of actual sales. From the Eye: a patient who out-diagnosed her doctors with ChatGPT, the chip deal nobody's watching, and the AI gap opening between rich and poor hospitals.

---

## 1. The "medical" AI loses to the general one

A study published this month in *Nature Medicine* pitted three general-purpose frontier models — GPT-5.2, Gemini 3.1 Pro and Claude Opus 4.6 — against the dedicated clinical products doctors are told to trust, OpenEvidence and UpToDate's Expert AI. Across three benchmarks, including a set of 100 de-identified real questions physicians had actually put to a chatbot in a live clinical setting, the general models won every round. On the MedQA knowledge test Gemini scored 97.4% and GPT 94.2%, against 89.6% for OpenEvidence and 88.4% for UpToDate. Clinicians also preferred the generalists' answers.

**Why it matters:** This is genuinely uncomfortable for the regulatory model we are building everything on. We are constructing supplier registries, DTAC assessments and MHRA device classifications around the premise that "clinical AI" is a distinct, validated category — and a decent study has just found the unregulated consumer chatbot answers the doctor's question better. It does not mean we should prescribe off ChatGPT. It does mean the validation gap runs the opposite way to the one we assumed, and the people writing the rules should read it twice.

## 2. The MHRA wants your scribe's mistakes on a Yellow Card

The regulator has asked GPs to report every inaccuracy or fabrication produced by an AI ambient scribe through the Yellow Card scheme — the same route we use for adverse drug reactions and dodgy devices. The RCGP has acknowledged that scribes can invent details, misattribute who said what, flip a negative into a positive, and stumble over the full range of UK accents. The wrinkle: a search of the Yellow Card database turned up essentially no AI-scribe reports at all, despite the tools now being in widespread daily use across general practice.

**The view from the surgery:** As a daily user of the technology, I'd say that absence of reports tells you nothing reassuring. It tells you we are all quietly correcting the howlers in the note and moving on to the next patient, because Yellow Carding a hallucinated chest exam at 6pm on a full list is nobody's priority. But the scheme only works if the signal reaches it. If we don't report the fabrications, the MHRA's evidence base stays empty — and "no adverse incidents recorded" becomes the line used to wave the next product through.

## 3. Medicare's AI gatekeeper gets a formal telling-off

In the United States, the Centers for Medicare and Medicaid Services has ordered Virtix Health — the Arizona firm running AI prior-authorisation reviews under the grimly named WISeR ("Wasteful and Inappropriate Service Reduction") model — to submit a corrective action plan for failing to process claims fast enough. Procedures that were previously authorised in about a fortnight are now taking four to eight weeks, according to the Washington State Hospital Association, with doctors and hospitals reporting technical failures and seniors left waiting for care.

**The view:** This is the future the prior-authorisation enthusiasts don't advertise. Strip out the AI branding and it's an automated denial-and-delay machine bolted onto public healthcare, and the first measurable result is that it's slower than the humans it replaced. For a UK reader the lesson isn't smugness — it's that "AI will cut bureaucracy" and "AI will ration access" are frequently the same project wearing different press releases.

## 4. PwC: AI is splitting the jobs market in two

PwC's 2026 Global AI Jobs Barometer argues the labour market is forking into two tracks. "Professionalised" roles — those that lean on judgement, leadership and skills AI complements rather than replaces — are seeing roughly twice the growth in available jobs and 42% faster wage growth than "democratised" roles where AI does more of the task. The firms best at deploying AI are hiring *faster* than their peers, not slower, which complicates the simple "robots take the jobs" story.

**Why it matters:** Medicine sits squarely on the professionalised side of that line, for now — the judgement, the examination, the sitting with uncertainty are exactly what the models complement rather than do. But the same logic should worry us about the support roles around the clinician: coding, summarising, triage admin, first-line call handling. Those are the "democratised" tasks, and they are the jobs that have long made a practice or an out-of-hours service actually function.

## 5. The AI economy, finally counted

Azeem Azhar's Exponential View has published what it calls the first bottom-up, de-duplicated measure of the generative-AI economy: $110bn in real customer sales over the past twelve months, on an annualised run-rate now north of $175bn. Built from spending data across more than 1,000 companies and adjusted to strip out the double-counting that inflates most estimates, the report's framing is deliberately double-edged — bigger and faster-growing than any prior technology wave, yet still small enough that we are early.

**The view:** Useful ballast against both the boosters and the bubble-callers. $110bn is a real economy, not vapour — but it's also a fraction of the capital being poured into the data centres meant to serve it, which is the gap the sceptics keep pointing at. For health, the relevant number isn't the headline total; it's how little of that $110bn yet comes from anything that has improved a single patient's outcome rather than a clinician's paperwork.

### From the Eye

**Item one** — the patient who out-diagnosed the doctors: the *Washington Post* reports a woman who fed sixteen years of meticulously kept medical records into ChatGPT, which flagged a different condition from the one her clinicians had settled on — and that reframing is what won her a place in a clinical trial she'd otherwise have missed. File under "uncomfortable anecdotes that will become a trend". **Item two** — the chip deal under the radar: while everyone watches Nvidia, Qualcomm is reportedly in early talks to buy Tenstorrent for $8–10bn, a bet on the open RISC-V standard as the cheaper foundation for AI hardware — the unglamorous plumbing that decides what the models cost to run. **Item three** — the gap nobody's funding: clinicians at this month's HIMSS forum warned of a widening "digital divide" between resource-rich systems experimenting freely with AI and the safety-net and community providers who can't afford to — the quiet story being that AI may not level healthcare so much as harden the league table.
