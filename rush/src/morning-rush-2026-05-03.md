## 1. Harvard tests an OpenAI reasoning model on real ED cases — and the model wins

Researchers at Harvard Medical School and Beth Israel Deaconess Medical Center, writing up a real-world evaluation reported by NPR on **30 April**, ran an OpenAI reasoning model against two experienced emergency physicians on a panel of actual ED presentations — including the lupus case familiar to BIDMC's morbidity rounds. **Working only from the same electronic-record text the doctors had at the time, the model matched and often outperformed both clinicians, and beat GPT-4 on the same cases.** The authors explicitly say the result does not support "supplanting doctors with AI", noting the model never saw the images, sounds and nonverbal cues a clinician triages on.

**The view from the surgery:** The caveat is correct and it is also the thing the headline writers will not be reproducing. What this study really measures is the gap between "the doctor's working note plus their thinking time" and "the same note read by a tireless reasoner". For triage, differential generation and bedside cognitive load, that gap is now uncomfortably large — and is the reason any honest GP doing ten-minute appointments has felt the consultation change in the last twelve months.

## 2. Frimley Health pauses Epic's AI Charting after the regulator-shaped hole shows up

HSJ reported this week that **Frimley Health Foundation Trust has halted a planned trial of Epic Systems' native AI Charting tool because the product holds neither MHRA Class I medical-device status nor a place on NHSE's Ambient Voice Technology supplier registry** — both required under March's NHSE guidance for any AI scribe doing summarisation. Frimley says the trial had not cleared its own internal governance and is paused pending further work. Awkwardly, two other trusts are already live on Epic's AVT, and a queue including UCLH, Birmingham Women's & Children's, the Royal National Orthopaedic and East Suffolk & North Essex is in conversations.

**Why it matters:** This is the first visible test of whether the new accreditation regime is binding or aspirational. A mid-sized trust noticing a major US supplier is shipping into the NHS without the paperwork is the right kind of friction; it also shows the friction depends on someone reading the small print. ICBs procuring AVT this quarter should assume the registry is now a hard gate, not a courtesy line.

## 3. Palantir's "AI director" is going to sit in on the top NHSE/DHSC meetings

HSJ has confirmed Palantir is being commissioned to deliver an AI decision-support tool to the joint NHSE/DHSC top team — internally referred to as **"Milly"**, in honour of lead non-exec director and former Health Secretary Alan Milburn. The tool is being trained on an evidence base of "successful health policy interventions" curated by **McKinsey** and is intended to run in the background of senior meetings, flagging where decisions are likely to clash with previous policy or produce poor outcomes. The published guard-rail is that Peter Thiel's personal views on the NHS are excluded from the corpus.

**The view:** This is what live AI policy advice looks like in the wild — a US data firm's product, trained on a US consultancy's curation, sitting next to the people deciding next year's primary-care contract. The Milburn naming will please some of the older readers; the McKinsey training set should worry everyone. A tool intelligible enough to flag bad ideas is also a tool that quietly defines what "good" means.

## 4. *The Lancet* calls human-in-the-loop oversight "symbolic reassurance"

A commentary published in *The Lancet* on **30 April** argues that human-in-the-loop oversight — the regulatory comfort-blanket cited in nearly every recent NHS, FDA and EU AI document — **functions more as symbolic reassurance than substantive protection**. The authors argue clinicians operate under time and cognitive constraints that preclude meaningful interrogation of algorithmic outputs, that intersectional harms elude oversight models designed around individual risk, and that AI in healthcare can amplify existing structural inequities at unprecedented scale.

**The view from the surgery:** This is the paper that turns a year of half-articulated worries into a citable position. "Validate the output" is the line in NHSE's March guidance, the GMC framework and every indemnity letter — but the real consultation does not contain the spare 30 seconds that line assumes. Worth printing for the next LMC meeting, and for any commissioner who still talks about "the human check" as if it were a working piece of safety engineering.

## 5. The "automation trap" — Wharton and BU put a number on the AI labour-cut feedback loop

A Wharton / Boston University paper by **Falk and Tsoukalas, circulated 29 April**, argues firms are caught in a self-defeating "automation arms race": each company sees savings from cutting headcount, but the demand collapse from a shrinking wage bill is shared across the whole economy. **Q1 2026 tech layoffs hit roughly 78,000, with around 48% explicitly attributed to AI; Meta has now signalled an 8,000-job cut beginning 20 May**; Glassdoor's tech-sector confidence index sits at 47.2%. The authors' suggested fix is a Pigouvian "automation tax".

**Why it matters:** The mechanics matter for NHS budgeting too. Tax receipts and the consultancy-led "AI productivity" lines cited in the upcoming spending review depend on the labour share holding. If the Wharton/BU model is even half right, the next spending round's "AI dividend" is partly imaginary — savings real for the firm cutting, partly absorbed by everyone else who suddenly has fewer customers.

### From the Eye

Three things humming under the front pages. **NHS England has issued guidance note SDLC-8** — quietly dated 29 April, broken by *New Scientist* this week — instructing teams to close most of NHSE's public open-source code repositories from **11 May**, citing AI-driven security-scanning risk; neither the NCSC nor the AI Safety Institute recommended this, and NHSE's own audit notes most of the affected repos are research tools, datasets and design code, not production secrets. **Stanford HAI's 2026 AI Index**, published in mid-April, contains the year's tidiest jagged-frontier line: a frontier model has just won a gold at the International Mathematical Olympiad, and the same class of model reads an analog clock correctly **50.1% of the time** — a coin-flip. **And on World Press Freedom Day on 3 May**, the US fell to **64th out of 180** on the World Press Freedom Index, its lowest ever; the data, AI-curated content moderation and platform-level reach decisions sitting behind that ranking are arguably the policy story of the year, even if the AI Index does not log them.
