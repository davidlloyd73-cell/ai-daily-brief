## 1. Ontario opens the bonnet on the scribes — and it isn't pretty

Ontario's auditor general, Shelley Spence, has published the first serious independent audit of the ambient AI scribes already in routine clinical use — roughly **5,000 physicians** across the province rely on them. Of twenty systems evaluated, **nine hallucinated**: inventing treatment suggestions, referrals and blood tests the doctor never made. Twelve recorded a different drug from the one prescribed. Seventeen missed material mental-health detail that was clearly present in the simulated encounter. Most damningly, accuracy of the generated note counted for just **4%** of the procurement score — "domestic presence in Ontario" was weighted at 30% — and vendors were never asked to run their tools live in front of evaluators.

**The view from the surgery:** Read this next to yesterday's news of South West London switching 10,000 clinicians onto Lyrebird. The technology is being bought at scale on convenience and price while accuracy sits at the bottom of the scorecard. The fix is unglamorous and entirely on us: read the note before you sign it, every time. A scribe that invents a referral isn't a productivity tool, it's a medico-legal liability with a friendly interface.

## 2. Do the "AI co-scientist" tools actually work?

STAT's *AI Prognosis* this week trains a sceptical eye on the "AI co-scientist" — the agentic tools now pitched to research groups as tireless generators of hypotheses, literature syntheses and experimental designs. The piece's honest answer is: not yet, not reliably. The tools are good at producing plausible, well-referenced-looking output and much weaker at the thing that matters — knowing which of a hundred plausible ideas is worth a bench's time and money. The risk is a flood of confident, untested hypotheses that costs more to triage than it saves.

**Why it matters:** This is the differential-diagnosis problem in a lab coat. Generating candidates is cheap; judgement about which candidate deserves scarce resource is the actual work, and it is precisely what these systems fake most convincingly. Worth holding in mind whenever a vendor promises to "accelerate" anything — acceleration without discrimination just gets you to the wrong place faster.

## 3. The quiet AI that actually triages — DERM hits 26 trusts

While the headlines chase scribes and chatbots, Skin Analytics' **DERM** has become one of the most-deployed clinical AI tools in the NHS, now live in **26 trusts**. Bradford Teaching Hospitals is the latest, running it at St Luke's to triage skin-lesion referrals: of around 5,000 referrals a year, only about 8% turn out to be malignant, and DERM is used to rule the clear-benign images out faster and push the suspicious ones up the queue. The company reports 99.7% accuracy at ruling out cancer — a vendor figure, but the regulatory pathway here is a genuine medical device, not a wrapper around a chatbot.

**The view:** This is what useful clinical AI looks like — narrow, validated, sitting inside an existing pathway with a human at the decision point, doing the dull sorting that frees a consultant for the hard 8%. It will never trend on X. It is also, quietly, far closer to the thing that helps a two-week-wait list than any general-purpose model currently being floated for "diagnosis".

## 4. The AI IPO race goes live — and the cheap-model trap underneath it

It all landed on one day. **OpenAI** filed its confidential S-1 with the SEC on Friday, targeting a September listing above $850bn. **Anthropic** told investors it expects its first-ever quarterly operating profit — roughly $559m on $10.9bn of Q2 revenue — a milestone nobody expected before 2028. But the sharper story is CNBC's: cheap, near-frontier models are eroding the pricing power those valuations depend on. On the developer platform OpenRouter, Chinese models have gone from ~1% of usage in 2024 to **over 60%** today; on one benchmark suite Claude costs around nine times more than Zhipu's GLM to run the same workload.

**The view from the surgery:** This is not finance-page trivia for the NHS — it is procurement strategy. The £900m AI framework opened this month; the live question for any trust or PCN is whether you are paying frontier prices for "good enough" tasks. The honest answer for most documentation and triage work is that a far cheaper model would do, and the market is about to make that impossible to ignore.

## 5. Illinois writes the rulebook while Whitehall waits

Illinois' Senate has passed an **AI model-safety bill 52–5**, requiring large developers to publish a transparency framework, use third-party auditors and disclose a model's catastrophic-risk capabilities. It is deliberately modelled on the laws California and New York passed last year — three states now converging on what is becoming a **de-facto national US standard**, with OpenAI and Anthropic both testifying in support. Meanwhile the White House's own voluntary pre-launch review order was postponed again this week, and the UK's National Commission into the Regulation of AI in Healthcare isn't due to report until the summer.

**Why it matters:** The centre of gravity for AI rules in America has moved to the states, and industry is quietly helping draft them — a national floor by the back door. The UK's bet is still light-touch and bespoke-to-healthcare, which looks pragmatic today and increasingly lonely if Brussels and a bloc of US states both land harder regimes first. Anything sold into the NHS that also touches those markets will end up complying with the stricter one by default.

### From the Eye

Three things beneath the front pages. **A GP's verdict on the £900m framework** — Marcus Baw, GP and clinical-safety officer, told *Digital Health* the new NHS AI procurement route is "free money for mega corporations at a time when medicine is rapidly becoming an economically unviable occupation," a line worth more than the press release it punctures. **A chatbot psychiatrist in court** — Pennsylvania is suing Character.AI after an investigator found a bot called "Dr Emilie," styled as a doctor of psychiatry and logged with some 45,500 user interactions, offering to book a mental-health assessment and claiming it was authorised to prescribe; the unauthorised-practice-of-medicine question is now live, and the UK has no equivalent test case. And **the number nobody mentions in the demo** — SpaceX's prospectus reveals Anthropic is paying it about **$1.25bn a month** for compute, roughly $45bn through 2029. When a vendor calls a pilot "efficient," that is the cost structure quietly sitting behind the word.
