*A short brief on AI in medicine — and the world it's moving through. Read it before the 8am phone lines open.*

**On the line today:** The headline number from London Tech Week is a big one — 505,000 NHS staff to get Microsoft Copilot by October, the largest healthcare AI rollout anywhere. But the mood music underneath is more sober: the woman leading the national scribe evaluation says users are already disappointed, and a new report wants proper oversight before the tools metastasise. Brussels, meanwhile, has blinked and pushed its high-risk AI rules out to 2027. Plus *From the Eye* — a chatbot posing as a psychiatrist, a hazard report listing AI's invented body parts, and a quiet consensus forming behind pausing the whole thing.

## 1. NHS bets big: Copilot for half a million staff by October

NHS England confirmed at London Tech Week that it will put Microsoft 365 Copilot on the desks of 505,000 clinicians and support staff by October — described, plausibly, as the largest healthcare AI deployment in the world. The £120m programme rolls out from July in phases, with each trust required to show readiness first: a named AI lead and certified training for at least a fifth of staff before licences arrive. NHS England points to pilots that saved clinicians an average of 47 minutes a day, and has ring-fenced £15m for an independent Oxford evaluation, with interim findings due before year's end.

**The view from the surgery:** Forty-seven minutes a day is the sort of number that sells a business case, and admin relief is the right place to start — low clinical risk, high tedium. But "largest in the world" should make us cautious, not proud. We are buying infrastructure from a single American vendor at colossal scale, and the readiness gates (an AI lead, 20% trained) are thin armour for a tool that will end up touching every letter, referral and note. The Oxford evaluation is the part to watch; everything else is procurement.

## 2. The scribe honeymoon is over

As the documentation gold rush rolls on, Professor Kathrin Cresswell — lead on the national NHS evaluation of ambient scribes — warns that "heightened expectations" around the technology can be "very damaging," and that some users are already disappointed, having expected far too much. A parallel report this month argues the scribe rollout needs national oversight rather than a thousand local pilots, noting the market remains immature and volatile. With 19 suppliers now on NHS England's registry and south-west London alone deploying to 20,000 clinicians, the gap between sales pitch and consulting-room reality is widening.

**The view:** This is the most useful thing said about scribes all year, and it comes from the person running the numbers. The disappointment isn't that the tools fail — it's that they were oversold as magic and turn out to be software that needs checking. Anyone signing the note still owns the note, hallucinations included. Realistic expectations and honest evaluation aren't pessimism; they're the only thing that stops the inevitable backlash when the hype curve breaks.

## 3. Washington commissions an autonomous AI cardiologist

Trump's ARPA-H is funding ADVOCATE, a programme to build the first FDA-authorised *agentic* AI in clinical care — a patient-facing agent for cardiovascular disease that can autonomously adjust medications, book appointments and nudge diet and exercise, wired into the record and the patient's wearables, available around the clock. Crucially, it also funds a supervisory AI "overseer" to police the front-line agents for safety. Award teams were due to be selected this month, with development, deployment and FDA review spanning roughly 39 months.

**Why it matters:** This is a genuine line in the sand — not AI advising a clinician, but AI *acting* on a patient and titrating their drugs without one in the loop. The honesty of building an AI to supervise the AI tells you how nervous even the proponents are. Whether the FDA can certify something that changes its own behaviour is the regulatory question of the decade, and the answer will eventually wash up on NICE's desk and ours.

## 4. The labour market splits in two

PwC's 2026 Global AI Jobs Barometer, drawn from over a billion job ads across 27 countries, describes a workforce cleaving into two tracks. Roles demanding AI skills are growing nearly eight times faster than the market as a whole, with a 62% wage premium for those skills. But the bottom rung is being pulled up out of reach: entry-level postings have fallen 29% since early 2024, and the junior roles that survive increasingly demand "senior" capabilities like judgement and leadership.

**The view:** Medicine has always trained by apprenticeship — the F1 who clerks badly until they clerk well. If AI quietly eats the junior work everywhere else, the question lands on us too: where do tomorrow's experienced clinicians come from if the machine does the spadework that used to teach them? A profession that automates away its own training grades is sawing off the branch it sits on.

---

## 5. Brussels blinks on its AI rulebook

The EU has provisionally agreed to delay the binding high-risk obligations of its flagship AI Act. Under the "Digital Omnibus" deal struck on 7 May, duties for use-based high-risk systems (the Annex III list, which includes much health AI) slip from August 2026 to December 2027 — a 16-month reprieve — with product-embedded systems pushed to 2028. Formal adoption is expected imminently, though until it lands the original August deadline technically still bites.

**Why it matters:** The world's most ambitious AI law has met the world's most reliable force — industry saying it isn't ready. A delay is defensible if the standards genuinely aren't baked; it's corrosive if it becomes the template every time a deadline looms. For UK readers it's a useful mirror: we have no equivalent statute at all, just MHRA guidance and good intentions, and Brussels just demonstrated how hard the hard version is.

### From the Eye

Three the front pages didn't run. **Item one** — Pennsylvania is suing Character.AI to stop its bots impersonating clinicians, after one named "Emilie" billed itself as a "Doctor of psychiatry" treating "her patient." Unlicensed practice used to require a human; now it requires a prompt. **Item two** — the patient-safety body ECRI has named misuse of AI chatbots the single biggest health-technology hazard of 2026, with the damning detail that the tools have recommended unnecessary tests, promoted subpar supplies and, memorably, "invented body parts" — confabulation isn't a quirk, it's the headline risk. **Item three** — buried in Azeem Azhar's *Exponential View* this week is the quiet news that a serious consensus is now forming behind *pausing* frontier AI development, a position that 18 months ago lived only on the fringe. When the boosters start arguing for the brakes, it's worth noticing who changed their mind and why.
