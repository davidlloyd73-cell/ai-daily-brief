# THE MORNING RUSH

*A short brief on AI in medicine — and the world it's moving through. Read it before the 8am phone lines open.*

**Monday 22 June 2026 · ~5 min read · Issue 054**

**On the line today:** A public dialogue on AI scribes lands with a clear verdict — patients will accept the listening machine, but only with national oversight, independent auditing and an opt-out that follows them around the NHS. Plus *From the Eye* — the citations that were never written, the silly money and musical chairs at the top, and a very capable model nobody had to pay for.

## 1. Patients will accept the scribe — but want someone watching it

A public dialogue commissioned by the AI firm Newton's Tree and run by Hopkins Van Mil, published last month, has been doing the rounds again this week as the policy crowd digests it. The reassuring headline — patients are broadly fine with AI scribes — comes with conditions that are anything but soft. Participants wanted national oversight with independent auditing, clear rules on data security and accuracy, proper staff training, and an opt-out that follows the patient across every NHS setting rather than being re-explained at each front desk. It dovetails with the MHRA's two landmark reports on public views of AI in healthcare, published 11 June, and with the Health Foundation's Jennifer Dixon arguing for ongoing review rather than a one-off pass/fail.

**The view from the surgery:** This is the public being more sensible than the procurement cycle. The opt-out-that-travels is the killer detail — at the moment a patient's "no" to being recorded is as portable as a paper note in 1987. And "independent auditing" is the right ask: every practice currently running a scribe is, in effect, its own regulator, marking its own homework on accuracy. We should welcome being watched. The alternative is finding out it went wrong from a coroner.

## 2. AI gives the GP chest X-ray a second pair of eyes

A real-world UK retrospective evaluation, out on medRxiv, looked at AI lung-nodule detection software run across GP-referred chest radiographs — the workhorse first-line investigation for the patient with a cough that won't settle. The headline is an improved detection rate for lung cancer against a low background prevalence, the unglamorous setting where most cancers actually hide. It builds on earlier UK work analysing thousands of consecutive chest films at cancer prevalence around 1.6%. The promise is catching the subtle nodule the rushed eye skates over at 6pm.

**Why it matters:** This is AI where it belongs — not diagnosing, but flagging. The chest X-ray is the most-ordered, least-loved image in primary care, and the missed early lung cancer on a plain film is one of the genuinely tragic recurring errors in our world. A reliable second read that says "look again here" is worth more than a hundred chatbots. The catch, as ever, is the false-positive tax: flag too eagerly and you flood an already-creaking CT pathway. Sensitivity is easy; the art is the threshold.

## 3. The therapy chatbot keeps booking appointments

Fresh survey data from the American Psychological Association finds more than a third of psychologists now report patients turning to AI as an additional mental-health professional — patients are, in effect, bringing a chatbot to therapy. It lands alongside a Brown University study showing chatbots routinely breach core mental-health ethics standards even when prompted to use evidence-based techniques: mishandling crisis, reinforcing negative beliefs, manufacturing a false sense of empathy. US states are scrambling — Illinois and Nevada have banned AI for behavioural health outright, New York and Utah merely demand the bot admit it isn't human.

**The view:** Our patients are already doing this, whatever we think. The anxious, the lonely, the 2am insomniac — they are talking to something that never tires, never judges and is always available, which is precisely its danger. A model engineered to be agreeable is a poor companion for someone whose problem is that their own thoughts are agreeing with them too readily. Worth asking the question gently in consultations: not "are you using one of these," but "what's it been telling you."

## 4. The AI talent war claims a Nobel laureate

John Jumper — who shared the 2024 Nobel Prize in Chemistry for AlphaFold, the system that cracked protein-structure prediction — is leaving Google DeepMind for Anthropic, CNBC reported on 19 June. It comes days after Noam Shazeer, a co-lead of Google's Gemini models, jumped the other way to the IPO-bound OpenAI. The poaching is happening against a backdrop of frankly unhinged numbers: Anthropic is reported to have closed financing at a valuation near $965bn and filed confidentially to go public.

**Why it matters:** Jumper is the rare figure whose work already changed medicine — AlphaFold sits inside drug-discovery pipelines worldwide. His move signals that the frontier labs increasingly see biology, not just chatbots, as the prize. For those of us downstream, the question is whether the next decade's diagnostic and therapeutic tools get built by mission-driven scientists or by whoever wins the bidding war for them. The money says the latter. History says the best science rarely came from the highest bidder.

## 5. The quiet land-grab for the layer where AI *acts*

While everyone watched the chatbots, the enterprise software giants spent the spring buying the plumbing. A run of acquisitions — Asana taking StackAI, Coupa buying Rossum, others in the same vein — all target the same thing: the "execution layer," the bit where an AI agent stops advising and starts *doing* — filing the form, moving the money, updating the record. Gartner's much-quoted forecast has 40% of enterprise applications embedding task-specific agents by year's end, up from under 5% a year ago.

**The view from the surgery:** This is the shift worth tracking, because it's coming for the back office of every NHS trust. An AI that drafts a letter is a convenience; an AI that *sends* it, books the follow-up and codes the encounter is a different animal — and a different liability. The agentic turn is where "decision support" quietly becomes "decision," and nobody has yet worked out who is accountable when the agent acts and is wrong. We should sort that out before, not after, it's wired into the referral system.

### From the Eye

Three off the front page. **Item one** — the footnotes are rotting. A study tracking fabricated citations in academic papers found the rate climbing from 1 in 2,828 papers in 2023 to 1 in 458 in 2025, and reaching 1 in 277 in the first weeks of 2026 — references to work that was never written, generated by LLMs and waved through peer review. Yesterday's NEJM photo retraction was one doctored image; this is the load-bearing scaffolding of the evidence base quietly turning to papier-mâché, one plausible citation at a time. **Item two** — the silly money and the musical chairs. The Jumper and Shazeer moves are the visible tip of a frenzy in which a chatbot company is reportedly valued near a trillion dollars and filing to float; keep an eye on whether the valuations or the talent settle first, because one of them is wrong. **Item three** — while Washington frets about frontier safety, Zhipu AI quietly released GLM-5.2 under a permissive MIT licence with a million-token context window. A genuinely capable model that anyone can download and run locally, free — the sort of thing that makes "sovereign AI" arguments and export controls look quaint, and that no NHS procurement process has a box for.
