## 1. South West London flips the switch — and St George's is in it

Four NHS trusts across **South West London** — **St George's**, Epsom and St Helier, Croydon Health Services, and Kingston and Richmond — have begun rolling out **Lyrebird** ambient voice technology to **10,000 clinicians** in year one, scaling to **20,000** over four years. It is being billed as the largest implementation of ambient scribing in the NHS to date, integrated directly into the shared **Oracle Cerner Millennium** record so notes, form-filling and medication history populate themselves. It follows fresh **NHS England** guidance confirming that explicit consent is not required for individual care, but clinicians must tell patients the technology is in use and offer them the chance to object.

**The view from the surgery:** This is the inflection point — scribing is no longer a pilot, it is procurement at scale, and it is landing in the trust where my own son works as an HCA. The "inform and offer to object" rule is sensible and quietly important: it is the line between a tool patients tolerate and one they resent. Say it at the top of the consultation, every time, and mean it.

## 2. The indemnity question most scribe users haven't asked

A cross-regional evaluation of AI scribes in practice found that only **42%** of clinicians knew their medical defence organisation's position on using one — this against **70%** who acknowledged clinical-accuracy risks and **64%** who flagged legal-liability concerns. The defence bodies' line, broadly, is that they would still defend and potentially indemnify a doctor where AI played a role in a claim, but the exposure sits in the gaps: software left un-updated, staff deployed without training, consent not properly taken.

**Why it matters:** Most of us are now dictating into these tools faster than we have checked whether we are covered doing so. The honest homework is dull but cheap — confirm your MDU or MPS position in writing, confirm your practice has logged the scribe's training and update regime, and confirm the consent line is actually being said. The claim, when it comes, will turn on the boring paperwork, not the clever software.

## 3. The FDA won't wave diagnostic AI through

The **FDA** has rejected a petition from Australian health-AI firm **Harrison.ai** that sought to exempt several categories of radiology and diagnostic AI — computer-aided detection and diagnosis — from traditional **510(k)** premarket review. The agency's reasoning was pointed: prior authorisation of one AI device does not demonstrate that the next product from the same company will perform safely. Notable timing, given this is the same FDA that has elsewhere been easing oversight of low-risk wearables and software under an administration committed to deregulation.

**The view:** A welcome flash of spine. The seductive argument — "we approved their last model, trust the next one" — is exactly the one that should never wash with software that drifts, retrains and ships silent updates. The principle travels: a vendor's track record is not a substitute for evidence on the specific tool in front of you. The MHRA, drafting its own AI-device framework this year, should take note.

## 4. The White House blinks on its FDA-for-AI order

The **White House** has postponed an executive order that would have created a pre-release vetting process for frontier AI models, with President Trump saying he delayed it because he "didn't like certain aspects". The framework under discussion would have given federal agencies up to **90 days** to security-review a model before release — an FDA-style gate explicitly prompted by **Anthropic's** decision to withhold its "Mythos" model after it proved unexpectedly capable at offensive cybersecurity.

**Why it matters:** Strip away the politics and this is a regulator reaching, late and reluctantly, for the same instinct medicine settled on decades ago: prove it is safe before it reaches the public. That the catalyst was a cyber-weapon rather than a clinical harm tells you where the perceived risk now sits. Worth watching, because whatever framework eventually lands on frontier models will shape the foundation systems our clinical tools are built on top of.

## 5. The compute crunch coming for your pilot pricing

In **Exponential View**, **Azeem Azhar** flags the "prelude to an AI supercycle" — and the numbers are stark. **Nvidia B200** GPU rental prices rose **114%** in six weeks; the premium to rent a B200 over the older H200 has grown more than **sixfold**; and one infrastructure provider reports forty customers between them chasing **400,000 GPUs** against a current fleet of 40,000. Demand is outrunning silicon, and the bill is heading downstream.

**The view from the surgery:** This is the unglamorous economics behind every AI quote you have been shown. The pricing in your 2025 scribe pilot was set in a buyer's market that is closing fast; when contracts come up for renewal, the friendly per-seat figure may not survive contact with 2027 compute costs. Lock terms while you can, and treat any business case that assumes today's prices hold as optimistic fiction.

### From the Eye

Three the front pages didn't run. **Item one** — at **Barts Health's** International Clinical Trials Day on **20 May**, the genuinely useful AI was the quiet sort: podiatrists at the **Royal London** testing a tool to spot diabetic foot disease, and the respiratory team mining NHS data to flag patients at risk of lung cancer and bump them up the screening queue. No differential-diagnosis theatre — just finding the right patient earlier, which is where the actual lives are saved. **Item two** — the study everyone now cites without naming was **GOSH's TORTUS** trial: **17,000+** patient encounters across nine London sites spanning hospitals, GP practices, mental health and ambulance teams, with an outpatient rollout planned for the autumn. It has quietly become the NHS reference evidence base for ambient scribing, which means its limitations are about to become everyone's limitations. **Item three** — the bit absent from every glossy demo: from this year an AI scribe in NHS use must carry a clinical safety case under **DCB0129/DCB0160**, with defined governance and an audit trail of clinician review. Ask any vendor pitching you for theirs. The ones who blink are telling you something.
