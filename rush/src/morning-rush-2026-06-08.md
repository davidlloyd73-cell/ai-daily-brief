*A short brief on AI in medicine — and the world it's moving through. Read it before the 8am phone lines open.*

**On the line today:** The **NHS 10-Year Workforce Plan** lands this month with AI written in as a substitute for whole roles — and the **BMA** has called it a "massive, dangerous gamble". On the technology itself, **Mayo Clinic and Microsoft** have started building a healthcare-only frontier model that even its makers say is years from being safe for high-stakes questions, while **Ontario's auditor general** has quietly shown what happens when the scribe revolution meets a proper audit — most approved tools got things wrong. Plus *From the Eye* — the drug pipeline with no approvals, California's companion-bot law, and Microsoft's quiet divorce from OpenAI.

## 1. The workforce plan bets the NHS on AI — and the BMA isn't buying it

The final **NHS 10-Year Workforce Plan**, due from health secretary **James Murray** this month, will reportedly expand AI across the service "including in instances where technology can completely substitute for a role" — admin, rostering, data management, even elements of occupational health triage. The **BMA** has called it "a massive, dangerous gamble", noting the plan implies up to **380,000 fewer staff** by 2035 and pointing out the UK already runs on **3.4 doctors per 1,000 people** against Germany's 4.6. Dr Amit Kochhar called it "crushingly disappointing" that low doctor numbers are being treated as a fixed fact of life.

**The view from the surgery:** The honest version of this plan would say "we cannot afford the workforce we need, so AI must close the gap". Instead it dresses a budget constraint as a technology strategy. Some of the admin automation is genuinely welcome — none of us went into medicine to roster. But "completely substitute for a role" is a phrase that should make every clinician read the small print, because the roles nearest the chopping block are the ones that quietly hold safety together.

## 2. Mayo Clinic and Microsoft start building a doctor-only frontier model

On **2 June**, Mayo Clinic and Microsoft announced a partnership to build a "frontier" AI model trained not on the open internet but on Mayo's de-identified clinical records, research and physician expertise. Mayo will own the model; Microsoft will offer it through Azure and may fold its reasoning into the consumer Copilot assistant. Tellingly, Microsoft AI chief **Mustafa Suleyman** said it would take "many years" to make it accurate enough to trust for high-stakes health questions.

**Why it matters:** This is the right architecture — a model whose training data is curated clinical knowledge rather than Reddit — and the right humility about timelines. The catch is ownership and access: a single US health system owning a licensable clinical model is a very different governance picture from a public-interest one. When this reaches UK shores via Azure, the question for any ICB won't be "is it clever" but "whose data trained it, who profits, and who carries the liability when it's wrong".

## 3. Ontario audited its AI scribes — and most of them failed

Ontario's auditor general reported (13 May) that of **20 government-approved AI scribe tools**, nine produced outright **hallucinations** — fabricating findings or treatment suggestions the doctor never made — and twelve recorded incorrect information, including the wrong drug. Most strikingly, **85% missed key mental-health details** in at least one test. Roughly 5,000 Ontario physicians use these tools; no patient harm has been formally reported, but the systems were never adequately evaluated before approval.

**The view:** Hold this next to the "transformative" headlines about ambient scribes and you have the whole debate in two data points. The technology really does hand time back to clinicians — and it really does invent things, with a confident, well-formatted bedside manner. The lesson isn't "don't use scribes"; it's that the human signing the note is the safety system, and an 8.2% shorter consultation is worth nothing if it ends with the wrong drug in the record. Read every line before you sign.

## 4. AI is now the No.1 reason employers give for cutting jobs

US outplacement data shows AI has overtaken every other stated cause of layoffs: AI-linked cuts in the first five months of 2026 already exceed the combined total for 2024 and 2025, and AI was cited in nearly **40% of announced cuts in May**, up from 7% in January. The technology sector has been hit hardest. Analysts caution that "AI" is sometimes a tidy label for ordinary cost-cutting — but the quieter signal, weak hiring for junior and entry-level roles, is harder to spin away.

**The view from the surgery:** This is the backdrop to every NHS conversation about "efficiency". When the wider economy is using AI as cover to thin out its juniors, a workforce plan that does the same thing to the health service deserves more scrutiny, not less. Medicine learns by apprenticeship; the entry-level role you automate away today is the consultant you don't have in fifteen years.

## 5. Colorado switches on America's first broad AI law on 30 June

While Brussels delays and Washington signs voluntary orders, **Colorado's AI Act** comes into force on **30 June 2026** — the first comprehensive US state law governing "high-risk" AI systems, with duties on both developers and deployers to guard against algorithmic discrimination and to disclose when AI is making consequential decisions. It has been delayed once already and narrowed under lobbying, but it survives.

**Why it matters:** The interesting regulatory action in America is at state level, not federal — Colorado on consequential decisions, California on companion chatbots. For anyone procuring clinical AI, this fragmentation matters: a tool's behaviour, disclosures and audit trail may differ by jurisdiction, and "compliant in Texas" tells you nothing. The UK's principle-led approach looks tidy by comparison, right up until you ask which principle is actually enforceable.

### From the Eye

Three the front pages didn't run. **Item one** — the AI drug-discovery story keeps selling the sizzle: there are now **200-plus AI-discovered drugs in clinical trials** (15 in Phase 3) and a fresh **$2.75bn** Eli Lilly–Insilico deal, yet the count of AI-discovered drugs with FDA approval remains exactly **zero**. The pitch decks are magnificent; the marketing authorisations are imaginary. Worth remembering when a rep tells you AI has "transformed" the pipeline. **Item two** — California's **SB 243**, live since January, regulates "companion chatbots" with a private right of action and **$1,000 per violation** — meaning the bots impersonating confidants and counsellors now carry a price tag enforceable by ordinary citizens, not just a regulator. It is the quiet template other states (and perhaps a future UK bill) will copy, and almost nobody is reading it. **Item three** — buried under the model-release noise, **Microsoft** has begun shipping its own in-house "MAI" models specifically to **reduce its reliance on OpenAI**, the partner it part-owns. When the largest backer of the leading lab starts building the exits, that tells you more about where the frontier-model economics are heading than any benchmark score — and the clinical tools riding on these platforms are downstream of that divorce.
