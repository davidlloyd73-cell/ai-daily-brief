## 1. Cresswell calls the top: ambient scribes hit the trough of disillusionment

**Professor Kathrin Cresswell** of the University of Edinburgh — the academic who led the formal evaluation of the NHS AI Lab — went on record this week with the sharpest critique yet of the ambient-scribe rollout. She argues the field is "approaching the trough of disillusionment", that funded research has prioritised the early-stage pilot story over "the solid foundations and infrastructures on which AI sits", and that time savings on one task simply generate work elsewhere. The market itself, she says, remains "immature and volatile".

**The view from the surgery:** Cresswell is not a sceptic from the outside — she is the academic who built much of the early NHS evidence base. When she says the field is over-promising, ICB digital leads listen. Read alongside the Newton's Tree report (below) and the Nuffield Trust 28% headline, the picture is clear: a technology that does what it says on the tin, mis-sold as a productivity miracle. The honest GP framing is "less burden, similar throughput" — not the 30%-more-patients pitch some vendors are still running.

## 2. Newton's Tree public dialogue: national oversight, opt-outs, NHS-held accountability

A separate dialogue report — commissioned by healthcare-AI firm **Newton's Tree** with 41 members of the public in March, published this week — found awareness of AI scribes among ordinary patients is low, and that learning the technology was already in routine use generated frustration. The respondents' three concrete asks: national oversight with audits and spot checks; clinician discretion in sensitive consultations (domestic abuse, mental health and child safeguarding were called out); and clear accountability resting with the NHS rather than the supplier.

**Why it matters:** That last point is the one to mark. If the NHS is the accountable party — not the vendor — the indemnity and complaints traffic over the next two years will run through MDOs and the GMC, not through software contracts. Your **MDU** subscription is already being priced against this. The patient appetite for an opt-out is the more interesting practical signal: the consent script at the start of the consultation is now part of the workflow, not an optional courtesy.

## 3. Mayo Clinic + Microsoft: a "frontier" healthcare model, owned by Mayo

On **2 June**, **Mayo Clinic** and **Microsoft** announced a strategic collaboration to build and deploy a frontier AI model purpose-built for healthcare, trained on Mayo's de-identified clinical data and longitudinal records and intended to handle "the broadest scope of clinical reasoning and healthcare use cases". The structural detail to note: **Mayo owns the model**. Microsoft will distribute it through Azure Foundry APIs and embed it in Mayo's clinician and patient products, including an AI assistant inside the hospital portal.

**The view:** Mayo retaining ownership is the negotiating posture every large provider organisation has been waiting to copy. It is the first deal of this scale where the clinical institution holds the asset and the hyperscaler is positioned as distribution. Expect **NHS England**, the **HSE**, **Karolinska** and **Singapore's MOH** to study the contract structure carefully — and expect a quieter UK conversation about whether NHS data ought to underwrite a sovereign equivalent rather than being licensed wholesale to a foreign LLM.

## 4. Anthropic files confidential S-1 — the year's largest AI listing in motion

**Anthropic** confidentially filed a draft **Form S-1** with the **SEC** on **1 June**, opening a path to a public listing at a private valuation already past **$960bn** — ahead of OpenAI's $852bn round in March. Revenue run-rate is **$47bn**, up from $10bn twelve months ago. The filing arrives in the same week NHSE cited the company's **Mythos** model as a code-reading cyber risk and Anthropic disclosed it is expanding **Project Glasswing** to 150 critical-infrastructure organisations across 15 countries.

**Why it matters:** A near-trillion-dollar IPO from a five-year-old AI lab is not just a market event — it is the moment frontier-AI safety stops being a research argument and becomes a fiduciary disclosure to public-market shareholders. Read the S-1 carefully when the public version surfaces in late summer: the risk-factors section is where the company has to admit, on the record, what its models can and cannot do.

## 5. Microsoft UK: Copilot in 100 NHS organisations by year-end

**Darren Hardman**, Microsoft UK chief, used a London policy summit on **1 June** to set out his UK ambition: **Copilot active in 100 NHS organisations and every central government department by the end of 2026**, with 15,000 civil servants already trained under the Cabinet Office's CDDO programme. The framing — that AI "lifts the cognitive load" rather than replaces people — is consistent with the **Dragon Copilot** ambient-scribe pitch already running in trusts. Trade unions and the **ICO** have separately flagged concerns about job displacement and the volume of personal data flowing to US-owned algorithms.

**The view from the surgery:** A hundred-organisation deployment is the right denominator to watch over the next six months — that is where the practical lessons (and the eventual incident reports) will surface, not in the NHS England Engineering Board's policy documents. Worth asking your ICB digital lead next month: is this practice on Microsoft's list, or not?

### From the Eye

Three the front pages didn't run. **Item one** — yesterday's **Energy Live News** UK data-centre piece is not a *medical* story, but it is one. **84%** of the **140** UK data-centre schemes currently in the planning pipeline sit in **water-stressed catchments**, and the **50GW** of power being sought is **5GW more than the UK's current peak demand**. Every NHS-hosted frontier-model query — the Mayo deal, the Dragon Copilot rollout, the Foresight rebuild — runs through one of these sites. The "AI in healthcare" cost line in your ICB's medium-term plan is downstream of grid and water policy, not just procurement. **Item two** — **Maryland** has become the **30th US state** to legislate against election deepfakes ahead of the November midterms, with three more bills pending and **58%** of US adults telling pollsters they expect synthetic political content to escalate before the ballot. The bit not getting written up: the same disclosure-and-labelling architecture is what the **AMA** is pushing the **FTC** to apply to **deepfake medical advertising** — the snake-oil-supplement video featuring your face. The two regulatory tracks are converging, and the medical limb is roughly six months behind the political one. **Item three** — **Heidi Health**, the Melbourne-headquartered ambient scribe with the largest UK GP footprint after Tortus, has quietly shipped **Heidi Remote**: a lapel-clip device that records the consultation **on-device**, encrypted, with no cloud round-trip until the clinician presses sync. It is the first serious hardware answer to the GDPR question winding through the **Foresight** row — and the first hint that the hardware layer of clinical AI may end up mattering as much as the model layer.
