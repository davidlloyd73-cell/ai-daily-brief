# THE MORNING RUSH

*A short brief on AI in medicine — and the world it's moving through. Read it before the 8am phone lines open.*

**Wednesday 24 June 2026 · ~5 min read · Issue 056**

---

**On the line today:** A sobering randomised trial shows even AI-trained doctors fold when the model is confidently wrong — automation bias, measured. An ophthalmology pathway cuts unnecessary diabetic eye referrals by more than half. Vermont becomes the first US state to ban therapy delivered by AI alone. On the wider stage, Azeem Azhar marshals the numbers on AI's awkward return-on-investment problem, and a Chinese open-weights model muscles into the frontier. From the Eye: a "spa" body scanner, blood tests read by chatbots, and the quiet scandal of unvalidated kit already in clinics.

## 1. The machine is confidently wrong, and the doctor agrees

A randomised trial in *NEJM AI* has put a number on something we have all half-feared. Forty-four physicians who had completed a 20-hour AI-literacy course worked through diagnostic cases with an LLM at their elbow. When the model's suggestions were sound, reasoning scores sat at 84.9%. When the suggestions were subtly wrong, scores collapsed to 73.3% — and top-choice accuracy fell from 90.5% to 76.1%. Crucially, nobody was forced to consult the model; they reached for it voluntarily and then deferred to it. A companion trial out this month suggests lightweight "nudges" can blunt the effect, so it is not destiny.

**The view from the surgery:** This is the finding that should be pinned above every consulting-room screen. Training did not inoculate these doctors — it may have lent the tool false authority. The scribe and the decision-support pop-up are only safe while we treat them as a junior we're duty-bound to overrule.

## 2. An eye-screening pathway that referred everyone now refers four in ten

In a randomised trial of 276 patients with suspected diabetic macular oedema, an AI-OCT pathway met its non-inferiority mark for false-positive referrals while cutting onward referrals from 100% to 39%. For a condition where the hospital eye service is groaning and most "suspected" cases turn out not to need the clinic, that is the kind of triage gain that actually unclogs a pathway rather than simply adding another alert.

**Why it matters:** Diabetic eye disease is bread-and-butter for us, and the bottleneck has always been secondary care capacity, not detection. A tool that safely keeps six in ten patients out of the queue is worth more than another headline-grabbing diagnostic accuracy figure — provided the "safely" survives real-world validation outside a trial.

## 3. Vermont says a chatbot cannot be your therapist

Vermont has become the first US state to prohibit corporations from independently delivering mental-health services through AI. Act 156 draws a hard line: an AI may assist a clinician, but it cannot *be* the clinician. A companion law tightens data-broker rules and adds disclosure requirements for generative-AI data sharing and geolocation. It lands just as a wave of "AI therapy" apps has been quietly booking and rebooking vulnerable users with no human in the loop.

**The view:** Crude, perhaps, but the right instinct. The therapeutic relationship is the treatment, not the wrapper around it. Expect the UK conversation to follow — and expect the apps to rebadge themselves as "wellbeing companions" to slip the net, exactly as the wellness-supplement trade has always done.

---

## 4. The bill is coming due on AI's return on investment

Azeem Azhar's *Exponential View* this week pulls together the figures the boosters skate over: only 27% of executives say AI has met their ROI expectations, and 71% of companies overshot their AI budgets in 2025. His framing is that most organisations are still in the "lightbulb phase" — wiring the new thing into old workflows and wondering why the savings don't appear. The gains, he argues, come only from redesigning the work, not bolting a model onto it.

**Why it matters:** The NHS is mid-spend on exactly this curve, and the lesson is direct. Bolting a scribe onto a 10-minute appointment that is still structured around 1990s paperwork will disappoint. The trusts that gain are the ones rethinking the consultation itself — which is harder, slower, and far less photogenic than a procurement announcement.

---

## 5. A Chinese open model crashes the frontier party

MiniMax has released M2.5, an open-weights model trained heavily through reinforcement learning that posts state-of-the-art results on coding and "agentic" tasks — the autonomous, multi-step work that the Western labs have been charging premium subscriptions for. It is the latest in a run of Chinese releases that match the frontier and then give the weights away, undercutting the closed-model business case as OpenAI lines up a reported autumn IPO.

**The view:** Open weights are a mixed blessing for health systems. They mean a model that can, in principle, run inside the NHS firewall with patient data never leaving the building — a real prize for information governance. They also mean capable models proliferating with no off-switch and no accountable vendor. The geopolitics is above our pay grade; the data-residency upside is not.

### From the Eye

**Item one:** Midjourney — yes, the image-generation outfit — has announced *Midjourney Medical*, a water-based ultrasound scanner promising a detailed "body map" in about 60 seconds, with the first installation pencilled in for a San Francisco spa in 2027. Whole-body screening rebranded as a wellness treatment, sold before any FDA submission for the interesting bits; the incidentaloma industry has found its luxury tier. **Item two:** the consumer "blood-test explainer" market — BloodGPT, Whoop, Levels and the general-purpose chatbots — is now happily interpreting lab results for paying punters, while clinicians point out there is scant evidence any of it is accurate or safe. Expect more patients arriving with a printout and a diagnosis already in hand. **Item three:** the least-trodden and most important — a multi-institutional analysis finds that many FDA-authorised clinical AI devices have *no publicly available validation data at all*. Kit is being deployed faster than it can be trusted, and a network is now forming explicitly to fix it. Worth remembering next time a glossy "FDA-cleared" sticker is waved at a procurement meeting.
