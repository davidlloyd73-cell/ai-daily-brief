## 1. NHS England closes the GitHub doors — effective today

From **today, 11 May**, all NHS England source-code repositories must be **private by default**. The guidance — first reported by *The Register* on **5 May** and confirmed by NHS England — reverses a longstanding open-source-by-default position in the *NHS Service Manual*. The Engineering Board's justification names **Anthropic's Mythos model** specifically, citing "rapid advancements in AI models capable of large-scale code ingestion, inference and reasoning". Teams wanting an exemption had to file by **6 May**. NHSE has described this as a temporary measure while the cyber posture is reviewed.

**The view from the surgery:** This is the first operational decision by a UK public body to cite a frontier model by name in a security policy. Months of community contributions to NHS code now sit behind a login. The strategic point is larger: the NHS has just told the world Mythos changed its threat model. Wednesday's Cyber Security Bill will arrive in a different light because of it.

## 2. Mayo Clinic's REDMOD spots pancreatic cancer three years early — on scans done for other reasons

Mayo published validation data this month for **REDMOD** — the **Radiomics-based Early Detection Model** — on routine **abdominal CT scans**. In the validation cohort, the model identified **73% of prediagnostic pancreatic cancers at a median of 16 months before clinical diagnosis**, and **nearly three times** as many early cancers as specialists reviewing the same scans unaided when looking more than two years out. It runs on the existing CT, no extra acquisition, no manual preparation, with new-onset diabetics flagged as the high-yield cohort. Mayo's prospective trial **AI-PACED** is now opening.

**Why it matters:** Pancreatic cancer is the disease GPs spend a career missing — by the time the textbook signs arrive the operative window is largely closed and five-year survival sits around 13%. A model that finds it on the scan the patient had two years ago for back pain is, on paper, the most consequential clinical-AI story of the year. The questions before celebrating: how it performs in UK populations, what the false-positive workup pathway looks like in NHS practice, and which ICB wires it into a high-risk register first. New-onset diabetes over fifty is in every GP's morning list.

## 3. Brussels quietly relaxes the AI Act it spent two years writing

The **EU Council and Parliament** reached political agreement on **7 May** to amend the AI Act before it has fully bedded in. The deadline for member states to establish regulatory sandboxes is pushed back to **August 2027**, the grace period for transparency watermarking is shortened to **December 2026** (a ban on AI nudification apps is added), and the Commission's supervision and enforcement powers against general-purpose AI providers come into force as planned on **2 August 2026** — with fines up to **€35m or 7% of global turnover**.

**The view:** "Simplification" is the policy euphemism of the year. Amnesty has called it a rollback; the Commission says high-risk obligations are coming on time and the rest needed making usable. For UK medtech the practical reading is unchanged — anyone selling AI into the EU still has to meet the high-risk regime from August, and the MHRA will keep aligning to that floor in the absence of a UK statute. With Wednesday's King's Speech floating an AI Growth Lab carve-out, the contrast is sharp: Brussels relaxes, London experiments.

## 4. The $700 billion year

Combined 2026 capex from **Microsoft, Alphabet, Amazon, Meta and Oracle** is now tracking to **$660–700 billion**, up from roughly **$410bn in 2025** — the largest single-year capital expenditure surge in the history of any industry, comfortably exceeding the cumulative dot-com build-out. **Google Cloud's backlog** alone now stands at **$460 billion**, almost doubled in two quarters. Q1 2026 alone delivered **$130 billion-plus** of hyperscaler capex.

**Why it matters:** That figure is roughly **twenty-eight per cent of the entire annual NHS budget**, every year, on data centres. The compute crunch is the headline; the underwriting story is more interesting — Google's backlog is essentially an enterprise queue for AI workloads everyone now assumes are coming. NHS digital teams who think AVT and triage are the high-watermark of their AI procurement should be looking past this year's RFPs at where the bottleneck is in 2027.

## 5. The UAE is at 70% adult AI use. We are not.

Microsoft's **Q1 2026 Global AI Diffusion Report** (published **7 May**) puts global working-age adoption at **17.8%**, up from 16.3% in Q4 2025. The **UAE** leads at **70.1%**, with **26 economies** now above 30%. The **US** has moved up to 21st at 31.3%. The UK is not in the headline ranking and the report flags a widening North–South gap.

**The view from the surgery:** A reminder that adoption beats invention. The UAE is doing this with state direction, English-language tools, and a labour market it can reorganise faster than ours. The UK position is the awkward middle — clinicians using ChatGPT off-piste, the Nuffield Trust's December figure of 28% of GPs already using AI in clinical work, and a regulator yet to publish the device framework promised for this year. We are above average. We are not on the leaderboard.

### From the Eye

Three items the front pages mostly missed. **The first US class action over an ambient scribe has landed**: *Sutter Health* and *MemorialCare* are being sued in the Northern District of California, filed **8 April**, over deploying **Abridge** without explicit patient consent. UK indemnifiers (MDDUS, MPS, MDU) have not yet published a position on recording-and-retention; worth a quiet look at your AVT consent template before the same case lands here. **Mythos has been turned on Mozilla** and found **271 bugs in Firefox**, some hidden since **2011** — a fortnight after Anthropic suggested the model could *also* be used defensively, and a week after the NHS read the same brief and pulled its repos. The "AI for defence" pitch is now testable in public. **And the OpenAI agent phone is being fast-tracked**: per Qualcomm's CEO on **9 May**, "pretty much all" the major AI players are working on devices, with OpenAI's production target pulled in to **1H 2027** — a phone where the apps are gone and an agent handles the stack on your behalf. The early users will be exactly the patients who arrive next year with an AI summary of their own symptoms, written by the model that booked the appointment.
