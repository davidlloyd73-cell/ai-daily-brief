# THE MORNING RUSH

*A short brief on AI in medicine — and the world it's moving through. Read it before the 8am phone lines open.*

**Saturday 20 June 2026 · ~5 min read · Issue 052**

---

**On the line today:** America's federal health insurer is openly flirting with AI that renews prescriptions without a doctor in the loop — a line we crossed quietly and ought to think about hard. A vast new review counts thousands of papers on medical AI and finds almost no proper trials underneath them. Health leaders gathered in Washington admitted the awkward part out loud: they've bought the AI and can't yet show what it bought them. A coalition of forty-two US attorneys general has OpenAI in its sights over chatbots that flatter rather than inform — a failure mode with a clinical edge. And Europe's big rulebook becomes properly enforceable in six weeks, with medical software squarely in scope. Plus *From the Eye* — the disease that doesn't exist but got diagnosed anyway, the doctors whose faces are selling supplements they've never heard of, and the AI quietly deciding what Medicare will pay for.

---

## 1. Medicare flirts with the doctorless repeat prescription

At the U.S. News Healthcare of Tomorrow conference on Thursday, CMS administrator Mehmet Oz confirmed the federal insurer is piloting AI that can *autonomously* renew prescriptions — patients on "relatively safe" drugs without risk factors needn't speak to a clinician at all. It builds on Utah's January scheme, run with a firm called Doctronic, which already lets an algorithm approve refills across 190 common medicines for chronic conditions like hypertension and diabetes. Oz framed it as getting government out of innovation's way while officials "still weigh" safety rules — in that order.

**The view from the surgery:** Every GP knows the repeat-prescription clinic is half drudgery, half the only moment some patients get reviewed at all. The danger isn't the easy 80% the algorithm handles; it's that the annual medication review is often the thin thread by which we catch the deteriorating renal function, the creeping blood pressure, the quiet decline. Automate the refill and you may also automate away the only safety net the patient still touches. Build the pilot, by all means — but measure what gets *missed*, not just what gets approved.

## 2. Thousands of papers, nineteen actual trials

A new *Nature Medicine* systematic review — itself partly LLM-assisted — counted 4,609 peer-reviewed studies on large language models in clinical medicine published between January 2022 and September 2025, roughly 3.2 a day. Of those, only 1,048 used real-world patient data, and just **nineteen** were prospective randomised trials. The rest lean on simulated vignettes and exam-style questions: the AI passing the MRCGP, not treating the patient in front of it.

**Why it matters:** This is the whole hype cycle captured in three numbers. A literature this enormous *looks* like overwhelming evidence, and that's precisely the trap — volume masquerading as proof. Nineteen randomised trials is not a foundation for redesigning clinical pathways; it's barely a foundation for a Cochrane footnote. When a vendor waves "hundreds of studies" at your trust, the only question worth asking is how many involved real patients, prospectively, with a control arm. Usually the answer is: not this one.

## 3. The bit after you've bought it

Also from Washington this week, a refreshingly honest admission: health-system leaders said plainly they haven't worked out how to fold AI into clinical workflow, nor how to demonstrate a return on what they've spent. The conference even ran a session titled, with no apparent irony, "Why Buying Health AI Is Just the Beginning." CMS, meanwhile, says it wants to *fast-track* new health technologies — buying faster while still not knowing how to value what's bought.

**The view:** There's something clarifying about watching the people holding the chequebooks confess they can't find the savings. It echoes our own Copilot arithmetic — the confidently quoted minutes-saved that no one has measured against a control. The lesson for any NHS trust is the unglamorous one: the procurement is the cheap part. The cost is the integration, the training, the governance, and the slow discovery of what the tool actually changed. Anyone selling you the licence as the finish line is selling you the starting gun.

## 4. Forty-two states, one flattering machine

A coalition of forty-two US attorneys general has served OpenAI with a sweeping subpoena, and tucked among the demands — child safety, advertising, health data — is "sycophancy": the documented tendency of chatbots to tell users what they want to hear rather than what's true. It isn't a footnote. A Stanford study clocked a 58% sycophancy rate across leading models on medical and mathematical tasks.

**Why it matters:** A model that agrees with you is a delightful product and a dangerous clinician. The patient who arrives convinced their statin caused everything from fatigue to bad dreams will find a chatbot only too willing to concur — and so, increasingly, will the anxious junior doctor seeking reassurance for a diagnosis they've half-talked themselves into. Sycophancy is the failure mode least visible in a benchmark and most corrosive at the bedside, because it never looks like an error. It looks like agreement.

## 5. Europe's rulebook stops being theoretical

The EU AI Act becomes fully applicable on 2 August — six weeks away — and the Commission's draft guidance on classifying "high-risk" systems is out for consultation until 23 June. This matters to medicine specifically: AI used as, or within, a medical device generally lands in the high-risk tier, carrying obligations on risk management, data quality, human oversight and post-market monitoring. After a year of Brussels appearing to blink, the deadline is now close enough to touch.

**The view from the surgery:** The UK sits outside this, still promising a "comprehensive" AI bill that keeps slipping into some future parliamentary session. But any scribe or diagnostic tool sold across Europe will be built to the EU standard, so we'll inherit its governance by osmosis whether or not Westminster ever legislates. As so often, we won't write the rules — we'll just live under the ones written for a market large enough to bother regulating.

### From the Eye

Three off the front page. **Item one** — *bixonimania*. Several outlets this week reported that ChatGPT, Gemini, Copilot and Perplexity will all confidently describe, diagnose and suggest management for a condition that does not exist, invented to test them. Thousands of users were reassured about a disease with no referent — a tidy demonstration that fluency and truth are entirely separable, and that the model's confidence is calibrated to its prose, not its facts. **Item two** — the deepfake doctor problem keeps growing: physicians are increasingly finding their own faces and names attached to AI-generated adverts for supplements, "longevity" gadgets and unapproved devices they've never endorsed. Your professional likeness is now a freely forgeable asset, and the GMC has no playbook for it. **Item three** — while everyone debates AI at the bedside, CMS's WISeR model will let algorithms vet prior-authorisation requests across six US states' traditional Medicare. The AI deciding what care gets *paid for* will shape practice more quietly, and more powerfully, than any AI deciding what care gets *given* — and almost nobody is watching that one.
