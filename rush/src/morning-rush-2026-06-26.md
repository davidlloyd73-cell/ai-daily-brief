*A short brief on AI in medicine — and the world it's moving through. Read it before the 8am phone lines open.*

## 1. An AI that reads your ECG and finds the hidden heart disease

Pathway Labs has won what it calls the world's first FDA clearance for a multi-condition cardiology AI: EchoNext, a model that reads a standard 12-lead ECG and flags structural heart disease the trace was never meant to reveal — left- and right-sided failure, valve disease, the severe hypertrophy that hints at infiltrative cardiomyopathy, and pulmonary hypertension. Trained on more than 700,000 ECG–echocardiogram pairs at NewYork-Presbyterian, it outperformed cardiologists, including cardiologists already using AI help. It clears for six indications and arrives bolted onto OpenEvidence, the decision-support tool roughly half of US clinicians already use.

**The view from the surgery:** This is the kind of AI I actually want — not a chatbot with opinions, but a cheap, ubiquitous test quietly doing more than it was designed to. Every surgery has an ECG machine and a drawer full of "non-specific" traces. If a model can pull a silent valve lesion out of one of those before the patient turns blue on the stairs, that is real screening, not theatre. The catch is the referral avalanche: find disease earlier and you find more of it, and someone has to scan, see, and reassure all those newly-flagged patients.

## 2. The scribe that examined a patient who was never examined

Ontario's auditor general has published an unflattering audit of the province's AI medical transcriber, finding it hallucinated, produced incomplete notes, and was rolled out without adequate evaluation. The documented failures are the ones that should make any clinician's stomach drop: examinations recorded that never took place, diagnoses conjured from nothing, medication names and doses garbled — drug errors alone accounting for nearly a fifth of safety-related complaints in parallel end-user studies. Headline error rates of 1–3% sound reassuring until you remember what the 3% contains.

**Why it matters:** The NHS has just committed half a million staff to this exact class of tool, and the saving is real — I use one, it gives me back my evenings. But the audit is a reminder that the note is a legal and clinical document, not a convenience. The discipline is unglamorous: read what the machine wrote before you sign it, every time, because the one consultation you rubber-stamp on autopilot is the one where it invented a chest examination you never did.

## 3. Your face is now selling supplements you've never heard of

The American Medical Association has escalated AI deepfakes of doctors to a "public health and safety crisis", with chief executive John Whyte demanding lawmakers close the identity-protection gaps. The trigger is a wave of synthetic videos: real, trusted physicians made to hawk dubious cures and supplements they have never touched — one prominent US doctor discovered a fleet of fake versions of himself this month. It is not confined to social media either; a recent *Radiology* study found most clinicians could not reliably spot a deepfaked X-ray.

**The view:** This is the mirror image of every AI-in-medicine story we usually run. The threat is not the machine in the consulting room but the machine impersonating the clinician outside it — eroding the one asset general practice still trades on, which is that a patient believes the doctor in front of them is real. When a faked GP can out-produce a real one, "I saw it from a doctor online" stops meaning anything, and the debunking lands back on our desks.

## 4. Someone has finally counted the AI economy

Azeem Azhar's *Exponential View* this week published the first bottom-up, deduplicated measurement of the generative-AI economy: $110bn in real customer sales over the past twelve months, on an annualised run rate now north of $175bn. The point of the exercise is the honesty of the accounting — no double-counting of the same dollar as it sloshes between model-makers, cloud providers and apps. The conclusion is bracingly two-handed: bigger and faster-growing than any prior technology wave, yet still small enough to be early, and barely covering its own colossal infrastructure bill.

**Why it matters:** Every hype cycle needs someone willing to add up the actual receipts, and this is that. For those of us watching the NHS sign nine-figure AI deals, the useful takeaway is that the whole global market is real but not yet self-sustaining — which means today's free or subsidised tools are being priced to win share, not to break even. Enjoy the introductory rate; plan for the renewal.

## 5. Washington pulls two frontier models off the shelf overnight

In a striking demonstration of where the real AI levers sit, the US government issued an export-control directive ordering Anthropic to suspend all access to its Fable 5 and Mythos 5 models for any foreign national — anywhere in the world, including the company's own foreign-national staff. Anthropic complied within hours, disabling the models for everyone, while publicly disputing the logic: the stated concern was a narrow "jailbreak", and the firm argues that recalling a model used by hundreds of millions over one such flaw would, applied evenly, freeze the entire industry.

**The view:** This is the story the medical-AI world should be watching most closely and is watching least. The tools the NHS is busy embedding sit on exactly these foundation models, and a directive in Washington can switch one off before breakfast in Harrow. Sovereignty stopped being a slide in a strategy deck the moment a single letter took a frontier model dark — and it is a reminder to ask, of any clinical AI you adopt, what happens on the morning the supply is cut.

### From the Eye

**Item one:** while everyone debates AI in the consulting room, America has quietly let it into the payments office — Medicare's WISeR pilot has been live since January across six states, using AI plus human review to wave through or block prior-authorisation requests on thirteen "wasteful" service categories; cardiology bodies are now lobbying hard against any expansion, having seen which way the algorithm leans. **Item two:** the MHRA closed phase two of its AI Airlock sandbox in May — seven innovators spanning ambient note-taking, cancer diagnostics and eye-disease detection, backed by £3.6m over three years — with a "lessons learned" report due this summer that will quietly shape how every AI device in the NHS gets licensed, and which almost nobody will read. **Item three:** and the number to keep by the screen — an LLM-assisted review in *Nature Medicine* counted 4,609 studies of language models in clinical medicine since 2022, roughly 3.2 a day, of which just 1,048 used real patient data and a grand total of 19 were prospective randomised trials. A literature a mile wide and an inch deep.
