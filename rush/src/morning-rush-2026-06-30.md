## 1. The trial that says AI didn't speed up the cancer pathway

LungIMPACT, just out in *Nature Medicine*, is the kind of study we should have demanded before the cheques were written. Across five NHS trusts and 93,326 primary-care chest X-rays, radiographs were randomised to either immediate AI prioritisation or the standard queue. The AI did exactly what it promised at the reporting step — median time from film to radiologist report fell from 47 hours to 34. And yet the thing that matters to the patient didn't move at all: median time to CT was 53 days in *both* arms. Faster reads, identical pathway.

**The view from the surgery:** The most quietly important item this month, precisely because it's negative. Government has just put £20m behind AI chest X-ray tools for every English trust, and here is a proper randomised trial saying the bottleneck was never the radiologist's reading speed — it's everything downstream: CT capacity, clinic slots, the human pathway. AI shaved a step and left the queue exactly where it was. Buy the tool by all means, but don't let anyone sell it as a cancer-pathway accelerator until they've shown you the days, not the hours.

## 2. Half a million NHS staff start getting Copilot this month

The first wave of NHS England's Microsoft 365 Copilot rollout begins in July — roughly 80,000 licences to start, building to all 505,000 staff by October. It follows a trial across some 90 organisations and 30,000 workers that claimed an average saving of 43 minutes a day, pitched as the largest single software rollout in the health service since the electronic patient record. Processing is confined to UK Azure regions, with audit and transparency commitments attached; unions and privacy groups have flagged the obvious worry about a black box near the record.

**Why it matters:** Note what this is and isn't. Copilot is general office AI — the email, the meeting summary, the spreadsheet — not a clinical tool, and the 43-minute figure is self-report, which flatters. The risk isn't the technology; it's the indemnity and governance grey zone the moment someone leans on a generic assistant for something that drifts from admin into advice. The GMC line is unchanged — the doctor owns the decision — but standard indemnity may not.

## 3. The scribe that watches as well as listens

A study in *npj Digital Medicine* tested whether giving an ambient scribe *vision* — letting it see the consultation, not just hear it — cuts the errors we keep complaining about. Using simulated medication histories, the vision-enabled scribes left out fewer details than audio-only systems, the omissions being exactly the failure mode that worries clinicians: the drug mentioned but not captured, the dose said but not written.

**The view:** A welcome counterpoint to a fortnight of scribe horror stories. If multimodal capture genuinely reduces omissions, that's a real safety gain, not a marketing one. But hold the champagne — this was simulated histories, not a noisy real surgery with three people talking over a crying toddler, and "sees the room" raises its own consent and data questions. Promising direction; not yet evidence you'd hang a note on.

## 4. America's flagship AI law dies on its own birthday

Today, 30 June, was the day Colorado's Artificial Intelligence Act — the first comprehensive US state AI law, covering "consequential decisions" in employment, housing, healthcare and education — was finally meant to take effect. Instead, Governor Polis signed SB 189 last month, gutting it and pushing it to January 2027: out go the duty of care against algorithmic discrimination, the mandatory impact assessments, and much of the deployer paperwork. HIPAA-covered entities are now largely exempt.

**The view from the surgery:** A small but telling story. The most-watched experiment in American AI regulation got quietly hollowed out under industry pressure and a federal mood hostile to state rules — and almost nobody noticed because there was no launch to report, only a non-event. For us across the Atlantic, it's a reminder that "we'll regulate the harms" is easy to legislate and easy to repeal, and that the gap between a statute's ambition and what survives the lobbying is where the real policy lives.

## 5. Moore's Law wasn't fast enough

Azeem Azhar's *Exponential View* this week (#580) makes a deceptively simple argument: the fifty-year miracle of Moore's Law — compute doubling on schedule — still hasn't kept pace with what modern AI demands. That mismatch is the real story behind the eye-watering capital flooding into chips and data centres, and behind a frontier turning "agentic," with knock-on promise in drug discovery and elsewhere.

**Why it matters:** It reframes the bubble debate. The capex looks insane only if you assume the hardware was keeping up; Azhar's point is that it wasn't, so the appetite for raw compute is structural, not a fad. For health, the question isn't whether the chips get built — it's whether any of that compute ends up serving a patient outcome rather than a clinician's inbox.

### From the Eye

**Item one** — Brussels' new red line: the EU's Digital Omnibus, expected to be formally adopted in July, keeps 2 August live as the day the Commission gains penalty powers over general-purpose model providers and transparency duties switch on — while quietly bolting on a fresh Article 5 prohibition against AI systems that generate non-consensual intimate imagery or child sexual abuse material. The high-risk obligations, meanwhile, slip to December 2027; enforcement of the easy bits arrives first, the hard bits last. **Item two** — your genome, meet your GP record: 23andMe's research arm is reportedly partnering with HealthEx to let users link their genetic data to their medical records for a "complete" health profile — a proposition that sounds convenient until you ask who holds the join, and what a future buyer of either dataset could do with it. **Item three** — the gig workers get a rulebook: governments spent early June in Geneva negotiating the ILO's first *binding* global standard for platform work, the crux being whether people managed entirely by an app must be treated as employees — the unglamorous labour story that will shape how algorithmic management is allowed to behave long after the chatbots stop being news.
