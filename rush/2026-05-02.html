THE MORNING RUSH

A short brief on AI in medicine — and the world it's moving through. Read it before the 8am phone lines open.

Saturday 2 May 2026 · ~5 min read · Issue 005

On the line today: An Uppsala-led survey says a quarter of UK GPs now use generative AI in clinical work and 95% have had no training; the Pentagon signs eight frontier-AI suppliers and pointedly leaves Anthropic out; the EU AI Act trilogue collapses with medical devices the sticking point; the MHRA's AI Airlock gets its first three-year settlement; and a BMJ paper finds diagnostic LLMs leaving up to 97% of clinical guidelines unmentioned. Plus — From the Eye — three quieter items including a fake eye disease that fooled four chatbots.

1. A quarter of GPs are using GenAI; ninety-five per cent have had no training

An Uppsala University-led survey of more than 1,000 UK GPs, picked up by Pulse this week, finds 25% are now using generative AI tools in clinical practice — up from 20% in 2024 — and 95% report no professional training in their use. The authors call adoption "fragmented and unsupervised" and call on regulators and the royal colleges for guidelines, training and a clear regulatory frame. The paper lands one month after NHSE's 31 March guidance on safe ambient scribing — which puts validation of every output squarely on the clinician.

The view from the surgery: This will not surprise anyone signing rotas in NW London — the only unfamiliar bit is the proportion. A quarter is a lot of decision-support, draft-letter-writing and silent summarisation happening across the workforce with no audit, no shared SOP and no liability map. The colleges' current statements are a position, not a curriculum. That gap has now been measured.

2. Pentagon names eight AI partners — and leaves Anthropic out

The Department of Defense announced on 1 May that it had signed agreements with seven Big Tech suppliers — OpenAI, Google, Microsoft, Amazon, Nvidia, SpaceX and Reflection — to deploy frontier models on classified networks. Anthropic was not on the list. Pentagon CTO Emil Michael told CNBC that Anthropic remains designated a "supply chain risk" — a label normally reserved for foreign-adversary firms — over its refusal to license Claude for fully autonomous weapons or domestic surveillance. He simultaneously called Anthropic's restricted Mythos model a "national security moment", which is its own paragraph of tension.

Why it matters: A US administration is now treating an American frontier lab's safety policy as a procurement disqualification. The precedent travels. UK MoD and NHS procurement should be watching: if the model with the strongest safety guarantees is also the one a major customer can punish for saying no, the safety case for any vendor's product has just become more political and less technical.

3. Brussels stalls on the AI Act — and medical devices are the bone

The political trilogue on the AI Act Omnibus collapsed on 28 April after twelve hours, with the unresolved file being how the Act interacts with sectoral safety law for AI sitting inside machinery, toys and — crucially for us — medical devices and IVDs. Parliament wants a carve-out so AI inside an MDR-regulated product doesn't sit under both regimes; the Council does not. A further trilogue is set for 13 May. Without an Omnibus, the 2 August 2026 high-risk obligations remain legally in force — three months out.

The view: UK trusts and ICBs procuring AI as a medical device have been quietly planning around the assumption a delay would land. The honest answer this morning is that it might not. Anyone sourcing software-as-a-medical-device with a CE-marked dependency should reread the contract's regulatory-change clause before 13 May. The suppliers will already be reading theirs.

4. The MHRA AI Airlock finally gets a multi-year settlement

DHSC has confirmed £1.2m a year for three years (2026–29) for the MHRA AI Airlock, the regulatory sandbox for AI as a medical device. The £3.6m total ends the annual funding cycle that has been the loudest complaint from the programme team and from applicants. Phase 2 reporting is due this summer; the Phase 3 design and a fresh applications call follow later in the year. The Airlock is run jointly with the NHS AI team, DHSC and Team AB.

Why it matters: Three years of certainty is what lets a sandbox test things that take three years to test — change-control plans, post-market surveillance for retraining, the scenarios that one-year contracts strangle in the cradle. A small line of money doing disproportionate work, and the Tallon-Denniston Commission webinar on 20 May will lean on it.

5. BMJ paper finds diagnostic LLMs leaving guidelines on the cutting-room floor

A study in BMJ Health Care Informatics on 24 April evaluated 12,197 diagnostic outputs from GPT-4.1 and DeepSeek-V3 against clinical guidelines for hypercholesterolaemia and type-2 diabetes. Omission rates ran up to 97% for DeepSeek-V3 and 46% for GPT-4.1; hallucinations up to 9%; and the guideline-citation rate swung from 0% to 78% depending on the case vignette's geography. Sex and ethnicity barely moved the dial; patient location moved it sharply.

The view from the surgery: The pattern matters more than the headline number. These are not random errors — they are systematic gaps that shift with patient context in ways no clinician will notice in the 90 seconds they spend on the AI's draft. For QOF-touched chronic disease management, this is the failure mode to design around: it is not what the model says, it is what it quietly leaves out.

From the Eye

Three things humming under the front pages. A team at Gothenburg invented a fake eye disease called "bixonimania" — periorbital pigmentation, supposedly caused by blue light — planted preprints crediting the Sideshow Bob Foundation and the Starfleet Academy, and watched ChatGPT, Gemini, Copilot and Perplexity confidently describe the condition as real (Perplexity invented an incidence of one in 90,000). A paper in Cureus has already been retracted for citing the bogus references; the authors' point is that LLMs hallucinate more when text looks professionally medical than when it looks like a tweet. At Pulse LIVE London on 28-29 April, Baroness Clare Gerada told the room GPs "can't be frightened of AI" — and disclosed that on the standard "spot the AI letter against a GP's" test, she went from 100% three years ago to 0% on the rerun. And Nathaniel Whittemore's first AI Lab Power Rankings dropped on the AI Daily Brief on 29 April, scoring OpenAI, Anthropic, Google, Microsoft, Amazon, Meta, xAI and Apple across compute, enterprise, platforms, models, momentum and X-factor. Worth a subscription if only because the order of the table will probably be a clinical-AI procurement signal by Q3.
