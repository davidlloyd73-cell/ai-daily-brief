1. The BMA calls the draft workforce plan what it is — a gamble on AI

The British Medical Association, responding to leaked detail of the NHS England 10-Year Workforce Plan due in June, has condemned its assumption that artificial intelligence will let the service get by with fewer doctors. Dr Amit Kochhar, the BMA's workforce lead, used the phrase "massive, dangerous gamble" — the language is not throwaway. The draft envisages AI substituting for clinical and admin roles in instances where "technology can completely substitute for a role", and previews "My NHS GP", an AI triage tool to be baked into the NHS App by 2028, pitched as an alternative both to A&E and to a GP appointment. The whole plan lands on the desk of James Murray, two weeks into the brief after Wes Streeting's resignation.

The view from the surgery: Helpful to see this said plainly. Ambient scribing buys back ten minutes a consultation; what it does not do is take the eleventh patient. A workforce strategy that books the productivity gain twice — once to clinicians, once to the Treasury — is a budget exercise, not a clinical one. Murray's first real choice in the brief is whether to soften the substitution language before publication, or defend it.

2. Cambridgeshire's MSK clinic — the proof-of-concept GP commissioners will read

Cambridgeshire Community Services NHS Trust, with Flok Health, has won the HSJ Digital Awards 2026 prize for Driving Change through AI and Automation for the first large-scale AI-supported musculoskeletal clinic in England. The numbers: 2,500 patients seen between February and May 2025 for back pain; over 98% managed entirely through Flok's CQC-registered, AI-led physiotherapy app — without an in-person appointment. Onward referral rates were low; patient-reported outcomes held up against the conventional pathway.

Why it matters: MSK is the right pilot — roughly a fifth of GP consultations, much of it self-limiting, and Flok is a regulated provider, so the indemnity question is answered. The model is not "AI advises a doctor"; it is "AI delivers the care, a clinician supervises the cohort". That is a structurally different proposition from the scribe, and one PCNs will be asked to commission within the year. Worth a careful read before saying yes.

3. Anthropic closes in on a $900bn valuation — and flips the table

Anthropic is expected to close its $30bn-plus round as early as this week at a pre-money valuation above $900bn, co-led by Sequoia, Dragoneer, Greenoaks and Altimeter, each putting in around $2bn. If the round signs at the top of the range, the lab surpasses OpenAI's $852bn March valuation for the first time. Anthropic also expects Q2 revenue to more than double to $10.9bn, hitting a $50bn annualised run-rate by end-June, with its first quarterly operating profit landing two years ahead of internal plan.

The view: The flip matters less for bragging rights than for what it does to procurement leverage. Two trillion-dollar labs with different safety postures, different pricing structures and competing infrastructure deals — Microsoft on one side, SpaceX on the other — means the next NHS or trust contract is being negotiated against a genuinely two-horse market for the first time. The buyer side has not adjusted its assumptions.

4. The Trump administration aims ChatGPT at the Medicaid audit pile

The US Department of Health and Human Services has confirmed it is using ChatGPT and other LLMs to ingest the recurring single-audit reports from all 50 states that document how federal Medicaid and public-health dollars are spent — looking for fraud, waste and improper payments. CMS says it now flags suspicious claims with AI "every day", and the administration is positioning AI as the operational backbone of its fraud crackdown.

Why it matters: This is the first sustained use of a general-purpose LLM by a federal department against its own oversight pipeline. The defensible case — pattern-spotting at scale across documents no human could read — is real. The risk is that a model trained mostly on internet text starts adjudicating rather than triaging claims it does not understand. Worth watching as a template: if it works there, NHS counter-fraud will not be far behind.

5. The pharmacist cohort that lands in September — and the AI behind them

From September 2026 every newly qualified pharmacist in Great Britain will register as an independent prescriber on day one — the first cohort to do so, with around 3,000 a year joining a profession whose pathfinder sites have already done over 33,000 consultations, 59% of which resulted in prescribing interventions that would otherwise have bounced to a GP or hospital. A national community pharmacy prescribing service is in negotiation from this spring. The AI piece, quietly: pilot studies of AI-enabled clinical consultation tools in community pharmacy report 15–20 minutes saved per consultation and structured documentation aligned to NHS requirements.

The view from the surgery: This is the workforce shift the workforce plan should be honest about. The substitution that will actually happen in primary care over the next two years is not chatbot-for-GP — it is pharmacist-prescriber with an AI consultation tool taking the minor-illness slice. Defensible if the supervision and feedback loops are built; a hospital-pass if they aren't.

From the Eye

The ICO redraws the security incident. The Information Commissioner's mid-May five-step guide on AI-powered cyber threats added deepfakes, prompt injection and data poisoning to the list of things organisations must consider in a data-protection impact assessment under Article 32 — quietly making "the AI did it" a reportable category. Any practice running an ambient scribe should be checking its DPIA names these explicitly; few do yet. The lab that didn't make the list. When the Pentagon last week confirmed the eight firms cleared to deploy AI on its classified networks — Amazon, Google, Microsoft, OpenAI, SpaceX, Nvidia, Reflection and Oracle — the conspicuous absence was Anthropic, still in court with the Trump administration after being branded a national-security risk earlier this year. Worth marking the moment the AI procurement map split along political lines. The presidential mobile that leaked. A flaw on the Trump Mobile preorder site quietly exposed the names, addresses, phone numbers and email addresses of around 27,000 customers of the gold-coloured T1 handset — small in volume, considerable in irony, and a tidy reminder that the AI-fraud era still loses to a poorly configured signup form.
