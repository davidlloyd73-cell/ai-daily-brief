THE MORNING RUSH

A short brief on AI in medicine — and the world it's moving through. Read it before the 8am phone lines open.

Saturday 27 June 2026 · ~5 min read · Issue 059

On the line today: The Treasury has found £28m to put AI chest X-ray tools in every English trust and to pilot six more technologies across thirteen sites — money where the mouth has been. The psychiatrists, sensibly, are slamming the brakes on ambient scribes for the patients who can least afford a hallucinated note. At BIO 2026 the drug-discovery crowd are quietly declaring that the AI-designed molecule has stopped being a slide and started being a Phase II read-out. On the wider stage, OpenAI has let Washington decide who may use its newest model, and the EU's enforcement clock on general-purpose AI runs out on 2 August. From the Eye: Alibaba's 29 million stolen conversations, America's AI statute-book splintering state by state, and Brussels reaching for the cloud.

1. £28m, and this time it buys X-rays

The government has committed £20m to put AI chest X-ray tools into every NHS trust in England by 2029, aimed squarely at speeding up suspected lung-cancer diagnosis and the follow-up that so often stalls after the first scan. Alongside it sits a smaller but more interesting £8.1m to pilot six AI and digital technologies across thirteen NHS sites, targeting faster care for heart failure, stroke and lung cancer. After a year of strategy decks and half-million-staff Copilot headlines, this is comparatively modest, concrete money attached to a specific clinical pathway rather than a vague promise of "freed-up time".

The view from the surgery: Lung cancer is exactly where this should land — too many of my referrals turn on a chest film read days late by an overstretched department, and the gap between the abnormal film and the someone-actually-acting-on-it is where patients are lost. AI triage of plain radiographs is one of the few genuinely mature use-cases, well past the demo stage. The worry, as ever, is the back end: flag more nodules and you need the CT slots, the MDTs and the respiratory clinics to absorb them, or you have simply moved the queue upstream.

2. The psychiatrists put the brakes on the scribe

The Royal College of Psychiatrists and NHS England confirmed last month that they are jointly developing guidance on ambient voice technology for people with mental health needs, learning disabilities or who are neurodivergent — with the document not expected until September. The honesty of the move is welcome: ambient scribes are already in widespread clinical use, including in psychiatry, well ahead of any setting-specific rules. The College's implicit point is that a tool trained on tidy ten-minute physical-medicine consultations behaves very differently when fed a disjointed, distressed, hour-long psychiatric history.

Why it matters: This is the right instinct applied to the right population. A garbled medication dose in a routine review is dangerous; a misattributed statement of intent, or a hallucinated denial of suicidal thoughts, in a mental-health record is catastrophic and medico-legally radioactive. The uncomfortable truth is that the guidance is arriving after the technology, not before — a familiar NHS sequence — and "we are writing the rules" is doing a lot of work while the recordings keep being made.

3. AI's first drugs stop being a promise

At BIO 2026 this week the drug-discovery field has, in effect, declared a turning point: the AI-designed molecule has moved from speculation to clinical evidence. The anchor remains Insilico Medicine's rentosertib, a TNIK inhibitor for idiopathic pulmonary fibrosis whose Phase IIa results — a 98mL lung-function improvement against a placebo decline — were published in Nature Medicine last year. What makes it more than a curiosity is the provenance: the AI proposed the target from multi-omics data and then designed the compound, rather than a chemist using AI as a fancier calculator. The convention's subtext was geopolitical — much of this momentum is Chinese, and the BIOSECURE Act has not slowed the science.

The view: Worth keeping a sense of proportion. No AI-discovered drug has yet been approved, one Phase IIa does not make a pharmacopoeia, and IPF is a long way from the consulting room. But the direction of travel matters for prescribers downstream: the pipeline feeding the drugs I will hand out in a decade is already being shaped by models, not just medicinal chemists, and the cost and speed advantages are real enough that the trend will not reverse.

4. OpenAI lets Washington pick who gets the new model

In a striking echo of the export-control drama that took Anthropic's frontier models dark, OpenAI this week unveiled three new models — GPT-5.6 Sol, Terra and Luna — and agreed to a White House request to limit the rollout to a small group of government-approved "trusted partners". The stated concern is the usual one: capabilities, particularly in cybersecurity, deemed too potent for an open launch. The pattern is now unmistakable — the most advanced models are increasingly released at the pleasure of the US government rather than the market.

Why it matters: For anyone embedding these tools in clinical workflows, the lesson from a fortnight of headlines is sovereignty. The foundation models under NHS-facing AI now sit behind a political gate in Washington that can be opened or shut overnight, for reasons that have nothing to do with medicine. When you adopt a clinical AI, the question is no longer just "is it safe and accurate" but "what happens to it the morning a directive lands".

5. Brussels' enforcement clock runs out on 2 August

The grace period is ending. From 2 August 2026 the European Commission's supervision and enforcement powers over providers of general-purpose AI models come into force — a year after the obligations themselves applied. From that date, documentation requirements, copyright-compliance policies, training-data summaries, adversarial testing and systemic-risk assessment for the largest models stop being aspirational and become enforceable, with real penalties attached. Providers that have signed up to the Commission's code of practice get a lighter-touch monitoring path; everyone else faces direct scrutiny.

The view: The UK sits outside this regime, but no British clinician should assume it is irrelevant. The major models the NHS relies on are global products, and EU rules tend to set the floor for how they are built and documented everywhere — the Brussels effect by another name. It is also a quiet rebuke to the Anglo-American "move fast" posture: while Washington gatekeeps by decree, Brussels is doing it by statute, on a published timetable.

From the Eye

Item one: the model-theft story nobody is calling theft loudly enough — Anthropic has written to US senators and the White House accusing Alibaba of running the largest known "distillation" campaign against Claude, some 25,000 fake accounts generating 28.8 million conversations between April and June to harvest the model's reasoning, more than the three previous Chinese labs combined; the quiet implication is that frontier capability now leaks faster than it can be guarded. Item two: America's AI rulebook is fracturing into fifty — in a single week Rhode Island's governor signed a ban treating AI-delivered psychotherapy as practising without a licence (plus a clinical-AI disclosure law), California sent a bill banning AI public-school teachers to the governor's desk, and Arizona's governor vetoed all three AI bills his legislature passed; the patchwork is becoming the policy. Item three: while everyone watches the chatbots, Brussels has published its Cloud and AI Development Act — a bid to wean European hospitals and governments off American hyperscalers and onto sovereign infrastructure, the unglamorous plumbing that will decide, long after the model wars, whose servers your patient data actually sits on.
