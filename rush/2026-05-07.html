1. Palantir's NHS platform gets a "clean bill of health" — but the break clause is still in the diary

Health minister Zubir Ahmed today, 7 May, issued a written response to the Westminster Hall debate of 16 April, pronouncing the federated data platform fit for purpose and noting that all 240 NHS trusts and integrated care systems are now in scope. NISTA — the new National Infrastructure and Service Transformation Authority — has rated the £330m programme "green" and put projected benefits at £777m. None of that quietens the underlying argument: the same minister has confirmed the break clause next spring is real, that Patrick Vallance has promised to "do something very different" once it bites, and the BMA's resistance to Palantir handling primary-care data is still on the record. The Lib Dems' Martin Wrigley — who pulled the original debate — says the platform "only benefits a quarter" of its user organisations.

The view from the surgery: This is the dance the FDP has been doing for two years. Whitehall says the contract is performing; the field says the user experience is grim and the data flows are opaque; the minister says "trust us, we have a break clause"; the BMA says "trust us, we won't use it". Worth reading the small print of which AI tools NISTA is rating green — theatre scheduling, the Demand Centre, AI discharge summaries — because those are the bits that will land in trust IT roadmaps long before the contract decision is made.

2. Bradford turns on a three-year AI skin-cancer service

Bradford Teaching Hospitals NHS Foundation Trust went live with an AI dermatology service at St Luke's Hospital on 30 April, with a three-year operational contract. Two-week-wait skin-cancer referrals are imaged at the front door, with the AI giving a same-visit benign/malignant probability and dermatology consultant sign-off on every "concerning" case. Bradford projects an average diagnostic interval cut from weeks to days for the c.7,000 annual two-week referrals it handles.

Why it matters: Skin cancer two-week-wait is one of the few clinical pathways where the AI economics genuinely stack up — a high-volume, low-acuity gate where photographs are the substrate and a sensitivity-tuned model is in fact safer than the current "GP refers, six-week wait, dermatologist looks at it" loop. Other trusts will follow; the question for primary care is who's going to do the patient consent conversation when 70% of the Lyrebird/Heidi-equipped surgeries don't have a dermatology imaging device in the building.

3. The MHRA AI Commission's inbox is open — and 770 clinicians have written in

The MHRA's National Commission on the Regulation of AI in Healthcare closed its call for evidence in February with 770 responses, and the secretariat is now in synthesis mode ahead of the summer report. The early-read piece doing the rounds — Resultsense, 6 May — argues the consensus is reformist rather than revolutionary: clinicians want a register of deployed systems, executive sponsorship for AI committees, quarterly board-level surveillance reporting, and tighter alignment between MHRA, GMC and CQC — not a new freestanding regulator. The Commission has a public webinar on Wednesday 20 May.

The view: This is the more grown-up cousin of the EU AI Act conversation — same problem, different national temperament. If summer 2026 actually delivers a register, an audit cycle and a single front-door for AI-as-medical-device, the UK's much-mocked "no AI Bill" position will have produced something more usable than Brussels' eight-month conformity-assessment fight (see story 5). The risk, as ever, is delivery — three-letter agencies in summer have a poor track record.

4. Anthropic ships Opus 4.7 — and pushes Claude into Excel, PowerPoint and Outlook

On 5 May Anthropic released Claude Opus 4.7 alongside ten pre-built finance agents, a "Claude for Excel" add-in that builds models from raw filings and audits formulas, and a new Moody's MCP that pipes credit data on 600 million companies straight into Claude's reasoning. Vals AI's Finance Agent benchmark now scores Opus 4.7 at 64.4%, top of the leaderboard. FIS has built an anti-money-laundering investigation agent on it; BMO and Amalgamated are the first announced deployments.

Why it matters: The plumbing matters more than the model. Microsoft 365 is the actual operating system of NHS administration — the Excel-and-PowerPoint workflow that produces every business case, every pay-and-rations report, every committee paper. An Anthropic agent that lives inside the same tenancy as your trust's M365 instance is a different procurement question to a "ChatGPT pilot". Information governance leads who waved through M365 in 2022 are about to discover what they actually agreed to.

5. The EU AI Act Omnibus — what really broke the trilogue was medical devices

The 28 April trilogue on the Digital AI Omnibus didn't fail over the headline 16-month deferral of high-risk obligations. It failed over Annex I — the conformity-assessment architecture for AI embedded in already-regulated products: medical devices, industrial machinery and connected vehicles. Parliament wants such AI to remain firmly in AI Act scope; the Council wants sectoral primacy (i.e. the existing MDR/IVDR regime to absorb the AI conformity assessment). The next sitting is Wednesday 13 May, with the Cypriot Presidency racing to close the file before its term ends 30 June. If they fail, Lithuania picks it up — and the 2 August 2026 deadline stays in force untouched.

The view from the surgery: This is the file that determines whether ArteraAI Prostate or a Lyrebird-class scribe gets one or two conformity certificates to enter the EU market. Anything that prolongs the regulatory limbo benefits incumbents and punishes new entrants. Watch which side of the Annex I fight the UK aligns to in MHRA's International Reliance Framework, due Autumn 2026.

From the Eye

Three quieter items working their way through the foothills. A fake disease called "bixonimania" — invented by Almira Osmanovic Thunström specifically to embarrass language models — was cheerfully diagnosed by ChatGPT and Copilot and even cited as a real condition by three researchers at the Maharishi Markandeshwar Institute in a Cureus paper that has since been retracted; the giveaway, beyond the ridiculous name, was the lead author's affiliation with "Asteria Horizon University, Nova City, California" and an acknowledgement to "Professor Maria Bohm at The Starfleet Academy on board the USS Enterprise". Italian PM Giorgia Meloni on 5 May denounced an AI-generated image of her in lingerie circulating on Telegram — the image itself is forgettable, but it's the first sitting head of government this year to sue under the Italian deepfake-personality statute, and the case will set the European tort line. And Norway has just bought an entire country's worth of ambient scribing in one tender — a single national contract worth approximately €15 million signed at the end of 2025, dispensing with hospital-level procurement entirely. The NHS England equivalent, by contrast, is still 42 ICBs running 42 mini-procurements on the AVT registry. Worth knowing which model produces a working national service first.
