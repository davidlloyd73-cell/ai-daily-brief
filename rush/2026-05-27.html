THE MORNING RUSH

A short brief on AI in medicine — and the world it's moving through. Read it before the 8am phone lines open.

Wednesday 27 May 2026 · ~5 min read · Issue 029

On the line today: Sam Altman and Dario Amodei spent yesterday quietly burying the AI "jobs apocalypse" they were forecasting twelve months ago — with the Yale Budget Lab data backing them up. Utah's Doctronic experiment publishes its first numbers, and the agreement rate is the one to anchor on. The LMC conference demands state-backed indemnity be extended to cover AI triage failures, putting Crown Indemnity squarely on the hook for the next My NHS GP misfire. Pope Leo XIV makes AI the centrepiece of his first encyclical. And Azeem Azhar flags a compute crunch nobody is pricing in. Plus From the Eye — the watermarking deadline that just got cut in half, Gemma 4's quiet arrival, and the NHS GitHub repos going private.

1. Altman and Amodei walk back the jobs apocalypse

OpenAI's Sam Altman, speaking to CBA's Matt Comyn yesterday, said he was "pretty wrong" about AI's near-term labour market impact and "delighted to be wrong". Anthropic's Dario Amodei, who twelve months ago claimed AI could eliminate half of entry-level white-collar jobs, has shifted similarly — automation, he now says, may "actually expand the work people do". The data has caught up: the Yale Budget Lab finds no significant change in occupational mix or unemployment duration in high-AI-exposure jobs since ChatGPT launched. Goldman Sachs puts the net drag at 16,000 fewer payroll additions per month and 0.1 points on unemployment — measurable, but a long way short of apocalypse.

The view from the surgery: A useful reset. Both men are talking their books — Anthropic is closing a $30bn round and OpenAI is positioning for an IPO, and "AI will gut the workforce" is a harder pitch to underwriters than "AI will augment it". But the empirical point stands: eighteen months of mass deployment has produced productivity gains and few redundancies. The NHS workforce plan should take note. Substitution is the wrong frame; reallocation is the right one.

2. Utah's Doctronic pilot publishes early numbers

STAT's Health Tech newsletter reports the first data from Utah's regulatory-sandbox pilot of Doctronic, the autonomous AI authorised since January to renew prescriptions for around 190 chronic-disease medications. In phase one, with every decision reviewed by a physician, the AI granted the renewal 72% of the time without escalating; in the 28% it kicked up — usually for missing labs — the doctor agreed with the AI's call in 91% of cases. Controlled drugs, injectables and short-course antibiotics are excluded. The Utah Medical Licensing Board has asked the state to halt the pilot, having learned of it only after launch.

Why it matters: First agentic-AI clinical deployment with public outcome data — and 91% concordance is the number any UK proposal, including My NHS GP, will be measured against. The medico-legal angle is the interesting one: when the AI grants a refill and the prescriber agrees three days later, who is actually deciding? Worth thinking through before the same shape of pilot lands here.

3. LMCs demand Crown Indemnity covers AI triage failures

At the LMC Conference last week, GP leaders passed a resolution demanding the state-backed Clinical Negligence Scheme for General Practice be extended to cover claims arising from AI triage failures, vicarious liability for AI tools the practice did not specify, and data-sharing breaches flowing from AI deployments. The motion comes after months of warnings from MDU, MDDUS and MPS that current contracts leave the clinician carrying the liability while vendors cap exposure at the licence fee.

The view: Harder for Treasury to ignore than another workforce paper. If the government wants AI triage in the NHS App by 2028 — the working assumption in the leaked workforce plan — it has to underwrite the downside. Asking salaried GPs to carry vicarious liability for tools they did not commission is the kind of arrangement that quietly empties the profession.

4. Pope Leo XIV makes AI the centrepiece of his first encyclical

Pope Leo XIV signed Magnifica Humanitas on 15 May — the 135th anniversary of Leo XIII's Rerum Novarum — and its reception filled the bank holiday weekend. The document warns AI risks concentrating power, distorting truth, reshaping labour and "deepening risks in warfare", and calls for "adequate regulatory tools" while insisting technology is not inherently evil. The framing — labour, dignity, justice, peace — is the most explicit attempt by a major moral institution to set the terms of the AI policy debate.

Why it matters: Less for what it changes tomorrow than for what it now licenses. Catholic hospital systems run a meaningful chunk of US healthcare and a fair slice of European, with a long tail beyond. A papal encyclical pinning AI procurement to human dignity gives medical ethics committees a clear reference point they did not have last week. Watch for the language to surface in NHS Confederation framing within the month.

5. The compute crunch nobody is pricing in

In Exponential View this week, Azeem Azhar flags that Nvidia B200 rental prices have risen 114% in six weeks, with the per-hour premium over the older H200 up more than sixfold. Lightning AI reports forty customers chasing 400,000 GPUs — ten times its current fleet. Frontier model releases are pulling premium customers onto the newest silicon faster than supply can absorb.

The view from the surgery: Every NHS AI-scribe contract assumes a stable token price. If frontier compute keeps tightening, the unit cost of inference will harden through 2026 — possibly faster than procurement frameworks can adjust. Practices renewing licences this summer should ask their vendor what the compute hedge looks like, and read the price-review clause closely.

From the Eye

The watermarking date that quietly halved. Inside the EU AI Act omnibus deal agreed on 7 May, the Article 50 generative-AI watermarking obligation lands on 2 December 2026 — three months from publication, not the six originally written. The harmonised standard isn't finished, so providers will be implementing against guidance that does not yet exist; expect an autumn scramble that catches healthcare deployers as well as social-media platforms. Gemma 4 arrives without fanfare. Google DeepMind released Gemma 4 yesterday — four open-weight models tuned for reasoning and agentic workflows, the 31B variant ranking third on the open-model Arena leaderboard. Open weights matter for any trust considering on-premise deployment to satisfy ICO concerns; this is the first family that gets close to closed-model quality at a footprint a hospital trust could plausibly host. NHS England goes dark on GitHub. Per a directive effective 11 May, all NHSE source-code repositories are now private by default, with an internal note citing "the impact of rapid developments in AI models". Translation: NHSE has noticed public code is being scraped to train commercial systems, and is closing the stable door.
