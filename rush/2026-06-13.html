THE MORNING RUSH

A short brief on AI in medicine — and the world it's moving through. Read it before the 8am phone lines open.

Saturday 13 June 2026 · ~5 min read · Issue 046

On the line today: After a fortnight of breathless time-saving figures, an Edinburgh evaluation lands the counterpunch — some clinicians are quietly disappointed by their ambient scribes, and the researchers say the hype itself is the hazard. Meanwhile OneAdvanced unveils what it calls Britain's first sovereign healthcare LLM, trained on real NHS triage requests and claiming to out-navigate both frontier models and a GP control group. From the States, a growing pile of evidence that AI is quietly inflating medical bills by coding sicker. Anthropic puts $350m towards studying the job losses its own products may cause, and Brussels publishes the rulebook for labelling AI-generated content. Plus From the Eye — the FDA rule that lets a device rewrite itself, the £900m framework nobody read, and the American map where AI therapy is both banned and booming.

1. The scribe backlash arrives, dressed as a research paper

Just as the sector was toasting Philips' claim of 132 saved hours a year, Professor Kathrin Cresswell of Edinburgh has published a real-time qualitative evaluation of a regional ambient-scribe rollout across NHS primary and secondary care — and her headline is that some users are plainly disappointed, having expected "far too much." The market, she notes, is immature and volatile, and the "heightened expectations" loaded onto these tools can be "very damaging." Her sharper point is methodological: with complex health IT, productivity usually dips before it climbs, and judging scribes on short-term time-savings is a category error. She calls for realistic definitions of success and faster, more "agile" ways to evaluate them.

The view from the surgery: This is the most useful thing written about scribes all month, precisely because it isn't trying to sell me one. We've all watched a "time-saving" system quietly relocate the work rather than remove it. Cresswell's warning — that overpromising is itself a clinical risk — should be pinned above every procurement meeting.

2. Britain builds its own NHS-trained triage model

OneAdvanced, with Nvidia, has launched what it bills as the UK's first private sovereign healthcare LLM — a "Care Navigator" model trained on pseudonymised real-world triage requests submitted through its Patchs platform, which handles around 500,000 patient interactions a month. Its job isn't note-taking but triage: spotting the clinical topic in a patient's online request, asking the right follow-ups, and routing them faster. The firm claims it beat both leading frontier models and a GP control group on navigation accuracy, and — neatly — that GPs' own corrections feed back as continuous training data.

Why it matters: "Sovereign" is the word doing the heavy lifting, and for once it earns its keep: data trained and held under UK control, on British primary-care patterns, is a real answer to the governance worry that's dogged every American scribe. Two caveats. It's still built on Nvidia's Nemotron stack, so "sovereign" has an American floor. And a model that learns continuously from GP corrections is only as good as the GPs correcting it — garbage in, sovereign garbage out.

3. The quiet way AI inflates the bill: it codes you sicker

A run of US analyses is converging on an uncomfortable finding: AI documentation tools are nudging up the cost of care by capturing more granular, higher-severity diagnoses. A Blue Cross Blue Shield study found the billing code for acute post-haemorrhagic anaemia in new mothers leapt from 4% to 12.3% of maternity admissions between 2022 and 2025 — while actual transfusions barely moved. PwC now lists AI among five drivers pushing US health costs up as much as 9% in 2027. North Carolina has a bill in committee to curb exactly this.

The view: We don't bill per code here, so the British instinct is to shrug. Don't. The same scribes documenting "more thoroughly" are arriving in the NHS, and thoroughness has a direction of travel — towards whatever the system rewards. In a payment-by-results world that's money; in ours it could just as easily be inflated coding for QOF, risk stratification, or referral thresholds. Watch what the machine is optimising for, because it isn't always your patient.

4. Anthropic spends $350m studying the mess it's making

Anthropic has launched an "Economic Futures Program" — a $200m research fund plus a $150m fellowship paying early-career workers $85,000 to sit in nonprofits — to study AI's effect on jobs. Chief executive Dario Amodei went further than his rivals would, arguing that displacement may be intrinsic to a technology that replicates human cognition, and floating taxes on AI firms to fund a universal basic income. The labour data remains ambiguous: no clear rise in aggregate unemployment yet, but the entry-level door for young workers in exposed roles is visibly closing.

Why it matters: There's something bracing about the maker of the disruption funding the autopsy in advance. Cynically, it buys goodwill and shapes the research that will judge them. Less cynically, no other lab is saying the quiet part this loudly. For a profession forever short of juniors, the "closing entry-level door" finding is the one to watch — it's how workforces hollow out from the bottom without anyone noticing the top.

5. Brussels writes the rulebook for spotting a fake

On 10 June the European Commission published the final, voluntary Code of Practice on marking and labelling AI-generated content — the practical companion to the AI Act's transparency duties that bite on 2 August. Deepfakes and AI-generated text on matters of public interest must be clearly labelled; users must be told when they're talking to a chatbot rather than a human. Drafted by six independent experts with input from 180-odd stakeholders, it's the EU's attempt to make synthetic media announce itself.

The view: Voluntary codes have the structural integrity of wet cardboard, but the direction is right and it will leak into clinical software regardless. The day a patient brings me a confidently-worded, entirely fabricated "AI medical summary," I'd quite like it to carry a label. The harder question the code dodges: who checks, and what happens when the label is simply left off?

From the Eye

Three the front pages didn't run. Item one — buried in the FDA's 2026 quality-system overhaul is the "predetermined change control plan," a mechanism letting manufacturers update an authorised AI device's model weights without a fresh submission. Translated: the diagnostic tool you signed off in January can quietly become a different tool by June, fully approved, with no one in the surgery told it changed. Item two — while everyone watched the headline scribe deals, NHS Shared Business Services quietly stood up a £900m healthcare-AI procurement framework. That's the real plumbing — the pipe through which the next decade of tools will flow — and almost nobody has read the spec. Item three — the American map for AI therapy is now openly contradictory: Illinois and Nevada have banned AI from providing behavioural health outright, even as 36 states filed over 70 chatbot bills in a single quarter and millions of users carry on confiding in the things. Same technology, same week — outlawed in one state, unregulated next door.
