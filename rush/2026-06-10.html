THE MORNING RUSH

A short brief on AI in medicine — and the world it's moving through. Read it before the 8am phone lines open.

Wednesday 10 June 2026 · ~5 min read · Issue 043

On the line today: Washington has just funded the first FDA-authorised "agentic" AI meant to run cardiovascular care around the clock — the moment the technology stops drafting letters and starts adjusting drugs. Congress, meanwhile, has unveiled a bipartisan AI bill that would knock out state safeguards for three years. Closer to home, the RCGP and MHRA are settling the question of what a GP's pet AI tool legally is — and the answer is "a medical device". On the markets, $1.3 trillion evaporated from the chip sector on a single disappointing forecast, a useful splash of cold water for anyone selling efficiency miracles. And the patient-chatbot gap widens: the public increasingly trusts a chatbot that its makers swear is "not for diagnosis". Plus From the Eye — a US state law you'll never read, a quiet pipe into your patients' records, and Britain outsourcing its safety checks.

1. America funds the first AI agent allowed to act on heart patients

ARPA-H — the US health research agency modelled on DARPA — has confirmed funding for ADVOCATE, a programme to build the first FDA-authorised agentic AI for round-the-clock cardiovascular care. This is not a chatbot. The agent is designed to schedule appointments, adjust medications, prompt on diet and exercise, read the electronic record and pull live data from wearables. Crucially it ships with a second, supervisory AI whose only job is to police the first one's safety. Teams are being selected this month, with the whole thing — FDA approval included — squeezed into a 39-month timeline.

The view from the surgery: This is the line we've all been circling. An assistant that drafts a letter I check is one thing; an agent that titrates a patient's ramipril while I sleep is quite another, and that's precisely what's being built. The "supervisory agent watching the agent" design is clever and honest about the risk — but it's also AI marking AI's homework. Three years is breathtakingly fast for something this consequential. Watch who sits on the human side of that loop, and whether they're a clinician or a dashboard.

2. Congress's "Great American AI Act" would switch off the states for three years

Representatives Jay Obernolte and Lori Trahan have released a discussion draft of the Great American AI Act, a rare bipartisan attempt at a federal AI framework. It would require frontier developers to disclose model details and submit to third-party audits — but its most contested clause would pre-empt state AI laws for three years, a moratorium dressed as harmonisation. Consumer group Public Citizen called it a strip-out of states' power to protect "consumers, workers, and children".

Why it matters: Federal rules sound tidier than fifty squabbling states, but the timing is the tell. A three-year freeze on state law lands exactly as those states — California and Colorado especially — were starting to regulate AI in healthcare. Pause the only people writing enforceable rules, and the "framework" becomes a holiday. Worth watching from here, because the global standard the US sets tends to wash up on our shores within the year.

3. Your surgery's favourite AI tool is, legally, a medical device

The UK's National Commission into the Regulation of AI in Healthcare, feeding into the MHRA, is converging on a position the profession should note: the RCGP agrees that AI tools used in general practice should be treated by default as no less than Class II medical devices. That pulls the ambient scribe, the triage helper and the auto-drafted referral squarely inside formal device regulation — with the obligations that brings. The MHRA's AI-specific framework is due this year, informed by its "AI Airlock" sandbox pilot.

The view: This is the grown-up conversation we needed, and it's good that the RCGP is in the room rather than reacting after the fact. But "Class II by default" collides head-on with reality: a quarter of GPs are already using these tools, most bought off the shelf, few of them assessed as devices at all. Either a lot of current practice is about to be reclassified as non-compliant, or the rule arrives pre-watered-down. Keep your own list of what you're using and on what basis — you may need it.

4. A $1.3 trillion reminder that the AI trade can go backwards

In a single session last week the global semiconductor sector shed roughly $1.3 trillion in value — Nvidia down a chunk, Broadcom off 14%, the Nasdaq's worst day since April 2025. The trigger was almost comically mild: Broadcom merely held its AI forecast steady rather than raising it, and a market addicted to ever-bigger numbers read "steady" as "stalling".

Why it matters: I keep this one next to every "AI will save the NHS £x billion" headline. The entire edifice — the scribes, the agents, the copilots heading for half a million NHS desktops — rides on companies valued for perpetual hypergrowth. When a flat forecast can vaporise more than the NHS's entire annual budget in an afternoon, "efficiency savings" projected a decade out deserve a sceptical eyebrow. The clinical case for these tools should stand on its own, not on a share price.

5. Patients trust the chatbot. Its makers say don't.

The gap between how doctors and patients experience medical AI is widening into a chasm. Reporting this year keeps surfacing the same figure — particularly women with complex, dismissed chronic illness — turning to chatbots and arriving with a credible answer the system missed: long COVID dysautonomia, mast cell activation syndrome, a flagged bleeding risk. Meanwhile OpenAI's new ChatGPT Health, which links patient portals and lab results into the chatbot, ships with the careful disclaimer that it is "not intended for diagnosis and treatment."

The view from the surgery: Both things are true at once, which is the hard part. Sometimes the chatbot is the patient who got listened to for the first time — and we should sit with the uncomfortable bit of that. But "we built a tool that ingests your lab results and answers health questions, and also it's definitely not for diagnosis" is having it both ways. The patient hears the answer, not the small print. Better to ask what they've already concluded than to pretend they haven't asked.

From the Eye

Three the front pages didn't run. Item one — Colorado's AI Act takes effect on 30 June, quietly imposing risk-management duties on AI that drives healthcare decisions, while California tightens rules against AI that misleads patients into thinking they're talking to a licensed clinician. State law is where the actual enforceable healthcare-AI rules live in the US right now — which is exactly why the federal bill in item two wants them frozen. Item two — the plumbing nobody's watching: ChatGPT Health is piped into patient portals via a connectivity firm called b.well, turning a chatbot into a hub for your medical records. The data governance question — who holds it, who can mine it — is being settled by partnership announcements, not by debate. Item three — from H1 this year the MHRA's "reliance route" lets a device approved by the US FDA, Health Canada or Australia's TGA be waved through for Great Britain on a streamlined basis. Pragmatic, yes — but it means an AI tool's fitness for your patients may have been judged in Maryland, not Westminster. Convenient until the day it isn't.
