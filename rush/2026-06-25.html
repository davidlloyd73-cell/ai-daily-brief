THE MORNING RUSH

A short brief on AI in medicine — and the world it's moving through. Read it before the 8am phone lines open.

Thursday 25 June 2026 · ~5 min read · Issue 057

On the line today: Half a million NHS staff are about to get Copilot, with a 43-minutes-a-day saving claim that deserves a raised eyebrow. Regulators on both sides of the Atlantic have, for the first time, blessed an AI tool to adjudicate a clinical trial endpoint. An indemnity provider reports patients now firing off AI-drafted 25-page legal letters at practices. On the wider stage, AI becomes the leading stated reason for job cuts — with a healthy dose of "AI-washing" scepticism — and Europe picks a consortium to build its own sovereign frontier model. From the Eye: a quiet £900m NHS AI framework, a genetics-meets-records data tie-up, and an audit that finds half of chatbot health answers wanting.

1. Half a million NHS staff are getting Copilot

NHS England has confirmed that 505,000 clinicians and support staff will be given Microsoft 365 Copilot, with rollout to complete by October. The headline claim, trumpeted at London Tech Week, is that AI-assisted admin could save the average staff member 43 minutes a day — pitched as five weeks of reclaimed time a year. It is the largest single AI deployment the NHS has attempted, and it rides in on Microsoft's much-publicised £30bn UK investment.

The view from the surgery: Forty-three minutes a day is the sort of number that survives a press release and rarely survives contact with a Tuesday morning. Copilot drafting a letter is genuinely useful; Copilot freeing five weeks a year is a modelled best case. The real test is whether the saved minutes go back to patients or simply get absorbed by the next layer of administrative demand — which, in my experience, is where reclaimed time usually goes to die.

2. Regulators sign off an AI to judge a trial endpoint

In a quietly significant first, the EMA and FDA have both endorsed AIM-NASH — an AI tool that reads liver biopsy slides to adjudicate the endpoint in steatohepatitis (NASH) drug trials. Writing in NEJM AI this week, Kadakia and Krumholz argue this marks a genuine threshold: not AI as a clinical aide, but AI trusted to generate the evidence on which approvals turn. Endpoint adjudication has always been a slow, expensive, inter-observer-variable business; handing it to a model is both an efficiency prize and a new dependency.

Why it matters: This is regulators letting AI mark its own homework's homework. If the model drifts or carries a systematic bias, that bias is now baked into the trial result rather than the bedside. The upside in speed and consistency is real, but it shifts a chunk of trust upstream, into a place clinicians never see and cannot easily challenge.

3. The patient's complaint now arrives as a 25-page AI brief

An indemnity provider reports a sharp rise in patients using AI to generate lengthy, legalistic letters — some running to 25 pages — threatening action against GP practices. The RCGP's Victoria Tzortziou Brown notes that while AI may help patients articulate genuine concerns, it also lowers the barrier to complaints made in the heat of the moment, stripped of context. It lands against a backdrop where 89% of GPs not using AI cite medico-legal risk as the chief reason, and there is still no national guidance for AI in general practice.

The view: We spent a decade worrying about doctors leaning on chatbots; the chatbot has quietly changed sides. A reception team that could absorb a one-page grievance now faces a synthetic dossier with citations. The defence is unglamorous and familiar — contemporaneous notes, calm documentation — but the asymmetry of effort has shifted firmly against the practice.

4. AI is now the headline reason for the redundancy

The labour data has turned a corner. Employers cited AI as the primary reason for nearly 40% of announced job cuts in May, up from 7% in January, and Azeem Azhar's Exponential View has been marshalling the numbers behind it. Yet the scepticism is growing in step: Glassdoor's chief economist warns of "AI-washing" — firms reaching for a fashionable, blameless explanation for cuts they had planned anyway. Entry-level hiring, meanwhile, shows a measurable structural decline that is harder to wave away.

Why it matters: Both things are true at once, and that is the interesting part. AI is genuinely eating some entry-level work, and "the algorithm did it" is a convenient cover for ordinary cost-cutting. For anyone training the next generation — junior doctors included — the entry-level squeeze is the signal worth watching, because the bottom rung is how a profession reproduces itself.

5. Europe decides to build its own frontier model

The European Commission has named the EUROPA Consortium as winner of its Frontier AI Grand Challenge, tasked with building a sovereign, open-source model of over 400 billion parameters, trained natively across all 24 official EU languages. It sits alongside the Commission's new Cloud and AI Development Act, published this month, which imposes strict "sovereignty" tiers on public-sector data. The direction is unmistakable: Europe no longer wants to rent its intelligence from California or Shenzhen.

The view: Digital sovereignty is the phrase of the year, and for health systems it is not abstract. A capable open model that can run inside a European firewall is exactly what NHS information governance has been quietly praying for — patient data that never leaves the building. Whether a committee-built consortium can actually ship something frontier-grade is the open question, but the instinct to stop outsourcing the brains is sound.

From the Eye

Item one: while everyone watched the Copilot announcement, NHS Shared Business Services quietly stood up a £900m framework for healthcare AI solutions — the procurement plumbing through which much of this will actually flow, and the document nobody outside the trade press will read until their trust is locked into it. Item two: 23andMe's research arm has tied up with HealthEx to let users bolt their medical records onto their genetic data — a "comprehensive personalised health profile" that is also, viewed from a colder angle, the most commercially valuable dataset a person can assemble about themselves, handed over for the convenience of a dashboard. Item three: and the one to keep by the screen — a BMJ Open audit of five leading chatbots found 49.6% of 250 health responses problematic, with nearly one in five rated potentially harmful. Half wrong is not a rounding error when the patient has already arrived with the printout.
