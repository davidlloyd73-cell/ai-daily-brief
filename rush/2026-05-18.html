THE MORNING RUSH

A short brief on AI in medicine — and the world it's moving through. Read it before the 8am phone lines open.

Monday 18 May 2026 · ~5 min read · Issue 020

On the line today: BMJ Open tests the five chatbots the public actually uses and finds half their answers to clear medical questions are "somewhat" or "highly" problematic, with fabricated references to match. npj Digital Medicine publishes the first systematic look at where ambient AI scribes stop working as you move out of the GP room. Eli Lilly signs Profluent to design site-specific recombinases — the plumbing of next-generation gene therapy — entirely in silico. Anthropic opens talks on a $30–50bn raise at up to $950bn, briefly the most valuable AI company on the planet. And Microsoft's Frontier Firms report names the four shapes of human-agent work and quietly turns Copilot Cowork into the operating system for it. Plus From the Eye — Phil Hammond on what A&G has done to the GP referral, the enterprise-chatbot churn stat that won't go away, and new evidence that patients tell AI less than they tell us.

1. BMJ Open — half the chatbot medical answers are "problematic", and the citations don't exist

A team led by Asad Naveed at the University of Western Australia put the five chatbots most often used by UK patients — ChatGPT, Gemini, DeepSeek, Meta AI and Grok — through a fixed set of evidence-based clinical questions covering antibiotic prescribing, hypertension and screening intervals. The paper, in BMJ Open, finds half of all answers graded "somewhat" or "highly" problematic against current guidelines. Every chatbot fabricated references at some rate; none provided a fully accurate reference list. The worst hallucinated a citation in nearly half its responses.

The view from the surgery: Pair this with last week's KCL number — one in seven UK adults consulting a chatbot instead of a GP — and the policy hole is obvious. The MHRA's SaMD perimeter is built around tools sold to clinicians. The actual exposure is consumer chatbots talking to patients in their kitchens, and they sit entirely outside it. Worth keeping the BMJ Open link to hand for the next patient who arrives with a printed ChatGPT response.

2. npj Digital Medicine — why the ambient-scribe story breaks outside the GP room

A perspective from Ohde, Thompson, Liu and colleagues at the Mayo Clinic, in npj Digital Medicine, audits ambient AI scribes across the settings the early adopters carefully avoided — inpatient wards, ICU, A&E, prehospital, and low-resource care. The pattern is consistent: time-saved flattens or inverts the moment the conversation involves more than two participants, switches language mid-encounter, or carries resus background noise. Accuracy in multilingual cohorts is materially worse, and the authors flag a real consent problem in obtunded and paediatric patients.

Why it matters: April's NHS England guidance was carefully scoped to ambulatory and primary care; this is the evidence base behind that scope. If your ICB is being sold an enterprise AVT licence on the back of GP figures, ask the supplier for their inpatient and A&E accuracy data — and watch the silence.

3. Lilly signs Profluent on AI-designed recombinases — the unglamorous bit of gene therapy

Eli Lilly announced a multi-programme collaboration with Bezos-backed Profluent Bio covering AI-designed site-specific recombinases — the enzymes that paste DNA precisely into a chosen genomic site, the workhorses behind the next generation of in-vivo gene therapies after CRISPR. Profluent's pitch is that its protein-language models can generate recombinases for targets no naturally occurring enzyme will hit. Lilly already has LillyPod online, the $2.75bn Insilico small-molecule deal running, and Boltz on structure prediction. This is Lilly building the full stack rather than buying a slot.

The view: The Sanger and the Crick are heavily invested in the same protein-design tooling. A Lilly–Profluent partnership pulls publication and recruitment gravity to California. The strategic question Streeting was meant to answer — how the UK life-sciences plan stays competitive on AI-native pharma — has just become James Murray's first big test.

4. Anthropic in talks for a $30–50bn raise at up to $950bn — bigger than OpenAI

Bloomberg confirmed on 12 May that Anthropic is in advanced discussions for a fundraise of $30–50bn at $900–950bn. At the top of the range this overtakes OpenAI's $825bn for the first time. Google committed up to $40bn in April; Amazon has stretched its stake to $25bn. The driver, per several sources, is that Claude Opus 4.7 and the Mythos cohort are generating enterprise revenue on a curve OpenAI has not matched.

Why it matters: The market is now pricing Anthropic and OpenAI as an AI duopoly. The NHS-adjacent read is procurement risk: if either lab decides Health is a separate product line — as Anthropic hinted with the Gates Foundation deal — the bargaining power shifts overnight. Hold the line on multi-year flat-rate contracts.

5. Microsoft's "Frontier Firms" report — and Copilot Cowork goes mobile

Microsoft's 2026 Work Trend Index, published 5 May, is built around a taxonomy of four human-AI working patterns: Author (human-led, AI assists), Editor (AI drafts, human approves), Director (human spec, AI delivers), Orchestrator (parallel agents, human handles exceptions). Alongside it Microsoft expanded Copilot Cowork with iOS/Android apps and a third-party plugin store — the not-very-subtle pitch being that the plugin store becomes for enterprise AI what the App Store became for the iPhone.

The view from the surgery: Skip the consultancy-deck framing. The substantive shift is that "human-in-the-loop" is being unbundled into four loops, each with a different liability profile. For a salaried GP signing an AVT consent form this autumn, the relevant question is no longer "is there a human in the loop" but "which of the four loops am I in" — and which NHS Resolution's revised indemnity wording actually covers.

From the Eye

Three the front pages didn't run. Phil Hammond's MD column in this week's Private Eye, on sale Wednesday, takes the scalpel to NHS Advice & Guidance — the e-RS gatekeeping pathway introduced in 2015 — arguing it has quietly become the largest unmeasured rationing instrument in the NHS, with consultant rejection rates now used as a perverse productivity metric inside acute trusts, and GPs carrying the medico-legal risk for specialist decisions they did not make. Item two — The AI Daily Brief's 11 May enterprise round-up flagged a figure quietly circulating in the consultancy decks: 74% of enterprise AI chatbots are shut down within six months of launch, the highest churn rate of any enterprise software category in a decade. The reason, repeatedly, is not accuracy — it is that the chatbots fail to integrate with the system of record. Worth the smile next time someone mentions "AI transformation" without naming the underlying database. And item three — a Med Xpress-reported study from the Max Planck Institute on 14 May finds that patients describing symptoms to AI chatbots produce markedly shorter, less specific histories than the same patients describing symptoms to a human doctor — average word count down 38%, omission of the key red-flag symptom up 22%. The consumer-chatbot risk is not just bad answers, it is bad input.
