## The One Idea

### The Fable 5 fight won't be resolved by engineers — it's interpersonal.

A jailbreak report from Amazon escalated into a Commerce Department export ban that forced Anthropic to pull Fable and Mythos entirely. But the more NLW digs into the dueling accounts, the clearer it gets: the technical merits barely matter. The administration felt Anthropic wasn't taking it seriously; Anthropic thought it could simply reason the White House into agreement. It can't. Anthropic is no longer a scrappy startup — it's one of two leaders in the most consequential industry of the era, and it has to play ball with the government it has, not the one it wishes it had.

## The Other Side

Today's brief leans on the premise that these labs are precious, load-bearing assets — Adam Thierer's line that roughly half of last year's stock-market gains rode on AI, and NLW's recurring claim that the economy now rests on them. In a same-day exclusive, Zitron reports the opposite picture from the accounts: OpenAI's losses widened nearly eightfold in 2025 as spending reached thirty-four billion dollars. On his reading, the thing Washington just fought over, and that markets price as inevitable, is an unprofitable money-incinerator sustained only by ever-larger capital raises. The power NLW treats as nation-state-scale, Zitron treats as financial fragility dressed up as destiny.

Source: Ed Zitron, "OpenAI Losses Increased Nearly 8X in 2025, With Spending Hitting $34 Billion", 15 June 2026 — https://www.wheresyoured.at/ais-brokenomics/

## The Truth Beneath

Strip the rhetoric and both men orbit one variable: whether these labs' dominance is durable economic power or capital-dependent fragility. They accept the same facts — the labs are now central enough that the White House intervenes and markets ride on them, AI drove a large share of recent gains, and OpenAI's spending is colossal. The empirical question is whether that spending converts to a profit path or the losses keep compounding; the observable that settles it is audited financials, with the IPO filings forcing disclosure. For NLW to be vindicated, revenue must scale and the buildout self-fund, so 'the economy rests on this' becomes strength rather than exposure. For Zitron, the losses must keep widening until the raises stop. Both are polemicists — NLW sells optimism and the anti-doom case, Zitron sells bubble-certainty and anti-hype — so the signal is where they agree despite opposite priors: both now call the sector's financial footing precarious. Watch OpenAI's next raise and its audited numbers.

## GP's Corner

_Claude's notes on what today means for UK general practice and the NHS - commentary, not from the podcast itself._

### When 'safety' blocks the word cancer

The most useful detail for a clinician was buried in the row: Fable's guardrails were reportedly so broad they blocked questions about mitochondria and any prompt containing the word 'cancer.' That is the practical face of heavy safety tuning — a model that refuses legitimate medical queries is worse than useless at the point of care. Before trusting any AI tool clinically, test it on real consultation language — cancer, overdose, self-harm, controlled-drug doses — and prefer tools tuned for healthcare over consumer models with blunt filters.

### This week's lesson: the risk is political, not just technical

A frontier model was pulled in ninety minutes over politics and personalities, not a demonstrated safety failure. For a practice that means the threat to a tool you rely on may have nothing to do with how well it works. If NLW is right that the labs are load-bearing, that concentration is itself systemic risk; if Zitron is right that OpenAI's losses jumped roughly eightfold last year, the more likely failure is financial. The precaution is the same either way: a manual fallback and the ability to move providers.

### Don't outsource judgement to conviction

A recurring theme was the danger of people convinced they alone understand the remedy. It transfers cleanly to clinical AI: a tool's confident tone is not evidence it is right. Keep the clinician, not the model, accountable for the decision — and treat any 'it's safe, trust us' assurance, from vendor or regulator, as a claim to be tested rather than accepted.

## NLW's Takes

### Sacks's post reads like a press release setting up Dario as the sacrificial lamb

NLW notes Sacks paints Anthropic as hypocritical — the safety company not taking a safety issue seriously — and pointedly shifts from 'Anthropic' to naming Dario Amodei personally. No resignation calls yet, but singling out Dario at least hints at a second way out of the standoff.

### When people decide they alone know the remedy, things go bad fast

NLW says he's seen this dynamic up close: the moment people convince themselves they're the only ones sufficiently concerned and the only ones with the right fix, danger follows — and the stakes are radically amplified by the power inherent in superintelligence.

### If Dario's not on the plane, nothing will change

NLW's read: the resolution won't be primarily technical, it'll be interpersonal. Anthropic thought it could reason the White House into its view; it can't. Investor Melinda Chu put the bottom line bluntly — sending senior staff won't matter unless Amodei himself shows up.
